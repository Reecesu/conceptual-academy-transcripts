He described the composition and behavior of matter in terms of four qualities: hot, cold, moist, and dry.

That's right. Aristotle's model was a remarkable achievement for its day, and people found it made practical sense. For example, when pottery is made, wet clay changes to ceramic because the heat of the fire drives out the moist quality of the wet clay and replaces it with the dry quality of the ceramic.


Aristotle combined these four qualities to explain all materials. Hot and dry gave fire, moist and cold gave water, hot and moist gave air, and dry and cold gave earth. He supposed that a hard substance like rock contained mostly the dry quality, and a soft substance like clay contained more of the moist quality.

Alia

Yes! The atomic model was advanced by several Greek philosophers, including Democritus, who lived from 460–370 BCE. He actually coined the term "atom" from the Greek phrase "a tomos," which means "not cut" or "that which is indivisible."

Student

So Democritus had it right all along? Why didn't people just believe him instead of Aristotle?

Alia

Aristotle's reputation and influence were so powerful that the atomic model wouldn't reappear for about 2,000 years! Democritus imagined that atoms of different elements had different shapes - like atoms of iron being shaped like coils, making iron rigid, strong, and malleable, while atoms of fire were sharp, lightweight, and yellow.

Student

That's wild! So we lost 2,000 years of progress because everyone trusted Aristotle more? Did anything useful come from Aristotle's theory?

Alia

An interesting question! According to Aristotle, it was theoretically possible to transform any substance to another substance simply by altering the relative proportions of the four basic qualities. This meant that, under the proper conditions, a metal like lead could be transformed to gold.


This concept laid the foundation of alchemy, a field of study concerned primarily with finding potions that would produce gold or confer immortality. Alchemists from the time of Aristotle to as late as the 1600s tried in vain to convert various metals to gold.

Student

Oh, like in Harry Potter! I didn't realize alchemy was based on actual historical beliefs. Did they ever succeed in making gold?

Alia

No, they never succeeded in making gold or finding immortality potions. However, despite the futility of their efforts, the alchemists learned much about the behavior of many chemicals and developed many useful laboratory techniques that helped lay groundwork for modern chemistry. So while their goals weren't achieved, their work wasn't entirely in vain!

Student

So when did science finally get back on track with the atom idea?

Alia

With the advent of modern science, Aristotle's views on the nature of matter came into question. A major breakthrough came in the late 1700s when the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier discovered the law of mass conservation, which we discussed earlier as matter not being created or destroyed in ordinary chemical reactions.


This verifiable law ran counter to Aristotle's idea that matter could lose or gain mass as its hot, dry, cold, or moist qualities changed. Lavoisier further hypothesized that an element is any material made of a fundamental substance that cannot be broken down into anything else.

Student

Wait, so that's what an element is? Something that can't be broken down further?

Alia

Exactly! That's how Lavoisier defined an element. Through experiments, he was able to transform water into two different substances—hydrogen and oxygen. According to Lavoisier, Aristotle was wrong to think of water as an element since it could be broken down into simpler substances.

Student

That makes sense. Water isn't on our periodic table of elements, but hydrogen and oxygen are. That's so cool that he figured that out in the 1700s!

Alia

Yes! It was groundbreaking work. Interestingly, Lavoisier had a tragic ending. To help finance his scientific projects, he took part-time employment as a tax collector, where he actually introduced reforms to help ease the tax burden on peasants. But because of this employment, he was beheaded in 1794 during the French Revolution. After hearing appeals to spare his life, the judge determined that "the Republic needs neither scientists nor chemists; the course of justice cannot be delayed." About 18 months after Lavoisier's execution, the French government sent a formal apology to his widow, Marie-Anne.

Alia

It was indeed a tragic loss for science. But his work inspired others. Further experimental work by Lavoisier and others led the English chemist John Dalton, who lived from 1766 to 1844, to reintroduce the atomic ideas of Democritus.

Student

So Dalton brought back the atom idea after 2,000 years? What made people start believing it then?

Alia

Dalton wrote a series of postulates—claims he assumed to be true based on experimental evidence. Some of these include:
1. Each element consists of indivisible, minute particles called atoms.
2. Atoms can be neither created nor destroyed in chemical reactions.
3. All atoms of a given element are identical.
4. Atoms of different elements have different masses.

Student

Those sound familiar from my reading. I think we now know that atoms can actually be split though, right? Like in nuclear reactions?

Alia

You're absolutely right! That's an excellent observation. Some of Dalton's postulates have been refined with our modern understanding. We now know atoms can be split in nuclear reactions, and we've discovered isotopes, which are atoms of the same element with different masses. But for ordinary chemical reactions, his model worked remarkably well.


John Dalton came from a very poor family, and although his formal schooling ended at age 11, he continued to learn on his own and even began teaching others when he was only 12. His primary research interest was weather, which led him to conduct many experiments with gases. Soon after publishing his conclusions on the atomic nature of matter, his reputation as a first-rate scientist increased rapidly.

Student

That's impressive! He taught himself and became a famous scientist without much formal education. So how did Dalton's ideas help chemistry progress?

Alia

It didn't matter that these tiny atoms were too small to be seen. What did matter was that Dalton's atomic model worked to explain much of what was then known about chemical reactions. Where alchemists using Aristotle's model failed, chemists using Dalton's model succeeded—not in making gold but in being able to understand and control the outcome of numerous chemical reactions.


Imagine their amazement at finally being able to control the creation of new materials...as though finally being given the wizard's cookbook, where the wizard in this case was nature herself.

Student

That's a cool way to put it! So after Dalton, did scientists just keep learning more about atoms?

Alia

Yes, knowledge continued to build. Another major breakthrough came in 1869, when a Russian chemistry professor, Dmitri Mendeleev, produced a chart summarizing the properties of known elements for his students.


Mendeleev was a devoted and highly effective teacher. Students adored him and would fill lecture halls to hear him speak about chemistry. Much of his work on the periodic table occurred in his spare time following his lectures. He taught not only in university classrooms but anywhere he traveled. During his journeys by train, he would travel third class with peasants to share his findings about agriculture.

Student

Oh, so Mendeleev created the periodic table! I've seen that on the wall in our classroom. How did he organize all those elements?

Alia

Mendeleev's chart was unique in that it resembled a calendar. Elements were listed in horizontal rows in order of increasing mass. The first row contained the lightest elements, the second row contained the next heaviest elements, and so forth.


Aligning rows of elements above and below each other (like days of a calendar) revealed that elements within the same vertical column had similar properties, such as chemical reactivity. In order to achieve this pattern, however, he had to shift some elements left or right occasionally. This left gaps—blank spaces that could not be filled by any known element.

Student

That sounds frustrating - his pattern didn't work perfectly. Did he just ignore those gaps?

Alia

Actually, he did something remarkable! Instead of looking on these gaps as defects, Mendeleev boldly predicted the existence of elements that had not yet been discovered. His predictions about the properties of some of those missing elements led to their discovery.


That Mendeleev was able to predict the properties of new elements helped convince many scientists of the accuracy of Dalton's atomic hypothesis, upon which Mendeleev's periodic table was based. This in turn helped promote Dalton's proposed atomic nature of matter from a hypothesis to a more widely accepted theory. Mendeleev's chart ultimately led to our modern periodic table, which we'll discuss more fully later.

Alia

Exactly! It was powerful evidence. Since the time of Lavoisier, Dalton, and Mendeleev, our understanding of atoms has grown substantially. Although we have not discovered the alchemist's dream of immortality, we have learned how to design medicines that cure numerous diseases. From crude oil we can make fuels, plastics, clothing, and more. From thin air we can produce fertilizer.


Virtually every aspect of modern society has been and will continue to be affected by our ability to manipulate atoms to meet our needs. Of all the discoveries made by humans, our discovery of the atom is arguably one of our greatest and most profound.

They can produce images of atoms, like gallium and arsenic atoms. Scientists have even created the world's tiniest map where each dot consists of a few thousand gold atoms, each dot moved into its proper place by a scanning probe microscope.

They are indeed! Some atoms are larger than others, but they are all exceedingly small. Gold atoms, for example, are so small that about 4,000,000,000,000 (4 trillion) of them could fit within the period at the end of a sentence.

Absolutely! Just as important as our high-tech evidence is the fact that atoms and molecules can be used to explain common observations. For example, heat transforms moist clay into ceramic by driving off water molecules. In ice, water molecules are stuck together in a fixed orientation. Warmth melts the ice by helping the water molecules break away from each other.


Consider placing a fragrance, such as cinnamon oil, within an inflated rubber balloon. The balloon is sealed. How then does the outer surface of the balloon smell like cinnamon?

Similarly, we can use the idea of molecules to explain how moisture can collect on a tabletop before disappearing. If you place your palms down on a cool, dark, and reflective table such as a slate lab benchtop, water molecules exiting from your skin collect onto this surface. When you lift your hands, you can see this moisture, which quickly disappears as the water molecules evaporate into the air.

If you add Kool-Aid crystals to water, they settle to the bottom of the container. Without stirring, the crystals begin to dissolve as they are bombarded by water molecules in the liquid phase. The bustling movement of the water molecules eventually causes the Kool-Aid to be uniformly mixed with the water.

Precisely! The explanatory powers of the atomic model are great. This, along with our hi-tech evidence, leads us to trust that matter is made of these super-small particles we call atoms.
