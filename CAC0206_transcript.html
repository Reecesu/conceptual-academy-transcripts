Student

Sure. I guess temperature is just how hot or cold something is, right?

Alia

You're on the right track! Temperature is indeed how hot or cold something is relative to some standard. But there's something fascinating happening at the atomic level that explains temperature. You see, atoms and molecules are in constant motion, jiggling to and fro or bouncing from one position to another. By virtue of their motion, these particles possess kinetic energy.

Student

Wait, so tiny particles are always moving inside objects? Even in something solid like my desk?

Alia

Exactly! That's a great observation. Even in solid objects like your desk, the atoms are constantly in motion - they're just more restricted in their movement compared to liquids or gases. The key insight is that temperature is directly related to the average kinetic energy of these submicroscopic particles. Whenever something becomes warmer, the kinetic energy of its particles increases.

Student

So when something feels hot, it's because the atoms inside it are moving faster?

Alia

That's precisely correct! Let me give you an example from the textbook: if you strike a penny with a hammer, the penny becomes warm because the hammer's blow causes its atoms to jostle faster, increasing their kinetic energy. The hammer becomes warm for the same reason.

Student

Oh, that's interesting. So when I heat up water for coffee, I'm actually making the water molecules move faster?

Alia

Yes! That's an excellent connection to everyday life. When you put a flame to a liquid, the liquid becomes warmer because the energy of the flame causes the particles of the liquid to move faster, increasing their kinetic energy. In hot coffee, the molecules are moving faster on average than those in cold coffee.

Student

But how do we actually measure temperature? I mean, just touching something doesn't seem very accurate.

Alia

That's a very insightful point! Touching an object isn't a reliable way of measuring its temperature. Let me describe an experiment: if you place one hand in cold water and one hand in hot water, then put both hands in lukewarm water, each hand will feel a different temperature even though they're in the same water.

Student

That makes sense. I've noticed that when I come in from the cold, room temperature feels warm, but when I come in from the heat, the same room feels cool.

Alia

Exactly! That's why we need objective measurements. To measure temperature, we take advantage of the fact that nearly all materials expand when their temperature is raised and contract when it is lowered. With increasing temperature, the particles move faster and are on average farther apart—the material expands. With decreasing temperature, the particles move more slowly and are on average closer together—the material contracts.

Student

Is that why thermometers work? I've seen those red lines go up and down.

Alia

Yes! A thermometer exploits this characteristic of matter, measuring temperature by means of the expansion and contraction of a liquid, usually mercury or colored alcohol. As the liquid gets warmer, its molecules move faster and spread out more, causing the liquid to rise in the narrow column.

Student

I've never thought about that before. But wait, I have a question - I've noticed that telephone wires sometimes sag on really hot days. Is that related to this expansion stuff?

Alia

That's an excellent observation! You've spotted a real-world example of thermal expansion. On a hot day, the atoms in the wire are moving faster, and as a result, the wire expands and becomes longer, causing it to sag. On a cold day, those same atoms are moving more slowly, which causes the wire to contract and become shorter, so it's pulled tighter.

Student

That's cool! I never knew the science behind that. So what about different temperature scales? I know we use Fahrenheit in the US, but I've heard of Celsius too.

Alia

You're right, there are different temperature scales. The Celsius thermometer is actually the most common thermometer in the world. It was named after Swedish astronomer Anders Celsius, who suggested a scale of 100 degrees between the freezing point and boiling point of fresh water. On the Celsius scale, 0°C is assigned to the temperature at which pure water freezes, and 100°C is assigned to the temperature at which it boils at standard atmospheric pressure.

Student

And Fahrenheit is what we use in the US, right? Why is it so different?

Alia

Yes, in the United States, we use the Fahrenheit scale, named after German scientist G.D. Fahrenheit. He originally chose to assign 0 to the temperature of a mixture containing equal weights of snow and common salt, and to assign 100 to human body temperature. But since these reference points aren't dependable, the scale was modified so that the freezing point of pure water is 32°F and the boiling point is 212°F. On this recalibrated scale, the average human body temperature taken orally is around 98.2°F.

Student

That seems so arbitrary compared to Celsius! Are there any other scales?

Alia

There is one more important scale I should mention - the Kelvin scale, which is favored by scientists. It's named after British physicist Lord Kelvin. This scale is calibrated not in terms of the freezing and boiling points of water but rather in terms of the motion of atoms and molecules.

Student

How does that work?

Alia

On the Kelvin scale, zero is the temperature at which there is no atomic or molecular motion. This is a theoretical limit called absolute zero, which is the temperature at which the particles of a substance have absolutely no kinetic energy to give up. Absolute zero corresponds to –459.7°F on the Fahrenheit scale and to –273.15°C on the Celsius scale. On the Kelvin scale, this temperature is simply 0 K, which is read "zero kelvin" or "zero K."

Student

So we can never reach absolute zero? That's the coldest anything can possibly be?

Alia

Theoretically, yes! And interestingly, marks on the Kelvin scale are the same distance apart as those on the Celsius scale, so the temperature of freezing water is 273 kelvin. And it's important to note that the word "degree" is not used with the Kelvin scale. To say "273 degrees kelvin" is incorrect. The correct way is simply "273 kelvin."

Student

This is a lot to remember, but it's making more sense now. So temperature is about the average energy of molecules... but what about the total energy? Is that the same thing?

Alia

That's an excellent question that gets at a key distinction! Temperature is a measure of the average amount of energy in a substance, not the total amount of energy. Let me give you an illustrative example: The total energy in a swimming pool full of boiling water is much more than the total energy in a cupful of boiling water even though both are at the same temperature.

Student

So they're both 100°C, but the pool has way more energy overall?

Alia

Exactly! Your utility bill after heating the swimming pool water to 100°C would definitely show this difference! Whereas the total amount of energy in the pool is much more than in the cup, the average molecular motion is the same in both water samples. The water molecules in the swimming pool are moving on average just as fast as the water molecules in the cup. The only difference is that the swimming pool contains more water molecules and hence a greater total amount of energy.

Student

I think I get it now. So what's the difference between temperature and heat then? I think I've been using those terms interchangeably.

Alia

That's a common confusion! Heat is energy that flows from a higher-temperature object to a lower-temperature object. If you touch a hot stove, heat enters your hand because the stove is at a higher temperature than your hand. When you touch a piece of ice, energy passes out of your hand and into the ice because the ice is at a lower temperature than your hand.

Student

So heat is about energy transfer, not just how hot something is?

Alia

Precisely! From a human perspective, if you are absorbing heat, you experience warmth; if you are losing heat, you experience cooling. Here's an interesting thought experiment: the next time you touch the hot forehead of a sick, feverish friend, ask them whether your hand feels hot or cold to them. Whereas temperature is absolute, hot and cold are relative.

Student

That's fascinating! So my hand would actually feel cool to someone with a fever because I'm drawing heat away from them?

Alia

Exactly! You've got it. In general, the greater the temperature difference between two bodies in contact with each other, the greater the rate of heat flow. This is why a hot stove can cause much more damage to your skin than a warm stove. And because heat is a form of energy, its unit is the joule.

Student

This reminds me of something - when I get into a swimming pool, the water feels really cold at first, but after a while, it doesn't feel so bad. Why is that?

Alia

That's an excellent observation! Heat flows because of a temperature difference. When you enter the water, your skin temperature is much higher than the water temperature. The result is a significant flow of heat from your body to the water, which you experience as cold.


Once you have been in the water awhile, your skin temperature is much closer to the water temperature (due to the cooling effects of the water and your body's ability to conserve heat), so the flow of heat from your body is less. With less heat flowing from your body, the water no longer feels so cold.
