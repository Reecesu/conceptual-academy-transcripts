Metals are also malleable, which means they can be hammered into different shapes or bent without breaking. They're also ductile, which means they can be drawn into wires.

Exactly! That's a perfect everyday example of malleability. And yes, all but a few metals are solid at ambient temperature. The exceptions include mercury, gallium, cesium, and francium, which are all liquids at 30°C (86°F).

You're right that most metals are solid at room temperature, and it's a common assumption that all metals are solid. Mercury is indeed the most well-known liquid metal. But gallium, cesium, and francium are also liquid at a warm room temperature of 30°C (86°F). There's another interesting exception: hydrogen. Under normal conditions, hydrogen behaves as a nonmetallic gas, but it can take on the properties of a liquid metal at very high pressures.

Hydrogen can be a metal? That seems bizarre! Where would you even find hydrogen under that much pressure?

It does seem counterintuitive! Hydrogen exists as a liquid metal deep beneath the surfaces of planets like Jupiter and Saturn, where pressures are exceedingly high. In those environments, the extreme pressure forces hydrogen to behave like a metal.

Alia

The nonmetallic elements, with the exception of hydrogen, are found on the right-hand side of the periodic table. Most nonmetals are poor conductors of electricity and heat. Solid nonmetals tend to be neither malleable nor ductile. Rather, they are brittle and shatter when hammered. At 30°C (86°F), some nonmetals are solid (like carbon), others are liquid (like bromine), and still others are gaseous (like helium).

Student

I see. And you also mentioned metalloids. What are those?

Alia

Six elements are classified as metalloids: boron, silicon, germanium, arsenic, antimony, and tellurium. Situated between the metals and the nonmetals in the periodic table, the metalloids have both metallic and nonmetallic characteristics. For example, these elements are weak conductors of electricity, which makes them useful as semiconductors in the integrated circuits of computers.

Student

Oh! So that's why we hear about silicon in computer chips? Because it's a semiconductor?

Alia

Exactly! Looking at the periodic table, we can see how germanium (number 32) is closer to more metals than nonmetals. Because of this positioning, we can deduce that germanium has more metallic properties than silicon (number 14) and is a slightly better conductor of electricity. So, integrated circuits fabricated with germanium operate faster than those fabricated with silicon. Because silicon is much more abundant and less expensive to obtain, however, silicon computer chips remain the industry standard.

Student

That makes sense. I never realized the arrangement of elements on the table could tell us so much about how they behave. What else can the periodic table tell us?

Alia

Two other important ways in which the elements are organized in the periodic table are by horizontal rows and vertical columns. Each horizontal row is called a period, and each vertical column is called a group (or a family). There are 7 periods and 18 groups in the periodic table.

Student

So does that mean elements in the same row or same column have something in common?

Alia

Yes, absolutely. Across any period, the properties of elements gradually change. This gradual change is called a periodic trend. For example, the size of atoms generally decreases from left to right across any period. This trend repeats from one horizontal row to the next. This phenomenon of repeating trends is called periodicity, a term used to indicate that the trends recur in cycles. Each horizontal row is called a period because it corresponds to one full cycle of a trend.

Student

So atoms actually get smaller as you move across the table from left to right? Why would that happen?

Alia

That's correct. As we'll explore further in later chapters, there are many properties of elements that change gradually in moving across a row of the periodic table. The change in atomic size is just one example of these periodic trends.

Student

I thought all atoms were basically the same size, just with different numbers of particles inside them. This is kind of confusing.

Alia

I understand your confusion. It's a common misconception that atoms all have roughly the same size. But in reality, atomic size varies quite a bit across the periodic table. This is related to the number of protons and electrons, as well as how these particles interact. The periodic nature of these trends is part of what makes the periodic table such a powerful tool.

That's a great observation! Most nonmetallic materials do not conduct electricity, but there are important exceptions like graphene, which is a one atom thick sheet of carbon atoms. The graphite in your pencil is made of stacks of graphene. There are also nanotubes, which are graphene sheets rolled into tubes. All these materials conduct electricity rather well, which leads to many useful applications. Sheets of graphene, for example, are used to make ultra-thin and flexible OLED display screens.

That's cool! I didn't know my pencil lead was related to high-tech screens. Going back to the periodic table structure, what about the vertical columns? You called them groups or families?

Yes, down any group (vertical column), the properties of elements tend to be remarkably similar, which is why these elements are said to be "grouped" or "in a family." Several groups have traditional names that describe the properties of their elements.

What kind of traditional names?

Alia

For example, early in human history, people discovered that ashes mixed with water produce a slippery solution useful for removing grease. By the Middle Ages, such mixtures were described as being alkaline, a term derived from the Arabic word for ashes, al-qali. Alkaline mixtures found many uses, particularly in the preparation of soaps. We now know that alkaline ashes contain compounds of group 1 elements, most notably potassium carbonate, also known as potash. Because of this history, group 1 elements, which are metals, are called the alkali metals.

Student

So they named the elements based on what they were used for historically? That's interesting. Are there other groups with special names?

Alia

Yes! Elements of group 2 also form alkaline solutions when mixed with water. Furthermore, medieval alchemists noted that certain minerals (which we now know are made up of group 2 elements) do not melt or change when put in fire. These fire-resistant substances were known to the alchemists as "earth." As a holdover from these ancient times, group 2 elements are known as the alkaline-earth metals.


Over toward the right side of the periodic table, elements of group 16 are known as the chalcogens ("ore-forming" in Greek), because the top two elements of this group, oxygen and sulfur, are so commonly found in ores. Elements of group 17 are known as the halogens ("salt-forming" in Greek), because of their tendency to form various salts. Group 18 elements are all unreactive gases that tend not to combine with other elements. For this reason, they are called the inert noble gases, presumably because the nobility of earlier times were above interacting with common folk.

The noble gases don't interact with others? Like snobbish aristocrats? That's a funny way to think about it! What about the elements in the middle of the table?

Alia

The elements of groups 3 through 12 are all metals that do not form alkaline solutions with water. These metals tend to be harder than the alkali metals and less reactive with water; hence, they are used for structural purposes. Collectively, they are known as the transition metals, a name that denotes their central position in the periodic table.


The transition metals include some of the most familiar and important elements—iron, copper, nickel, chromium, silver, and gold. They also include many lesser-known elements that are nonetheless important in modern technology. People with hip implants appreciate the transition metals titanium, molybdenum, and manganese, because these noncorrosive metals are used in implant devices.

That's not a coincidence at all! Copper (number 29), silver (number 47), and gold (number 79) are all in the same group in the periodic table (group 11), which suggests they should have similar—though not identical—properties.


These three metals are among the few that can be found naturally in their elemental state. They have found great use as coins and jewelry for a number of reasons, including their resistance to corrosion and their remarkable colors. Their similar properties are directly reflected by their placement in the same group of the periodic table.

You've noticed something important! Within the sixth period is a subset of 14 metallic elements (numbers 58 to 71) that are quite unlike any of the other transition metals. A similar subset (numbers 90 to 103) is found within the seventh period. These two subsets are the inner transition metals.


Inserting the inner transition metals into the main body of the periodic table produces a long and cumbersome table. So that the table can fit nicely on a standard paper size, these elements are commonly placed below the main body of the table.

Alia

Exactly. The sixth-period inner transition metals are called the lanthanides, because they fall after lanthanum, La. Because of their similar physical and chemical properties, they tend to occur mixed together in the same locations in the earth. Also because of their similarities, lanthanides are unusually difficult to purify.


Recently, the commercial use of lanthanides has increased. Several lanthanide elements, for example, are used in the fabrication of the light-emitting diodes (LEDs) of computer monitors and flat screen televisions. Neodymium is useful for making powerful magnets.

Good question! The lanthanides were first discovered within rare ores containing the elements scandium and yttrium. Collectively, these elements have come to be known as the "rare earth metals". The nuclear power industry faces this obstacle because it requires purified samples of two of the most publicized actinides: uranium and plutonium. Actinides heavier than uranium are not commonly found in nature but are synthesized in the laboratory.
