From a chemist's point of view, if a material is pure, it consists of only a single element or a single compound. In pure gold, for example, there is nothing but the element gold. In pure table salt, there is nothing but the compound sodium chloride. If a material is impure, it is a mixture and contains two or more elements or compounds.

According to a chemist's definition, however, orange juice is anything but pure, as it contains a wide variety of materials, including water, pulp, flavorings, vitamins, and sugars.

Alia

Yes! Outside the language of chemistry, sometimes a mixture can be identified as pure. A cook, for example, might ask for pure baking powder. To a chemist, however, this doesn't make sense because baking powder is a mixture of baking soda and sodium aluminum sulfate, plus many other chemicals.

Student

Alia

That's a thoughtful question. Because atoms and molecules are so small, there is a countless number of them in even a tiny sample. If just one atom or molecule out of many were different, then this sample could not be classified as 100 percent pure. Samples can be "purified," however, by various methods, such as distillation. Exactly. Comparing the purity of two samples, the purer one contains fewer impurities. The 99.9999 percent pure water would be much more expensive, because this high degree of purity is rather difficult to attain.

Student

Ok, I think I'm getting the classification part. So chemists divide matter into pure substances and mixtures. What about mixtures - are there different types?

Alia

Yes, mixtures may be heterogeneous or homogeneous. In a heterogeneous mixture, the different components can be seen as individual substances, such as pulp in orange juice, sand in water, or oil globules dispersed in vinegar. The different components are visible.

Student

And homogeneous mixtures? Are those where everything looks the same throughout?

Alia

That's right! Homogeneous mixtures have the same composition throughout as judged by the unaided eye. Any one region of the mixture has the same ratio of substances as does any other region. The reason for this is because the different components are mixed at a very fine level such that the components cannot be seen as individual identifiable entities.

Student

Alia

Yes, that's a perfect example! Once the sugar is completely dissolved, you can't distinguish the sugar from the water with your eyes. Every part of the solution would taste equally sweet.

Student

Are there different types of homogeneous mixtures too?

Alia

Yes, a homogeneous mixture may be either a solution or a suspension. In a solution, all components are in the same phase. The atmosphere we breathe is a gaseous solution consisting of the gaseous elements nitrogen and oxygen as well as minor amounts of other gaseous materials. Salt water is a liquid solution, because both the water and the dissolved sodium chloride are found in a single liquid phase. An example of a solid solution is white gold.

Student

Oh, I didn't know white gold was a mixture! What exactly is it?

Alia

White gold is a mixture of gold with smaller amounts of white metals, such as silver, palladium, or rhodium, that increase its hardness. Unlike gold, the precious metal platinum is used in jewelry in almost its pure form, about 95 percent. Platinum is a very white metal and is also very dense. A platinum ring will feel heavier than a typical yellow or white gold ring, but it will also be much more expensive.

Student

That's fascinating! Now I understand why platinum jewelry costs so much more. What about suspensions? How are they different from solutions?

Alia

A suspension forms when the particles of a substance are finely mixed but not dissolved. The components of a suspension can be of different phases, such as solid particles suspended within a liquid or liquid droplets suspended within a gas. In a suspension, the mixing can be so thorough that the different phases are not readily distinguished.

Student

Can you give me some examples of suspensions I might encounter in everyday life?

Alia

Absolutely! Milk is a suspension because it is a homogeneous mixture of proteins and fats finely dispersed in water. Blood is a suspension composed of finely dispersed blood cells in water. Another example of a suspension is clouds, which are homogeneous mixtures of tiny water droplets suspended in air.

Student

Those are great examples! Is there a way to tell if something is a suspension or a solution just by looking at it?

Alia

Sometimes it can be difficult to distinguish just by looking. One interesting feature of suspensions is that light shining through a suspension results in a visible cone as the light is reflected by the suspended components. But the easiest way to distinguish a suspension from a solution in the laboratory is to spin a sample in a centrifuge.

Student

A centrifuge? That's the spinning machine, right? How does that help?

Alia

Yes, a centrifuge is a device that spins at thousands of revolutions per minute. It separates the components of suspensions but not those of solutions. For example, blood, because it is a suspension, can be centrifuged into its components, which include the blood plasma (a yellowish solution) and white and red blood cells. The components of the plasma cannot be separated from one another because a centrifuge has no effect on solutions.

That's a good question, but no. Notice that blood plasma, white blood cells, and red blood cells can be isolated from blood. None of these components of blood, however, are in themselves pure materials. The plasma contains many dissolved substances.

How much is present is frequently measured in units of milligrams per liter (mg/L), micrograms per liter (μg/L), or nanograms per liter (ng/L) of solution.
