Exactly! From this reasoning, Thomson put forth what he called a "plum-pudding model" of the atom. Alia

It does sound unusual today, but scientists often use familiar objects as analogies. In Thomson's time, plum pudding was a common dessert, especially during Christmas. This model suggested the negative electrons were embedded throughout a positive "pudding" of charge. However, further experimentation soon proved this model to be wrong.

Student

Oh, so they figured out atoms don't look like desserts after all. Who discovered the real structure?

Alia

Around 1910, a more accurate picture of the atom came from one of Thomson's former students, the New Zealand physicist Ernest Rutherford. Rutherford oversaw the now-famous gold-foil experiment, which was the first experiment to show that the atom is mostly empty space and that most of its mass is concentrated in a tiny central core called the atomic nucleus.

Student

Gold foil? How does shooting something through gold help you learn about atoms?

Alia

In Rutherford's experiment, a beam of positively charged particles, called alpha particles, was directed through a very thin sheet of gold foil. Since alpha particles were known to be thousands of times more massive than electrons, it was expected that the alpha-particle stream would not be impeded as it passed through the "atomic pudding" of gold foil.

Student

So they thought the particles would just go straight through without any problem?

That's right! And this was indeed observed to be the case—for the most part. Nearly all alpha particles passed through the gold foil. However, some particles were deflected from their straight-line path as they passed through the foil. A few of them were even deflected straight back toward the source!

Student

Wait, that's weird. If the plum pudding model was right, wouldn't all the particles just pass through?

Alia

You've hit on exactly the critical insight! These alpha particles must have hit something relatively massive inside the atom. This unexpected result led Rutherford to question what was causing some particles to bounce back.

Student

So what did Rutherford think was happening?

Alia

Rutherford reasoned that undeflected particles traveled through regions of the gold foil that were empty space, and the deflected ones were repelled by extremely dense positively charged centers. Each atom, he concluded, must contain one of these centers, which he named the atomic nucleus.

Student

So it's like the particles that got deflected hit something solid in the middle of the atom?

Alia

That's a good way to think about it. Rutherford guessed that atomic nuclei must be positively charged to balance the negative charge of the electrons in the atom. He also guessed that the electrons were not part of this nucleus but still somewhere in the atom. Today we know that the electrons exist outside the nucleus, swirling around it at ultrahigh speeds.

Student

So the electrons are like... I don't know, planets orbiting the sun?

Alia

That's a common analogy people use, though it's not perfectly accurate. Electrons form what can be best described as an ultrathin cloud around the nucleus. What's remarkable is that an atom is mostly empty space, with the diameter of the whole atom being about 10,000 times greater than the diameter of its nucleus.

Student

10,000 times? That's hard to imagine. Is there a better way to picture that?

Alia

Absolutely! If a nucleus were the size of the period at the end of a sentence, the outer edges of the atom would be located 3.3 meters (11 feet) away. Because electrons are even smaller than the nucleus, and because they are widely separated from each other as well as from the nucleus, atoms are indeed mostly empty space—just as our solar system is mostly empty space.

Wow, that's mind-blowing. So atoms are basically tiny solar systems? That's a lot of empty space.

It is quite remarkable! Speaking of Rutherford, there's an interesting historical note about him. Indeed! In addition to discovering the atomic nucleus, Rutherford was also first to characterize and name many nuclear phenomena. At the time, the idea of one element transforming to another was shocking and met with great skepticism, because it seemed reminiscent of alchemy.

That's an excellent question! If atoms are mostly empty space, why don't they simply pass through one another? They don't because electrons repel the electrons of neighboring atoms. Therefore, two atoms can get only so close to each other before they are repelled.

That's a good analogy. This explains why gravity doesn't pull you through the floor as you stand. While the force of gravity pulls you down, the electric force of repulsion between the atoms of the floor and those of your feet pushes you up. As you stand on the floor, these two forces are balanced and you find yourself neither falling nor rising—there's more to standing than most people realize!

In the strictest sense, no! Similarly, when the atoms of your hand push against the atoms of a wall, repulsions between electrons in your hand and electrons in the wall prevent your hand from passing through the wall. You sense this repulsion as a pressure that pushes back. Our sense of touch comes from these electrical repulsions.

That's right! Interestingly, when you touch someone, your atoms and those of the other person do not meet. Instead, atoms from the two of you get close enough so that you sense an electrical repulsion. There is still a tiny, though imperceptible, gap between the two of you.
