Alia

Let's start with understanding unstable nuclei. As we learned in Chapter 4, atoms are made of electrons, neutrons, and protons. The neutrons and protons are at the highly dense center of each atom—its nucleus. Most atoms have stable nuclei, which means they hold together well. This stability is due to an optimal balance of the number of protons with the number of neutrons. These stable nuclei remain unchanged.

Student

So most atoms are stable... but I'm guessing some aren't?

Alia

Exactly! Some atoms have nuclei that are unstable because they contain "off-balance" numbers of protons and neutrons. They may either contain too many neutrons and not enough protons or vice versa. Sooner or later, these nuclei spontaneously change to a more stable composition. For example, excess neutrons may transform into protons to provide a better nuclear balance. During these transformations, the nucleus ejects highly energetic particles while also emitting highly energetic electromagnetic radiation.

Student

Alia

That's a good way to put it! A material containing unstable nuclei undergoing these sorts of transformations is said to be radioactive. The high-energy particles and radiation emitted by a radioactive substance is called radioactivity. Notably, a radioactive nucleus disintegrates as it emits radioactivity. In other words, after emitting the radioactivity, the radioactive nucleus is no longer what it was. We say that the nucleus has "decayed," which is why the process of emitting radioactivity is often called radioactive decay.

Student

Ok, so radioactive things are basically atoms falling apart to become more stable. What exactly comes out when they decay?

Alia

Radioactive atoms emit three distinct types of radiation, named by the first three letters of the Greek alphabet, α, β, γ —alpha, beta, and gamma. Alpha rays carry a positive electric charge, beta rays carry a negative charge, and gamma rays carry no charge. The three rays can be separated by placing a magnetic field across their paths.

Student

I remember from a TV show that magnets can bend the path of charged particles. Is that why the magnetic field separates them?

Alia

That's exactly right! In a magnetic field, alpha rays bend one way, beta rays bend the other way, and gamma rays don't bend at all. Alpha rays bend less than beta rays because alpha particles have more inertia (mass) than beta particles. Think of it like a bowling ball versus a ping pong ball - both will be affected by wind, but the ping pong ball will move much more.

Student

That makes sense. So what exactly are these different types of radiation? They sound dangerous.

Alia

Let me explain each one. Alpha radiation is a stream of alpha particles. An alpha particle is the combination of two protons and two neutrons—in other words, it is the nucleus of the helium atom, atomic number 2. Alpha particles are relatively easy to shield against because of their relatively large size and their double positive charge (+2). For example, they do not normally penetrate such lightweight materials as paper or clothing.

Student

So alpha radiation isn't that dangerous? That's a relief.

Alia

Well, I should clarify - because of their great kinetic energies, alpha particles can cause significant damage to the surface of a material, especially living tissue. When traveling through only a few centimeters of air, alpha particles pick up electrons and become nothing more than harmless helium. As a matter of fact, that's the origin of the helium in a child's balloon—practically all Earth's helium atoms were at one time energetic alpha particles.

Student

Alia

Beta radiation is a stream of beta particles. A beta particle is merely an electron ejected from a radioactive nucleus. We will discuss in Section 5.3 how an electron arises from the nucleus. For now, just keep in mind that a beta particle is a fast-flying electron. A beta particle is normally faster than an alpha particle, and it carries only a single negative charge (–1).

Student

That's an excellent question that shows you're thinking critically! The electrons that surround the nucleus are indeed different from beta particles. For now, just understand that beta particles are not the same as the orbital electrons.

Student

Alia

Beta particles are not as easily stopped as alpha particles, and they are able to penetrate light materials such as paper or clothing. They can penetrate fairly deeply into skin, where they have the potential to harm or kill living cells, but they are unable to penetrate even thin sheets of denser materials, such as aluminum. Beta particles, once stopped, simply become a part of the material they are in, like any other electron.

Student

And gamma rays? I'm guessing those are the worst?

Alia

Gamma rays are the high-frequency electromagnetic radiation emitted by radioactive nuclei. Like photons of visible light, a gamma ray is pure energy. The amount of energy in a gamma ray, however, is much greater than that in visible light, ultraviolet light, or even X rays. Because they have no mass or electric charge, and because of their high energies, gamma rays are able to penetrate through most materials. However, they cannot easily penetrate very dense materials, such as lead.

Student

Alia

That's a great connection! Lead is commonly used as a shielding material in laboratories or hospitals, where there can be much gamma radiation. Delicate molecules inside cells throughout our bodies that are zapped by gamma rays suffer structural damage. Hence, gamma rays generally cause more damage to our cells than does alpha or beta radiation.

Student

This is actually making more sense than I expected. So basically, alpha particles are big and charged, so they're easily stopped but damaging on the surface. Beta particles are smaller, so they penetrate more, and gamma rays have no mass or charge, so they go through almost everything?

Alia

That's an excellent summary! The relative penetrating power of the three types of radiation is exactly as you described. Alpha particles are the least penetrating and can be stopped by a few sheets of paper. Beta particles will readily pass through paper but not through a sheet of aluminum. Gamma rays penetrate several centimeters into solid lead.
