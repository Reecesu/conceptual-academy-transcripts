As we learned previously, atoms are made of electrons, neutrons, and protons. The neutrons and protons are at the highly dense center of each atom—its nucleus. These stable nuclei remain unchanged.


Some atoms, however, have nuclei that are unstable because they contain "off-balance" numbers of protons and neutrons. They may either contain too many neutrons and not enough protons or vice versa. Sooner or later, these nuclei spontaneously change to a more stable composition.

A material containing unstable nuclei undergoing these sorts of transformations is said to be radioactive. The high-energy particles and radiation emitted by a radioactive substance is called radioactivity. Notably, a radioactive nucleus disintegrates as it emits radioactivity. In other words, after emitting the radioactivity, the radioactive nucleus is no longer what it was. We say that the nucleus has "decayed," which is why the process of emitting radioactivity is often called radioactive decay.
