Hi Alia. I need to learn about radioactivity for my science class. To be honest, I've always been a bit scared of anything related to radiation. It seems dangerous and unnatural.

Alia

That's a common concern! Let's talk about radioactivity. Many people share the misconception that radioactivity is something new in our environment or entirely human-made. Actually, radioactivity has been around far longer than humans. It has always been in the soil we walk on and in the air that we breathe, and it even warms Earth's interior. In fact, radioactive decay in Earth's interior heats the water that spurts from a geyser or wells up from a natural hot spring.

Student

Wait, so you're saying radioactivity is natural? I always thought it was just from nuclear power plants and bombs.

Alia

Yes, radioactivity is absolutely natural. Most of the radiation we encounter is natural background radiation that originates from the Earth, from the Sun, and from other stars. At sea level, the protective blanket of the atmosphere reduces this radiation, but radiation is more intense at higher altitudes, where the air is thinner. For example, in Denver, the "Mile-High City," a person receives more than twice as much cosmic radiation as someone does at sea level.

Student

That's surprising! I never realized people in Denver get more radiation just from living at a higher altitude. What about radiation that comes from Earth itself?

Alia

A common source of radiation of an Earthly origin is radon-222, an inert gas arising from uranium, which is widely found at low levels within all rock, soil, and water. Radon is a heavy gas that tends to accumulate in basements after it seeps up through cracks in the floor.

Student

Oh, I think my parents had our basement tested for radon once. Is it really that common?

Alia

Yes, it is. Levels of radon vary from region to region, depending upon local geology. You can check the radon level in your home with a radon detector kit. If levels are high, corrective measures, such as sealing the basement floor and walls and maintaining adequate ventilation, should be taken. Radon gas poses a serious health risk.

Student

So if most radiation is natural, how much of our radiation exposure comes from human activities like nuclear power?

Alia

That's a great question. About 20 percent of our annual exposure to radiation comes from sources outside of nature, primarily medical procedures. Fallout from nuclear testing and the coal and nuclear power industries are also contributors. Interestingly, the coal industry outranks the nuclear power industry as a source of radiation. The global combustion of coal annually releases about 13,000 tons of radioactive thorium and uranium into the atmosphere, in addition to other environmentally damaging molecules, including greenhouse gases.

Student

Wait, coal power plants release radiation? I had no idea!

Alia

Yes, both thorium and uranium are found naturally in coal deposits, so their release is a natural consequence of burning coal. Nuclear power plants also produce radioactive by-products. Worldwide, the nuclear power industries generate about 10,000 tons of radioactive waste each year. Most of this waste, however, is contained and not released into the environment.

Student

I'm starting to understand better now. But I'm still worried about what radiation does to our bodies. Isn't it really harmful?

Alia

When radiation encounters our intricately structured cells, it can create chaos. However, cells are able to repair most kinds of damage caused by radiation if the damage is not too severe. A cell can survive an otherwise lethal dose of radiation if the dose is spread over a long period of time to allow intervals for healing. When radiation is sufficient to kill cells, the dead cells can be replaced by new ones.

Student

That's good to know that our bodies can repair some radiation damage. But what happens if the radiation doesn't kill the cell but damages it?

Alia

Sometimes a radiated cell will survive with damaged DNA. This can alter the genetic information contained in a cell, producing one or more mutations. Although the effects of most mutations are inconsequential in terms of a person's health, some mutations affect the functioning of cells. Genetic mutations are the cause of most cancers, for example. In addition, mutations that occur in an individual's reproductive cells can be passed to the individual's offspring. In this case, the mutation will be present in every cell in the offspring organism's body—and may well have an effect on the functioning of the organism.

Student

That sounds scary. How do we measure how much radiation is dangerous?

Alia

We measure the ability of radiation to cause harm to living tissue in rems. Lethal doses of radiation begin at 500 rems. A person has about a 50 percent chance of surviving a dose of this magnitude received over a short period of time. During radiation therapy, a patient may receive localized doses in excess of 200 rems each day for a period of weeks.

Student

500 rems sounds like a lot. What about regular exposure—like from getting an X-ray at the doctor?

Alia

Throughout our lives, all the radiation we receive from natural sources and medical procedures is only a fraction of 1 rem. For convenience, the smaller unit millirem is used; 1 millirem (mrem) is 1/1000 of a rem. The average person in the United States is exposed to about 360 millirems a year. About 80 percent of this radiation comes from natural sources, such as cosmic rays—radiation from our sun as well as other stars—and the Earth.

Student

And what about medical X-rays?

Alia

A typical diagnostic X-ray exposes a person to between 5 and 30 millirems (0.005 and 0.030 rem), which is less than 1/10,000 of the lethal dose.

Student

Oh, that's a relief. I've always been nervous about getting X-rays. Are there any positive uses for radioactive materials?

Alia

Absolutely! Radioactive isotopes can be incorporated into molecules whose location can then be traced by the radiation they emit. When used in this way, radioactive isotopes are called tracers. For example, to check the action of a fertilizer, researchers incorporate radioactive isotopes into the molecules of the fertilizer and then apply the fertilizer to plants. The amount taken up by the plants can be measured with radiation detectors.

Student

That's pretty cool! So scientists can actually see where the fertilizer goes in the plant?

Alia

Exactly. From such measurements, scientists can tell farmers how much fertilizer to use, because fertilizer uptake is a physical and chemical process that is not affected by the radioactivity of the materials involved.

Student

Are there other industrial uses for tracers?

Alia

Yes, tracers are also used in industry. Motor oil manufacturers can quantify the lubricating qualities of their products by running oil in engines containing small but measurable amounts of radioactive isotopes. As the engine runs and the pistons rub against the inner chambers, some of the metal from the engine invariably makes its way into the motor oil, and this metal carries with it the embedded radioactive isotopes. The better the lubricating qualities of a motor oil, the fewer radioactive isotopes it will contain after running in the engine for a given length of time.

Student

That's fascinating! I never would have thought of using radiation to test motor oil. What about medical uses? I've heard about people getting nuclear medicine scans.

Alia

In a technique known as medical imaging, isotopes are used for the diagnosis of internal disorders. Small amounts of a radioactive material such as sodium iodide, NaI, which contains the radioactive isotope iodine-131, are administered to a patient and traced through the body with a radiation detector. The result is an image that shows how the material is distributed in the patient's body.

Student

How does that work? Does the radiation damage the body?

Alia

This technique works because the path the tracer material takes is influenced only by its physical and chemical properties, not by its radioactivity. The tracer may be introduced alone or along with some other chemical, known as a carrier compound, that helps target the isotope to a particular type of tissue in the body. For example, the thyroid gland, located in the neck, absorbs much of the iodine that enters the body through food and drink. Images of the thyroid gland can be obtained by giving a patient the radioactive isotope iodine-131. These images are useful in diagnosing metabolic disorders.

Student

I'm actually starting to see how radioactivity can be useful, not just dangerous. Are there any everyday applications of radioactive elements that might surprise me?

Alia

Here's an interesting fact: The only element beyond uranium to find a commercial application is americium, which is a key component of nearly all household smoke detectors. This element completes an electric circuit by ionizing air within a chamber. Smoke particles interfere with this ionization, thus breaking the circuit and triggering the alarm.

Student

Really? There's a radioactive element in my smoke detector? That's both surprising and kind of cool. I'm starting to see that radioactivity is all around us in ways I never realized. And mostly it's not something to be afraid of.

Alia

That's right! And here's another interesting fact about Earth's natural radioactivity: The deeper you go below the Earth's surface, the hotter it gets. At a depth of merely 30 kilometers, the temperature is over 500°C. At greater depths, it is so hot that rock melts into magma, which can rise to the Earth's surface to escape as lava. The main reason it gets hotter down below is that the Earth contains an abundance of radioactive isotopes and is heated as it absorbs the radiation from these isotopes. So, volcanoes, geysers, and hot springs are all powered by radioactivity. Even the drifting of continents (plate tectonics) is a consequence of the Earth's internal radioactivity.
