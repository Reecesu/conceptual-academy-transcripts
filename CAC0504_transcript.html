It's actually a fascinating concept! When a radioactive nucleus emits an alpha or beta particle, the atomic number of the nucleus is changed. This means it becomes another element entirely.

Student

Alia

That's a great observation! In fact, transmutation is the changing of one element to another, and it's the same word that alchemists used. But unlike the mystical processes alchemists imagined, this is a natural process that follows the laws of physics. Let me give you a specific example from your textbook to make this clearer.

Student

That would help a lot, thanks.

Alia

Consider a uranium-238 nucleus, which contains 92 protons and 146 neutrons. When an alpha particle is ejected from this nucleus, it loses two protons and two neutrons. Since an element is defined by the number of protons in its nucleus, the 90 protons and 144 neutrons remaining no longer constitute a uranium atom. What we have now is the nucleus of a different element—thorium.

Student

So uranium just naturally turns into thorium? I didn't realize elements could change like that.

Alia

Yes, through this natural radioactive decay process. We can write this transmutation as a nuclear equation. It would look like this:
₉₂²³⁸U → ₉₀²³⁴Th + ₂⁴He

Student

Those little numbers are confusing me a bit. What do they represent?

Alia

Great question! In this notation, the bottom number (the subscript) represents the atomic number, which is the number of protons. The top number (the superscript) represents the mass number, which is the total number of protons and neutrons. So uranium-238 has 92 protons and a mass number of 238, meaning it has 146 neutrons (238 - 92 = 146).

Student

So in this equation, uranium-238 turns into thorium plus... is that helium?

Alia

Exactly! That ₂⁴He is the alpha particle, which is essentially a helium nucleus. When this transmutation occurs, energy is released in three forms: partly as kinetic energy of the alpha particle, partly as kinetic energy of the thorium atom, and partly as gamma radiation.

Student

I see. So does the equation have to balance somehow?

Alia

That's an excellent question! Yes, in this and all such nuclear equations, two things must balance: the mass numbers at the top and the atomic numbers at the bottom. In our example, the mass numbers balance because 238 = 234 + 4. The atomic numbers also balance because 92 = 90 + 2.

Student

That makes sense. So what happens to the thorium? Is it stable?

Alia

Actually, the thorium-234 produced in this reaction is also radioactive. When it decays, it emits a beta particle. The formation of a beta particle is interesting because it results in an additional proton, which means the atomic number of the resulting nucleus increases by 1.

Student

So after thorium with 90 protons decays, the new element would have 91 protons?

Alia

That's exactly right! After beta emission by thorium with 90 protons, the resulting element has 91 protons. It's no longer thorium but the element protactinium. Although the atomic number has increased by 1 in this process, the mass number (protons + neutrons) remains the same. The nuclear equation for this is:
₉₀²³⁴Th → ₉₁²³⁴Pa + ₋₁⁰e

Student

This is starting to make more sense. So the "e" is the electron being emitted?

Alia

Yes! We write an electron as ₋₁⁰e. The superscript 0 indicates that the electron's mass is insignificant relative to that of protons and neutrons. The subscript –1 represents the electric charge of the electron.

Student

Are there other particles involved in beta decay? I vaguely remember hearing something about neutrinos.

Alia

That's an excellent point! Beta emission is also accompanied by the emission of a neutrino, which is a neutral particle with nearly zero mass that travels at about the speed of light. Neutrinos are fascinating because they're incredibly hard to detect. They interact very weakly with matter—a piece of lead about eight light-years thick would be needed to stop half the neutrinos produced in typical nuclear decays.

Student

Eight light-years of lead? That's incredible!

Alia

It really is! And here's something that might surprise you: thousands of neutrinos are flying through your body every second of every day, because the universe is filled with them. Only occasionally, one or two times a year, does a neutrino interact with an atom of your body.

Alia

That's one of the fascinating aspects of nuclear physics! Now, to summarize what we've learned about transmutation: when an element ejects an alpha particle from its nucleus, the mass number of the remaining atom is decreased by 4 and its atomic number is decreased by 2. The resulting atom is an atom of the element two spaces back in the periodic table, because this atom has two fewer protons.

Student

And what about beta decay again?

Alia

When an element ejects a beta particle from its nucleus, the mass of the atom is practically unaffected, meaning there is no change in its mass number, but its atomic number increases by 1. The resulting atom becomes the element one space forward in the periodic table because it has one more proton.

That's an excellent question! The decay of uranium-238 doesn't continue indefinitely. It follows a specific decay chain, eventually ending as lead-206. Exactly! All uranium ultimately becomes lead. Along the way, it exists as the various elements indicated in the decay chain - thorium, protactinium, and several others before finally becoming stable lead-206.
