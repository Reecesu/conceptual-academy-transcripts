Alia

That's a great starting point. Let me explain how this process begins. Cosmic rays continually bombard the Earth's atmosphere. This bombardment causes many atoms in the upper atmosphere to transmute. These transmutations result in many protons and neutrons being "sprayed out" into the environment.

Student

Wait, what are cosmic rays exactly? And what does "transmute" mean?

Alia

Good questions! Cosmic rays are high-energy particles that come from space. "Transmute" means to change from one element to another. When these cosmic rays hit atoms in our upper atmosphere, they can change those atoms into different elements by altering their nuclear structure.


When this happens, protons and neutrons can be knocked loose. Most of the protons are stopped as they collide with other atoms in the upper atmosphere. These protons strip electrons from the atoms they collide with and thus become hydrogen atoms. The neutrons, however, continue for longer distances because they have no electric charge and therefore don't interact electrically with matter.

Student

So the neutrons can travel further because they don't have a charge... that makes sense. What happens to these neutrons then?

Alia

Eventually, many of these neutrons collide with atomic nuclei in the lower atmosphere. A particularly important reaction happens when a neutron collides with a nitrogen atom. When a nitrogen atom captures a neutron, it becomes an isotope of carbon by emitting a proton.


Specifically, this creates carbon-14, which is a radioactive isotope of carbon. The reaction looks like this: a nitrogen-14 atom plus a neutron produces carbon-14 plus a proton.

Student

Oh, so that's how carbon-14 is formed! I've heard of carbon-12 which I think is the normal carbon, right? How is carbon-14 different?

Alia

You're absolutely right. Carbon-12 is indeed the most common isotope of carbon, making up about 99% of all carbon on Earth. Carbon-14 makes up less than one-millionth of 1 percent of the carbon in the atmosphere.


The difference between them is in their nuclei. Carbon-12 has six protons and six neutrons, while carbon-14 has six protons and eight neutrons. That's why we call it carbon-14 - the number represents the total number of protons and neutrons. Carbon-12 is stable, but carbon-14 is radioactive, which means it will eventually decay into another element.

Student

So both carbon-12 and carbon-14 are present in the atmosphere. But how does this help with dating things?

Alia

That's where it gets interesting! Because both carbon-12 and carbon-14 are forms of carbon, they have the same chemical properties. Both of these isotopes, for example, chemically react with oxygen to form carbon dioxide, which is consumed by plants through the process of photosynthesis. This means that all plants contain a tiny quantity of radioactive carbon-14.

Student

So plants absorb both regular carbon-12 and radioactive carbon-14. But how do animals end up with carbon-14?

Alia

All animals eat either plants or plant-eating animals; therefore, all animals, including humans, have a little carbon-14 in them. So we can see why all living things on Earth contain some carbon-14.

Student

That makes me a little nervous to hear I have radioactive material in my body right now...

Alia

That's a common reaction! But don't worry - the amount is incredibly small and not harmful. It's actually a natural part of all life on Earth. This radioactive isotope eventually decays back into nitrogen as it emits a beta particle. The reaction is basically the reverse of how it formed: carbon-14 decays to nitrogen-14 plus a beta particle (which is essentially an electron).

Student

OK, so I understand how carbon-14 gets into living things. But I still don't understand how this helps determine the age of ancient objects.

Alia

That's the key question! Here's how it works: Because plants absorb carbon dioxide while alive, any carbon-14 that decays is immediately replenished with fresh carbon-14 from the atmosphere. In this way, a radioactive equilibrium is reached in which there is a constant ratio of about 1 carbon-14 atom to every 100 billion carbon-12 atoms.


When a plant dies, replenishment of carbon-14 ends. Then the percentage of carbon-14 decreases at a constant rate determined by its half-life. The amount of carbon-12, however, does not change, because this isotope does not undergo radioactive decay. The longer a plant or other organism is dead, the less carbon-14 it contains relative to the constant amount of carbon-12.

Student

So it's like a clock that starts ticking down when something dies! That's actually pretty cool. So how fast does this carbon-14 disappear?

Alia

Exactly - it's like a molecular clock! The half-life of carbon-14 is about 5730 years. This means that half of the carbon-14 atoms now present in a plant or animal that dies today will decay in the next 5730 years. Half of the remaining carbon-14 atoms will then decay in the following 5730 years, and so on.

Student

So if I found something that only had half as much carbon-14 as a living plant, it would be about 5730 years old?

Alia

That's exactly right! With this knowledge, scientists are able to calculate the age of carbon-containing artifacts, such as wooden tools or ancient skeletons. Their age is measured by their current level of radioactivity compared to living organisms. This process is known as carbon-14 dating.

Student

Is there a limit to how old something can be for carbon dating to work?

Alia

Yes, there is. Carbon-14 dating enables investigators to probe as far as 50,000 years into the past. Beyond this time span, there is too little carbon-14 remaining for accurate analysis. After many half-lives, the amount becomes too small to measure precisely.

Student

So if I have some piece of wood that's a million years old, carbon dating wouldn't work?

Alia

That's correct. For something that old, there would be virtually no carbon-14 left to measure. After about 50,000 years (which is roughly 9 half-lives), the amount of remaining carbon-14 is less than 0.2% of the original amount, making it extremely difficult to detect accurately with current technology.

Student

Is carbon dating perfectly accurate? Like if something is dated to 500 years, is it exactly 500 years?

Alia

That's an important point. Because of fluctuations in cosmic ray bombardment rates over the centuries, carbon dating has an uncertainty of about 15 percent. This means, for example, that the straw of an old adobe brick dated to be 500 years old may really be only 425 years old on the low side or 575 years old on the high side. For many purposes, this is an acceptable level of uncertainty.

Student

Let me see if I can apply this. If an archaeologist finds an old wooden ax handle and extracts carbon from it that's only one-fourth as radioactive as a fresh tree branch, how old would the ax handle be?

Alia

That's a great question to test your understanding! Let's think through this. If the radioactivity is one-fourth of the original, that means the carbon-14 has gone through two half-lives.


In the first half-life (5730 years), the radioactivity would drop to 1/2 of the original.
In the second half-life (another 5730 years), it would drop to 1/4 of the original.


So the ax handle would be approximately 11,460 years old (2 × 5730 years), assuming the ratio of C-14/C-12 was the same when the ax was made as it is in freshly cut wood today.

Student

That makes sense! But what about really old things like rocks? Carbon dating wouldn't work for those since they weren't alive, right?

Alia

Excellent observation! Artifacts derived from nonliving materials can also be dated, but using different radioactive isotopes with much longer half-lives. The naturally occurring mineral isotopes uranium-238 and uranium-235, for example, decay very slowly and ultimately become lead—but not the common isotope lead-208.


Instead, uranium-238 decays to lead-206. Uranium-235, on the other hand, decays to lead-207. Thus, the lead-206 and lead-207 that now exist in a uranium-bearing rock were at one time uranium. Older rocks contain higher percentages of these trace isotopes.

Student

So for rocks, scientists look at how much uranium has turned into specific types of lead?

Alia

That's right! If you know the half-lives of uranium isotopes and the percentage of lead isotopes in some uranium-bearing rock, you can calculate the date of the rock's formation. Rocks dated in this manner have been found to be as much as 3.7 billion years old. Samples from the Moon have been dated at 4.2 billion years, which is close to the estimated age of our solar system: 4.6 billion years.

Wow, that's amazing that we can determine ages that far back! Who figured all this out initially?

Alia

Carbon-14 dating was developed by the American chemist Willard F. Libby at the University of Chicago in the 1950s. For this groundbreaking work, he received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1960. His technique revolutionized archaeology and paleontology by giving scientists a reliable method to date organic materials.
