Not to worry! This is a natural process that's been happening throughout Earth's history. Most of the protons that are released are stopped as they collide with atoms in the upper atmosphere. When these protons collide, they strip electrons from the atoms and become hydrogen atoms. The neutrons, however, continue for longer distances because they have no electric charge and therefore don't interact electrically with matter. Eventually, many neutrons collide with atomic nuclei in the lower atmosphere.

Student

So what happens when these neutrons hit atoms in our atmosphere?

Alia

A good example is when a nitrogen atom captures a neutron. When this happens, the nitrogen atom becomes an isotope of carbon by emitting a proton. Specifically, it becomes carbon-14.

Student

Wait, is that the carbon-14 I've heard about in carbon dating?

Alia

Exactly! This carbon-14 isotope makes up less than one-millionth of 1 percent of the carbon in the atmosphere. It's radioactive and has eight neutrons, unlike the most common isotope, carbon-12, which has six neutrons and is not radioactive.

Student

So there are different types of carbon atoms? That's confusing.

Alia

Yes, these are called isotopes. The important thing to understand is that both carbon-12 and carbon-14 are forms of carbon, so they have the same chemical properties. Both of these isotopes, for example, chemically react with oxygen to form carbon dioxide, which is consumed by plants through the process of photosynthesis. This means that all plants contain a tiny quantity of radioactive carbon-14.

Student

Oh, so plants absorb both regular carbon and radioactive carbon. But why would I care about plants having radioactive carbon?

Alia

That's where the connection to living things comes in! All animals eat either plants or plant-eating animals; therefore, all animals, including humans, have a little carbon-14 in them. So we can see why all living things on Earth contain some carbon-14.

Student

Wait, I have radioactive material in my body right now? That can't be good!

Alia

It's completely natural and present in very small amounts. This radioactive isotope ultimately decays back into nitrogen as it emits a beta particle. It's part of the natural cycle of elements on our planet.

Student

I still don't understand how this helps with dating old things.

Alia

Let me explain that connection. Because plants absorb carbon dioxide while alive, any carbon-14 that decays is immediately replenished with fresh carbon-14 from the atmosphere. In this way, a radioactive equilibrium is reached in which there is a constant ratio of about 1 carbon-14 atom to every 100 billion carbon-12 atoms.

Student

So while something is alive, it maintains a steady level of carbon-14?

Alia

That's right! But here's the key point: When a plant dies, replenishment of carbon-14 ends. Then the percentage of carbon-14 decreases at a constant rate determined by its half-life. The amount of carbon-12, however, does not change, because this isotope does not undergo radioactive decay. The longer a plant or other organism is dead, the less carbon-14 it contains relative to the constant amount of carbon-12.

Student

Half-life? That's another term I've heard but don't really understand.

Alia

The half-life is the time it takes for half of the radioactive atoms to decay. The half-life of carbon-14 is about 5730 years. This means that half of the carbon-14 atoms now present in a plant or animal that dies today will decay in the next 5730 years. Half of the remaining carbon-14 atoms will then decay in the following 5730 years, and so on.

Student

So if I found something really old, like a wooden tool, it would have less carbon-14 than a living tree today?

Alia

Exactly! With this knowledge, scientists are able to calculate the age of carbon-containing artifacts, such as wooden tools or ancient skeletons. Their age is measured by their current level of radioactivity. This process, known as carbon-14 dating, enables investigators to probe as far as 50,000 years into the past. Beyond this time span, there is too little carbon-14 remaining for accurate analysis.

Student

That's actually pretty cool. So you can just measure how radioactive something is and that tells you how old it is?

Alia

That's the basic idea! Let me give you a visual example. Imagine a skeleton that was once alive. When it was alive, it had a certain amount of carbon-14. After 5730 years, that amount would be reduced by half. After another 5730 years, the remaining amount would be reduced by half again. So today, a very old skeleton contains only a trace amount of the original carbon-14.

Student

Alia

That's an excellent question! Because of fluctuations in cosmic ray bombardment rates over the centuries, carbon dating has an uncertainty of about 15 percent. This means, for example, that the straw of an old adobe brick dated to be 500 years old may really be only 425 years old on the low side or 575 years old on the high side. For many purposes, this is an acceptable level of uncertainty.

Alia

Let's work through that example! Suppose that an archaeologist extracts 1 gram of carbon from an ancient ax handle and finds that it is one-fourth as radioactive as 1 gram of carbon extracted from a freshly cut tree branch. If we know that carbon-14 reduces by half every 5730 years, we can calculate the age.

Student

One-fourth as radioactive... so that means it's gone through how many half-lives?

Alia

Let's think about it. After one half-life, the radioactivity would be 1/2 of the original. After two half-lives, it would be 1/4 of the original. So if the ax handle is one-fourth as radioactive as a fresh sample, it has gone through two half-lives.

So that would be... 5730 years times 2, which is 11,460 years old? That's ancient!

Alia

That's correct! Assuming the ratio of C-14/C-12 was the same when the ax was made, the ax handle is as old as two half-lives of C-14, or about 11,460 years old.

But what about things that aren't made from living things, like rocks? Can you date those too?

Alia

Yes, artifacts derived from nonliving materials can also be dated based on the radioactive minerals they contain. The naturally occurring mineral isotopes uranium-238 and uranium-235, for example, decay very slowly and ultimately become lead—but not the common isotope lead-208.

It does, through a series of decay steps. Uranium-238 decays to lead-206, while uranium-235 decays to lead-207. Thus, the lead-206 and lead-207 that now exist in a uranium-bearing rock were at one time uranium. Older rocks contain higher percentages of these trace isotopes.

So you can figure out how old rocks are by measuring the amount of these special lead isotopes?

That's right! If you know the half-lives of uranium isotopes and the percentage of lead isotopes in some uranium-bearing rock, you can calculate the date of the rock's formation. Rocks dated in this manner have been found to be as much as 3.7 billion years old. Samples from the Moon have been dated at 4.2 billion years, which is close to the estimated age of our solar system: 4.6 billion years.
