Student

Hi Alia. I need to learn about nuclear fission for my science class. I've always found physics intimidating, especially nuclear physics. It sounds complicated and dangerous.

Alia

I understand your concerns. Many students find nuclear physics intimidating at first, but we'll approach it step by step. Nuclear fission is actually a fascinating process that can be understood through some basic principles. Would you like to explore what nuclear fission is and how it works?

Student

Yes, that would be helpful. Does "fission" mean splitting something?

Alia

Exactly! Just as biology students know that living tissue grows by the division of cells, with the splitting in half of living cells called fission, the splitting of an atomic nucleus into two smaller halves is called nuclear fission.

Student

So it's like cell division but for atoms? That makes it a little less intimidating.

Alia

That's a great connection. Nuclear fission involves the delicate balance between two forces within the nucleus. One force is the strong nuclear force, which holds all the nucleons together. The second force is the repulsive electric force occurring among all the like-sign protons.

Student

Wait, so there are forces fighting against each other inside the nucleus? I thought atoms were just stable little balls.

Alia

That's a common misconception. In most nuclei, the strong nuclear force dominates and keeps everything stable. However, in uranium, this domination is weak. If the uranium nucleus is stretched into an elongated shape, the electric forces may push it into an even more elongated shape. If the elongation passes a critical point, the electric forces overwhelm the strong nuclear forces, and the nucleus splits. This splitting process is what we call nuclear fission.

Student

So basically, the protons all have positive charges so they want to push away from each other, but normally the strong nuclear force holds them together?

Alia

That's exactly right! Let me describe a figure that illustrates this: When an elongation of the nucleus occurs, the repulsive electric force can overcome the attractive strong nuclear force, resulting in fission.

Student

What causes the nucleus to get elongated in the first place?

Alia

The absorption of a neutron by a uranium nucleus supplies enough energy to cause such an elongation. The resulting fission process may produce many different combinations of smaller nuclei. More significantly, the energy released by fission is enormous—about 7 million times that of a TNT molecule explosion.

Student

7 MILLION times?! That's hard to believe. Where does all that energy come from?

Alia

This energy is mainly in the form of the kinetic energy of the fission fragments, which fly apart from each other. A much smaller amount of energy is released as gamma radiation. Let me share a typical uranium fission reaction equation with you:


ₙ¹ + ᵤ²³⁵ → ₃₆Kr⁹² + ₅₆Ba¹⁴¹ + 3ₙ¹ + energy

Student

That equation looks complicated. What does it mean exactly?

Alia

Let me break this down for you. In this reaction, 1 neutron starts the fission of a single uranium nucleus, which produces nuclear fragments (krypton-92 and barium-141) and 3 neutrons. These 3 neutrons can then cause the fission of 3 more uranium atoms, releasing 9 more neutrons. If each of these 9 neutrons succeeds in splitting a uranium atom, the next step in the reaction produces 27 neutrons, and so on.

Student

So it's like a domino effect where each split causes more splits? That sounds dangerous.

Alia

That's an excellent analogy! This sequence is called a chain reaction. A chain reaction is a self-sustaining reaction in which the products of one reaction event initiate further reaction events.

Student

So if one neutron creates three more, which each create three more... couldn't this get out of control really fast? Why don't chunks of uranium just spontaneously explode?

Alia

That's an excellent question! Chain reactions do not happen in naturally occurring uranium ore deposits for a few important reasons. Fission occurs mainly in the rare isotope U-235, which makes up only 0.7 percent of the uranium in pure uranium metal. That's only about 1 part in 140 of naturally occurring uranium.

Student

Oh, so natural uranium is mostly something else?

Alia

Yes, exactly. When the more abundant isotope U-238 absorbs neutrons created by fission of U-235, the U-238 typically does not undergo fission. So, any chain reaction is snuffed out by the neutron-absorbing U-238, as well as by the rock in which the ore is embedded.

Student

That's a relief! So natural uranium has a built-in safety mechanism.

Alia

Nature does provide that safety feature. Furthermore, chain reactions are more effective in large chunks of uranium than in smaller chunks. In smaller chunks, neutrons easily find the surface and escape. As the neutrons escape, the chain reaction no longer builds up.

Student

That makes sense - like if I drop a match on a small pile of leaves versus a big one, the small pile might not catch fire as easily because the heat escapes.

Alia

That's an excellent everyday comparison! In a small piece of uranium, the surface area is large relative to its mass. In a larger piece, more uranium and less surface are presented to the neutrons. If two small chunks of uranium are suddenly pushed together, they make a larger chunk where neutrons are no longer able to escape as easily. Instead, they continue the chain reaction, which becomes sustainable.

Student

So there's some minimum size needed for this to work?

Alia

Precisely. The minimum size of a chunk needed for a sustainable chain reaction is called the critical mass. Any chunk at or above the critical mass produces a steady release of energy. With the correct engineering, this release of energy can be controlled, which is what happens within a nuclear power plant. A different sort of engineering produces an explosion of energy, which is the basis of a nuclear fission bomb.

Student

So that's how nuclear bombs work? Just push two pieces together?

Alia

In simplified terms, yes. A uranium-fission bomb works like this: Two smaller masses of uranium are initially kept apart from each other. The bomb is ignited when high explosives (gunpowder) force the two masses to come together into a single lump with a critical mass.


But constructing a fission bomb is a formidable task. The difficulty is in separating enough uranium-235 from the more abundant uranium-238. Separation is difficult because, except for their slightly different masses, the two isotopes have the same physical and chemical properties.

Student

So they're chemically identical but just have different weights?

Alia

That's correct. Scientists took more than 2 years to extract enough of the 235 isotope from uranium ore to make the bomb that was detonated at Hiroshima in 1945. To this day, uranium isotope separation, also known as uranium enrichment, remains a difficult process.

Student

I've heard about nuclear power plants. Are they basically just controlled nuclear bombs?

Alia

That's actually a misconception. The awesome energy of nuclear fission was introduced to the world in the form of nuclear bombs, and this violent image still colors our thinking about nuclear power, making it difficult for many people to recognize its potential usefulness.


Currently, about 20 percent of electric energy in the United States is generated by nuclear fission reactors, whereas most electric power is nuclear in some other countries—about 75 percent in France.

Student

So what's the difference between a bomb and a power plant?

Alia

Nuclear reactors are simply nuclear furnaces. They, like fossil fuel furnaces, do nothing more elegant than boil water to produce steam for a turbine. The greatest practical difference is the amount of fuel involved: a mere 1 kilogram of uranium fuel, less than the size of a baseball, yields more energy than 30 freight-car loads of coal.

Student

Wow, that's incredibly efficient! But how do they keep it from exploding?

Alia

A fission reactor contains four components: nuclear fuel, control rods, moderator (to slow the velocity of the neutrons, making them effective for the fission process), and a liquid (usually water) to transfer heat from the reactor to the turbine and generator.


The nuclear fuel is primarily U-238, plus about 3 percent U-235. Because the U-235 isotopes are so highly diluted with U-238, an explosion like that of a nuclear bomb is not possible. The reaction rate, which depends on the number of neutrons that initiate the fission of other U-235 nuclei, is controlled by rods inserted into the reactor. The control rods are made of a neutron-absorbing material, usually metals like cadmium or boron.

Student

So the control rods can slow down the reaction if it's going too fast?

Alia

Exactly. Heated water around the nuclear fuel is kept under high pressure to keep it at a high temperature without boiling. It transfers heat to a second, lower-pressure water system, which operates the turbine and electric generator in a conventional fashion.

Student

Are there two separate water systems? Why?

Alia

Yes, in this design, two separate water systems are used so that no radioactivity reaches the turbine or the outside environment. The entire setup resides inside a building with a dome-shaped containment structure.

Student

I've seen those dome buildings in pictures. I guess they're designed for safety?

Alia

Yes, the nuclear reactor is housed within a dome-shaped containment building that is designed to prevent the release of radioactive isotopes in the event of an accident. The Soviet-built Chernobyl nuclear power plant that reached meltdown in 1986 had no such containment building, leading to the release of massive amounts of radiation into the surrounding environment.

Student

That makes me think about waste. Isn't radioactive waste a big problem with nuclear power?

Alia

You've touched on a significant disadvantage of fission power: the generation of radioactive waste products. Light atomic nuclei are most stable when composed of equal numbers of protons and neutrons, as we discussed earlier, and heavy nuclei need more neutrons than protons for stability.


For example, there are 143 neutrons but only 92 protons in U-235. When uranium fissions into two medium-weight elements, the extra neutrons in their nuclei make them unstable. They are radioactive, most with very short half-lives, but some with half-lives of thousands of years.

Student

So we're stuck with this dangerous waste for thousands of years? That doesn't sound good.

Alia

Safely disposing of these waste products, as well as materials made radioactive in the production of nuclear fuels, requires special storage casks and procedures. Although fission has been successfully producing electricity for a half century, disposing of radioactive wastes in the United States remains problematic.

Student

What about meltdowns? I've heard about Fukushima and Chernobyl - those sound terrifying.

Alia

Those are indeed serious accidents. A nuclear power plant "meltdown" occurs when the fissioning nuclear fuels are no longer submerged within a cooling fluid, such as water. The temperature rises to the point that the solid nuclear fuel and the reaction vessel itself melt into a liquid phase that has the potential of penetrating through the floor of the containment building.

Student

Are there any benefits to nuclear power? It seems to have a lot of risks.

Alia

There are significant benefits to fission power: plentiful electricity, conservation of the many billions of tons of fossil fuels that every year are literally turned to heat and smoke (and in the long run may be far more precious as sources of organic molecules than as sources of heat), and the elimination of the megatons of sulfur oxides and other poisons that are put into the air each year by the burning of these fossil fuels.

Student

I guess there are trade-offs with any energy source. I have a question though - you mentioned earlier that small chunks of uranium don't reach critical mass. Why not just split a big piece into small pieces to stop the reaction?

Alia

That's an excellent question! Let me pose this concept check to you: A 52-kilogram ball of uranium-235 has critical mass, but the same ball broken up into small chunks does not. Can you explain why?

Student

Hmm, let me think... Is it because the small pieces have more surface area in total compared to the big ball? So more neutrons can escape?

Alia

Excellent reasoning! The small chunks have more combined surface area than the ball from which they came. Neutrons escape via the surface of each small chunk before a sustained chain reaction can build up.

Student

That makes sense! So nuclear reactors have to carefully control the configuration of the uranium to keep the reaction going steadily.

Alia

Exactly! And here's an interesting fact: Sustainable fission chain reactions are a prime source of heat found within Earth's core, which is to say that Earth itself is a planetary-sized nuclear reactor. That's why it's important to understand nuclear processes before making judgments about them.

Student

Wow, I never thought of Earth having a natural nuclear reactor inside it! This has all been really helpful, Alia. Nuclear fission isn't as intimidating as I thought once you understand the basic principles.
