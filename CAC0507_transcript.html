Welcome to Conceptual Academy! I'm Alia, and I'm here to support the learning of core science concepts. Let's get started. How can I help you today?

Student

Hi Alia. My professor mentioned we'll be covering nuclear fission in class tomorrow, and honestly, I'm a bit nervous. I've never been good at science, especially physics topics that sound complicated like nuclear stuff.

Alia

I completely understand that feeling! Nuclear fission might sound intimidating, but we'll approach it step by step. Similar to how biology students learn about cell division, nuclear fission is also about splitting. Did you know that the word "fission" is used in biology too?

Student

Really? I actually did take a biology class last semester. We talked about cell division, I think.

Alia

Exactly! Just as biology students know that living tissue grows by the division of cells, and the splitting in half of living cells is called fission, the splitting of an atomic nucleus into two smaller halves is called nuclear fission.

Student

So nuclear fission is basically just atoms splitting in half? That doesn't sound as complicated as I thought.

Alia

That's the basic idea! But there's a bit more to understand about why and how they split. Nuclear fission involves a delicate balance between two forces within the nucleus. One force is the strong nuclear force, which holds all the nucleons together. The second force is the repulsive electric force occurring among all the like-sign protons.

Student

Wait, I'm a bit confused. What are nucleons again? And why would protons repel each other?

Alia

Great questions! Nucleons are the particles that make up the nucleus - so both protons and neutrons. And remember, protons all have a positive charge. What happens when you try to bring two positive charges together?

Student

Oh right, they push away from each other because like charges repel! I remember playing with magnets as a kid and seeing how they would push away from each other when turned a certain way.

Alia

Perfect analogy! In most nuclei, the strong nuclear force dominates over this repulsive electric force. However, in uranium, this domination is weak. If the uranium nucleus is stretched into an elongated shape, the electric forces may push it into an even more elongated shape. If the elongation passes a critical point, the electric forces overwhelm the strong nuclear forces, and the nucleus splits. This splitting process is what we call nuclear fission.

Student

I'm trying to picture this... so uranium atoms are kind of unstable and can be pulled apart more easily than other atoms?

Alia

That's a good way to think about it. Let me describe a figure from our textbook that illustrates this. Imagine a uranium nucleus that begins to elongate. As it stretches, the repulsive electric force starts to overcome the attractive strong nuclear force, and when it passes a critical point, fission occurs - the nucleus splits apart.

Student

What causes the uranium to start elongating in the first place?

Alia

The absorption of a neutron by a uranium nucleus supplies enough energy to cause such an elongation. The resulting fission process may produce many different combinations of smaller nuclei. More significantly, the energy released by fission is enormous—about 7 million times that of a TNT molecule explosion!

Student

Whoa! 7 million times more energy than TNT? That's hard to even imagine. Where does all that energy go?

Alia

This energy is mainly in the form of kinetic energy of the fission fragments, which fly apart from one another. A much smaller amount of energy is released as gamma radiation.


Here's a typical uranium fission reaction equation:
₉₂²³⁵U + ₀¹n → ₅₆¹⁴²Ba + ₃₆⁹¹Kr + 3₀¹n + energy

Student

That equation looks complicated. What does it actually show?

Alia

Let me break it down. On the left side, we have uranium-235 absorbing a neutron. On the right side, we have the products: barium-142 and krypton-91 (these are the smaller nuclei formed), plus 3 neutrons, plus a lot of energy. Notice something interesting about the neutrons?

Student

Well, we started with 1 neutron on the left, but we have 3 neutrons on the right. So... more neutrons are created?

Alia

Exactly! And this is crucial. In this reaction, 1 neutron starts the fission of a single uranium nucleus, which produces nuclear fragments and 3 neutrons. These 3 neutrons can cause the fission of 3 more uranium atoms, releasing 9 more neutrons. If each of these 9 neutrons succeeds in splitting a uranium atom, the next step in the reaction produces 27 neutrons, and so on. Such a sequence is called a chain reaction.

Student

So it's like a domino effect? One split causes more splits, which cause even more splits?

Alia

That's an excellent analogy! A chain reaction is a self-sustaining reaction in which the products of one reaction event initiate further reaction events. It's exactly like your domino example - the reaction keeps propagating itself.

Student

This sounds dangerous. If one split causes three more, and those cause nine more... that sounds like it would just keep growing exponentially and never stop! Why don't uranium deposits in nature just spontaneously explode?

Alia

That's a fantastic question! Chain reactions do not happen in naturally occurring uranium ore deposits for a few key reasons. Fission occurs mainly in the rare isotope U-235, which makes up only 0.7 percent of the uranium in pure uranium metal. That's just 1 part in 140 of naturally occurring uranium!

Student

Oh, I see. So most uranium isn't the type that easily undergoes fission?

Alia

Correct! When the more abundant isotope U-238 absorbs neutrons created by fission of U-235, the U-238 typically does not undergo fission. So, any chain reaction is snuffed out by the neutron-absorbing U-238, as well as by the rock in which the ore is embedded.

Student

That makes sense. Nature has a way of preventing these reactions from getting out of control.

Alia

Indeed. There's another important factor too. Chain reactions are more effective in large chunks of uranium than in smaller chunks. In smaller chunks, neutrons easily find the surface and escape. As the neutrons escape, the chain reaction no longer builds up.

Student

So size matters? Like, a bigger piece of uranium is more likely to have a chain reaction than a smaller piece?

Alia

Exactly right. In a small piece of uranium, the surface area is large relative to its mass. Many neutrons will reach the surface and escape before they can cause another fission event. But in a larger piece, more uranium and less surface are presented to the neutrons.

Student

So what happens if you suddenly pushed two small pieces together to make a bigger piece?

Alia

That's precisely the principle behind nuclear weapons! If two small chunks of uranium are suddenly pushed together, they make a larger chunk. Within this larger chunk, neutrons are no longer able to escape as easily. Instead, they continue the chain reaction, which becomes sustainable.

Student

Is there a specific size where this starts to happen?

Alia

Yes, there is. The minimum size of a chunk needed for a sustainable chain reaction is called the critical mass. Any chunk at or above the critical mass produces a steady release of energy. With the correct engineering, this release of energy can be controlled, which is what happens within a nuclear power plant. A different sort of engineering produces an explosion of energy, which is the basis of a nuclear fission bomb.

Student

How does a nuclear bomb actually work then? Is it just smashing two pieces of uranium together?

Alia

In simplified terms, yes. A uranium-fission bomb has two smaller masses of uranium initially apart from each other. The bomb is ignited when high explosives (like gunpowder) force the two masses to come together into a single lump with a critical mass.

Student

Wouldn't it be easy for someone to make a bomb then? That sounds scary.

Alia

Constructing a fission bomb is actually a formidable task. The difficulty is in separating enough uranium-235 from the more abundant uranium-238. Separation is difficult because, except for their slightly different masses, the two isotopes have the same physical and chemical properties. Scientists took more than 2 years to extract enough of the 235 isotope from uranium ore to make the bomb that was detonated at Hiroshima in 1945. To this day, uranium isotope separation, also known as uranium enrichment, remains a difficult process.

Student

That's somewhat reassuring. So the same technology is used in nuclear power plants, but it's controlled?

Alia

Yes, let's talk about nuclear fission reactors. The awesome energy of nuclear fission was introduced to the world in the form of nuclear bombs, and this violent image still colors our thinking about nuclear power, making it difficult for many people to recognize its potential usefulness. Currently, about 20 percent of electric energy in the United States is generated by nuclear fission reactors, whereas most electric power is nuclear in some other countries—about 75 percent in France.

Student

I had no idea France used so much nuclear power! So nuclear reactors are like... nuclear bombs that don't explode?

Alia

Not exactly. These reactors are simply nuclear furnaces. They, like fossil fuel furnaces, do nothing more elegant than boil water to produce steam for a turbine. The greatest practical difference is the amount of fuel involved: a mere 1 kilogram of uranium fuel, less than the size of a baseball, yields more energy than 30 freight-car loads of coal.

Student

Wow, that's incredibly efficient! So, how does a nuclear power plant actually work?

Alia

A fission reactor contains four components: nuclear fuel, control rods, moderator (to slow the velocity of the neutrons, making them effective for the fission process), and a liquid (usually water) to transfer heat from the reactor to the turbine and generator.


The nuclear fuel is primarily U-238, plus about 3 percent U-235. Because the U-235 isotopes are so highly diluted with U-238, an explosion like that of a nuclear bomb is not possible. The reaction rate, which depends on the number of neutrons that initiate the fission of other U-235 nuclei, is controlled by rods inserted into the reactor. The control rods are made of a neutron-absorbing material, usually metals like cadmium or boron.

Student

So the control rods absorb extra neutrons to prevent the reaction from going too fast?

Alia

Exactly! Heated water around the nuclear fuel is kept under high pressure to keep it at a high temperature without boiling. It transfers heat to a second, lower-pressure water system, which operates the turbine and electric generator in a conventional fashion. In this design, two separate water systems are used so that no radioactivity reaches the turbine or the outside environment.

Student

That seems like a good safety feature. But I've heard about nuclear meltdowns. What happens there?

Alia

A nuclear power plant "meltdown" occurs when the fissioning nuclear fuels are no longer submerged within a cooling fluid, such as water. The temperature rises to the point that the solid nuclear fuel and the reaction vessel itself melt into a liquid phase that has the potential of penetrating through the floor of the containment building.

Student

That sounds dangerous. Are there safety measures to prevent that?

Alia

Yes, the entire setup resides inside a building with a dome-shaped containment building that is designed to prevent the release of radioactive isotopes in the event of an accident. The Soviet-built Chernobyl nuclear power plant that reached meltdown in 1986 had no such containment building, leading to the release of massive amounts of radiation into the surrounding environment.

Student

What about nuclear waste? I hear that's a big problem with nuclear power.

Alia

That's a significant disadvantage of fission power - the generation of radioactive waste products. Light atomic nuclei are most stable when composed of equal numbers of protons and neutrons, and heavy nuclei need more neutrons than protons for stability. For example, there are 143 neutrons but only 92 protons in U-235.


When uranium fissions into two medium-weight elements, the extra neutrons in their nuclei make them unstable. They are radioactive, most with very short half-lives, but some with half-lives of thousands of years. Safely disposing of these waste products, as well as materials made radioactive in the production of nuclear fuels, requires special storage casks and procedures. Although fission has been successfully producing electricity for a half century, disposing of radioactive wastes in the United States remains problematic.

Student

So there are both benefits and drawbacks to nuclear power. It seems like a complex issue.

Alia

Absolutely. The benefits of fission power are plentiful electricity, conservation of the many billions of tons of fossil fuels that every year are literally turned to heat and smoke (and in the long run may be far more precious as sources of organic molecules than as sources of heat), and the elimination of the megatons of sulfur oxides and other poisons that are put into the air each year by the burning of these fossil fuels.

Student

I'm trying to put all this together. Let me see if I understand a key point - a ball of uranium might have critical mass, but if you break it into small pieces, it doesn't. Why is that exactly?

Alia

Excellent question for checking your understanding! A 52-kilogram ball of uranium-235 has critical mass, but the same ball broken up into small chunks does not because the small chunks have more combined surface area than the ball from which they came. Neutrons escape via the surface of each small chunk before a sustained chain reaction can build up.

Student

That makes sense now! I understand nuclear fission much better. It's actually fascinating, not just scary. I didn't realize that the Earth itself has nuclear reactions happening naturally.

Alia

Yes! It's fascinating to know that sustainable fission chain reactions are a prime source of heat found within Earth's core, which is to say that Earth itself is a planetary-sized nuclear reactor. As the saying goes: Know nukes before you say "No nukes"!

Student

Alia

I'm glad to hear that! You've done a wonderful job working through these concepts. Nuclear fission is indeed a complex topic, but as you've discovered, breaking it down into smaller pieces makes it much more manageable. Good luck with your class tomorrow, and feel free to come back if you have more questions!
