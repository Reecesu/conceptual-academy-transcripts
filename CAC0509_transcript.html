Student

Hi Alia. I need to learn about nuclear fusion for my science course. I've always struggled with physics concepts, so I'm a bit nervous about this topic.

Alia

I understand your concern. Many students find nuclear physics challenging at first, but I'm here to guide you through it step by step. Let's explore nuclear fusion together. Nuclear fusion is actually the opposite of nuclear fission. It's the process where light nuclei combine to form heavier nuclei, releasing energy in the process.

Student

Okay, so fission breaks things apart, and fusion puts them together? I'm already confused about why both would release energy. That seems contradictory.

Alia

That's an excellent question, and it's a common point of confusion. If we look at the graphs of binding energy per nucleon, we see that the steepest part of what we call the "energy valley" goes from hydrogen to iron. Energy is gained by the nucleons as light nuclei combine.


When two small nuclei fuse—for example, a pair of hydrogen isotopes—the mass of the resulting helium nucleus is less than the mass of the two small nuclei before fusion. This mass difference is released in the form of energy.

Student

Wait, so some mass actually disappears during fusion? Where does it go?

Alia

The mass doesn't disappear—it's converted to energy according to Einstein's famous equation E=mc². As we move along the list of elements from hydrogen to iron, the average mass per nucleon actually decreases. This means the nucleons in heavier elements (up to iron) are more tightly bound and have less mass individually than when they were in lighter elements.

Student

So the nucleons in a helium atom weigh less than the same number of nucleons in separate hydrogen atoms?

Alia

Exactly! The mass of each nucleon in a hydrogen-2 nucleus is greater than the mass of each nucleon in a helium-4 nucleus, which results from the fusion of two hydrogen-2 nuclei. This lost mass is converted to energy, which is why nuclear fusion is a process that releases energy.

Student

That's interesting. So is this why the sun gives off energy? I've heard something about the sun "burning" hydrogen.

Alia

Yes, that's precisely right! For a fusion reaction to occur, the nuclei must collide at a very high speed in order to overcome their mutual electric repulsion. The required speeds correspond to the extremely high temperatures found in the Sun and other stars. Fusion brought about by high temperatures is called thermonuclear fusion.


In the high temperatures of the Sun, approximately 657 million tons of hydrogen is converted into 653 million tons of helium each second. The missing 4 million tons of mass is converted to energy—a tiny bit of which reaches our planet as sunshine. So thermonuclear fusion is the energy source of our sun, which is, in turn, the ultimate energy source of life on Earth.

Student

Four million tons every second? That sounds like the sun would use up all its fuel pretty quickly!

Alia

It seems that way, but the sun is so massive that even losing 4 million tons per second will allow it to continue fusing hydrogen for billions of years. Such reactions are, quite literally, nuclear burning. Thermonuclear fusion is analogous to ordinary chemical combustion.


In both chemical and nuclear burning, a high temperature starts the reaction; the release of energy by the reaction maintains a high enough temperature to spread the fire. The net result of the chemical reaction is the combination of atoms into more tightly bound molecules. In nuclear fusion reactions, the net result is more tightly bound nuclei. In both cases, mass decreases as the corresponding amount of energy is released.

Student

So, the atoms in my body are somehow connected to this process in the sun? That's kind of mind-blowing.

Alia

Yes, there's a profound connection there! The energy from the sun powers photosynthesis, which creates the food we eat. And many of the atoms in your body were actually forged in stars through fusion processes. We sometimes say that we are literally "star stuff."

Student

I still don't understand something. If fusion releases energy, and fission also releases energy, could we get energy from both processes using the same element?

Alia

That's a great question. No, this isn't contradictory only if the same element is said to release energy by both fission and fusion. Only the fusion of light elements and the fission of heavy elements result in a decrease in nucleon mass and a release of energy.


Think of it like this: there's an "energy valley" with iron at the bottom. Elements lighter than iron (like hydrogen) release energy when fused because they move toward the valley bottom. Elements heavier than iron (like uranium) release energy when split because they also move toward the valley bottom.

Student

Oh, that makes sense! So what about iron itself? Could we get energy from iron through either fusion or fission?

Alia

Neither, because iron is at the very bottom of the "energy valley." Fusing a pair of iron nuclei produces an element to the right of iron on the curve, in which the mass per nucleon is higher. If you split an iron nucleus, the products will lie to the left of iron on the curve and again have a higher mass per nucleon. So, no energy is released. For energy release, "decrease mass" is the name of the game—any game, chemical or nuclear.

Student

I see. So we can't get energy from iron. But I've heard about hydrogen bombs. Are those using fusion? How do they work if fusion needs such high temperatures?

Alia

Yes, hydrogen bombs use fusion. Prior to the development of the atomic bomb, the temperatures required to initiate nuclear fusion on Earth were unattainable. But researchers found that the temperature inside an exploding atomic bomb is four to five times the temperature at the center of the Sun, making thermonuclear bombs possible.


The first thermonuclear bomb, a hydrogen bomb, was detonated in 1952. Whereas the critical mass of fissionable material limits the size of a fission bomb (atomic bomb), no such limit is imposed on a fusion bomb (thermonuclear, or hydrogen, bomb). Just as there is no limit to the size of an oil-storage depot, there is no theoretical limit to the size of a fusion bomb.

Student

That sounds scary. So a hydrogen bomb uses a regular atomic bomb just to get hot enough to start fusion?

Alia

That's correct. Like the oil in a storage depot, any amount of fusion fuel can be stored with safety until ignited. Although a mere match can ignite an oil depot, nothing less energetic than an atomic bomb can ignite a thermonuclear bomb. This is why there is no such thing as a "baby" hydrogen bomb. A typical thermonuclear bomb stockpiled by the United States today, for example, is about 1000 times more destructive than the atomic bomb detonated over Hiroshima at the end of World War II.


The hydrogen bomb is another example of a discovery used for destructive rather than constructive purposes. The potential constructive possibility is the controlled release of vast amounts of clean energy.

Student

Is anyone trying to harness fusion for constructive purposes? It sounds like it could be a great energy source if we could control it.

Alia

Yes, many scientists are working on controlling fusion for energy production. Carrying out fusion reactions under controlled conditions requires temperatures of millions of degrees. There are a variety of techniques for attaining high temperatures. The problem is that all materials melt and vaporize at the temperatures required for fusion.


One solution to this problem is to confine the reaction in a nonmaterial container—a magnetic field, which can exist at any temperature and can exert powerful forces on charged particles in motion. "Magnetic walls" of sufficient strength provide a kind of magnetic straitjacket for hot ionized gases called plasmas. Magnetic compression further heats the plasma to fusion temperature.

Student

So we need to create a container that isn't made of physical material because it would just melt? That sounds really challenging.

Alia

Exactly. Although there are no nuclear fusion power plants currently in operation, an international project now exists whose goal is to prove the feasibility of nuclear fusion power in the near future. This fusion power project is called the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER).


The reactor will house electrically charged hydrogen gas (plasma) heated to about 150 million degrees Celsius, which is about 10 times hotter than the center of the Sun. In addition to producing about 500 MW of power, the reactor could be the energy source for the creation of hydrogen gas (H₂), which could be used to power fuel cells.

Student

That's amazing! So fusion could be our future energy source? Would we ever run out of fuel for fusion?

Alia

If people are one day to dart about the universe in the same way we jet about the Earth today, their supply of fuel is assured. The fuel for fusion—hydrogen—is found in every part of the universe, not only in the stars but also in the space between them. About 90 percent of the atoms in the universe are estimated to be hydrogen.


For people of the future, the supply of raw materials is also assured, because all the elements known to exist result from the fusing of more and more hydrogen nuclei. Future humans might synthesize their own elements and produce energy in the process, just as the stars have always done.

Student

This is fascinating! I never thought I'd be interested in nuclear physics, but this fusion concept connects to so many things - stars, energy, even the atoms in my body. One last question: you mentioned stars make elements through fusion up to iron, but what about heavier elements like gold? Where do they come from?

Alia

That's a great question! Elements are created in stars as smaller nuclei fuse to form larger nuclei. This process is energy releasing only up to iron. The manufacture of elements heavier than iron cannot be sustained within a shining star.


The heavier elements, such as gold, come from the final stage of certain very large stars that involves a mighty collapse, called a supernova. The energy of a supernova can outshine an entire galaxy, and it is this energy that makes the heavier-than-iron elements. In fact, the gold atoms in your jewelry were created using the abundant energy of a supernova that exploded very long ago, likely in a galaxy very far away.
