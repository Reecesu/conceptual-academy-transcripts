Alia

That's a good start! A dipole occurs when electrons in a compound congregate more to one side than the other. This creates a partial negative charge on one side and a partial positive charge on the other side of the molecule.


An extreme example is sodium chloride, NaCl, where the electrons spend almost all their time with the chlorine. This makes chlorine a negatively charged chloride ion (Cl⁻) and sodium a positively charged sodium ion (Na⁺). These strong charges give sodium chloride a very high melting point of 801°C and boiling point of 1465°C because of the strong attractions between all those charged ions.

Student

That sounds familiar now. And water is a dipole too, right? Is that why water can dissolve so many things?

Alia

That's exactly right! Water has a milder form of dipole, where the oxygen side is slightly negative and the hydrogen side is slightly positive. We call molecules with dipoles "polar molecules." Water's polarity is why it has a relatively high boiling point of 100°C - the electrical attractions between polar water molecules are quite strong.


Let's look at different types of molecular attractions involving dipoles. Each is electrical in nature, involving the attraction between positive and negative charges.

Student

So these attractions are basically like tiny magnets, with positive and negative sides attracting each other?

Alia

That's a great analogy! Like magnets, opposite charges attract. Let's start with the first type of attraction: ion-dipole attractions. When polar molecules like water are near an ionic compound like sodium chloride, the opposite charges attract each other. A positive sodium ion attracts the negative side of a water molecule, and a negative chloride ion attracts the positive side of a water molecule.

Student

So when I add salt to water, the water molecules are actually pulling apart the salt crystal?

Alia

Precisely! Ion-dipole attractions are much weaker than ionic bonds. However, when a large number of water molecules surround a salt crystal, their collective ion-dipole attractions can disrupt the ionic bonds. This is exactly what happens to sodium chloride in water - attractions exerted by water molecules break the ionic bonds and pull the ions away from one another. The result is a solution of sodium chloride in water, which we call an aqueous solution.

Student

That makes way more sense than when my high school teacher tried to explain it! So that's why salt disappears when I add it to water - it's not really gone, it's just been broken apart by the water molecules?

Alia

You've got it! The salt isn't gone - it's dissolved. The sodium and chloride ions are dispersed throughout the water, separated from each other by water molecules. This is a physical change, so you could recover the salt by evaporating the water.


Now let's look at the next type of attraction: dipole-dipole attractions. This occurs between two polar molecules.

Student

So instead of an ion and a water molecule, this would be like... two water molecules attracting each other?

Alia

Exactly! An attraction between two polar molecules is called a dipole-dipole attraction. An unusually strong dipole-dipole attraction is the "hydrogen bond." This occurs between molecules that have a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to a small, highly electronegative atom, usually nitrogen, oxygen, or fluorine.


Remember, electronegativity describes how well an atom can pull bonding electrons toward itself. The greater the atom's electronegativity, the better it can attract bonding electrons and become negatively charged.

Student

Wait, so a hydrogen bond isn't actually a real bond? That's confusing.

Alia

That's a common point of confusion! A hydrogen bond is not a true chemical bond - it's a special case of a dipole-dipole attraction. Let me explain how hydrogen bonding works.


In a water molecule, the hydrogen side has a partial positive charge because the more electronegative oxygen atom pulls more strongly on the electrons of the covalent bond. This hydrogen is therefore electrically attracted to a pair of non-bonding electrons on the partially negatively charged atom of another water molecule. This mutual attraction between hydrogen and the negatively charged atom of another molecule is a hydrogen bond.


Even though hydrogen bonds are much weaker than covalent or ionic bonds, their effects can be very pronounced. Water owes many of its unique properties to hydrogen bonds. Hydrogen bonds are also extremely important in the chemistry of large molecules like DNA and proteins.

Student

Alia

That's a wonderful visualization! Yes, water molecules do "hold hands" through hydrogen bonds, and that's a major reason why water forms droplets - the hydrogen bonds create surface tension. These bonds also explain many of water's special properties, which we'll explore more in Chapter 8.


Let's move on to the next type of attraction: dipole-induced dipole attractions.

Student

That sounds complicated. Does that mean one molecule forces another one to become a dipole?

Alia

You're right on target! In many molecules, the electrons are distributed evenly, so there is no dipole. The oxygen molecule, O₂, is an example of a nonpolar molecule. However, when a nonpolar molecule comes close to a polar molecule like water, it can be induced to become a temporary dipole.


When the slightly negative side of the water molecule comes near an oxygen molecule, it pushes the electrons in the oxygen molecule away. This creates a temporarily uneven distribution of electrons, which we call an induced dipole. The resulting attraction between the permanent dipole (water) and the induced dipole (oxygen) is a dipole-induced dipole attraction.

Student

So water molecules can basically push the electrons in oxygen molecules around? Is this why fish can breathe oxygen from water?

Alia

That's an excellent connection! Yes, dipole-induced dipole attractions are strong enough to hold relatively small quantities of oxygen dissolved in water. This attraction between water and molecular oxygen is vital for fish and other forms of aquatic life that rely on dissolved oxygen.


These attractions are much weaker than dipole-dipole attractions, which is why not much oxygen can dissolve in water. Water that's fully aerated at room temperature contains only about 1 oxygen molecule for every 200,000 water molecules. That's why fish gills must be so efficient at extracting oxygen from water.

Student

That's fascinating! Are there any everyday examples of these dipole-induced dipole attractions that I might have experienced?

Alia

Absolutely! Dipole-induced dipole attractions are responsible for how plastic wrap sticks to glass. The plastic wraps are made of very long nonpolar molecules that are induced to have dipoles when placed in contact with glass, which is highly polar.

Student

Oh! That explains why plastic wrap sticks better to glass than to itself. Wait - but it does stick to itself too. How does that work?

Alia

Great observation! This brings us to our next type of attraction: induced dipole-induced dipole attractions.


Individual atoms and nonpolar molecules normally have a fairly even distribution of electrons. But because of the randomness of electron distribution, at any given moment, the electrons might be bunched to one side. This creates a temporary dipole.


Just as the permanent dipole of a polar molecule can induce a dipole in a nonpolar molecule, a temporary dipole can induce dipoles in neighboring molecules. This gives rise to induced dipole-induced dipole attractions, sometimes called dispersion forces.

Student

That sounds really weak. Do these forces actually do anything important?

Alia

They may be weak individually, but collectively they can be quite significant! These induced dipole-induced dipole attractions explain why natural gas is a gas at room temperature but gasoline is a liquid.


The major component of natural gas is methane, CH₄, and one of the major components of gasoline is octane, C₈H₁₈. The number of induced dipole-induced dipole attractions between two methane molecules is much less than the number between two octane molecules.

Alia

That's an excellent analogy! Like short pieces of Velcro, methane molecules can be pulled apart with little effort. That's why methane has a low boiling point of -161°C and is a gas at room temperature.


Octane molecules, like long strips of Velcro, are relatively difficult to pull apart because of the larger number of induced dipole-induced dipole attractions. The boiling point of octane is 125°C, so it's a liquid at room temperature. The greater mass of octane also plays a role in its higher boiling point.

The force of attraction between these hairs and the wall is the weak induced dipole-induced dipole attraction. But because there are so many hairs, the surface area of contact is relatively great, so the total force of attraction is enough to prevent the gecko from falling.

Let me explain one more important concept about these forces. Temporary dipoles are more significant for larger atoms. This is because their outermost electrons are relatively far from the nucleus and from each other, so these electrons are easily "pushed around."


Larger atoms are sometimes described as "soft" - more like a marshmallow than a marble. The technical term for this property is polarizability. A larger atom is more "polarizable," meaning it can form an induced dipole more easily.
