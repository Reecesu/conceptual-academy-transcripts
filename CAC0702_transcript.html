I guess I never really thought about it. The sugar just disappears, right?

That's a common perception! But let's think about it more deeply - is the sucrose actually destroyed when it dissolves? We know it isn't because it sweetens the water, and if we evaporate the water, the sugar reappears in its solid form.

Oh, that makes sense. So the sugar is still there, just not visible anymore?

Alia

Exactly! And here's another interesting question: when sucrose dissolves in water, does it disappear because it somehow ceases to occupy space or because it fits within the nooks and crannies of the water?

Student

I'd guess it fits in the spaces between water molecules?

Alia

That's a reasonable thought, but actually, the addition of sucrose changes the volume. This may not be noticeable at first, but if you continue adding sucrose to a glass of water, you'll see that the water level rises, just as it would if you were adding sand.

Student

Oh! So the sugar molecules are actually taking up space in the water. But why can't we see them anymore?

Alia

Great question! When sucrose is stirred into water, it loses its crystalline form. Each sucrose crystal consists of billions upon billions of sucrose molecules packed neatly together. When the crystal is exposed to water, an even greater number of water molecules pull on the sucrose molecules via hydrogen bonds formed between the sucrose molecules and the water molecules.

Student

So the water molecules are actually pulling apart the sugar crystal?

Alia

Yes! With a little stirring, the sucrose molecules soon mix throughout the water. The water molecules pull the sucrose molecules in a sucrose crystal away from one another. This pulling away does not, however, affect the covalent bonds within each sucrose molecule, which is why each dissolved sucrose molecule remains intact as a single molecule.

Student

That's interesting. So instead of having separate sugar crystals and water, we have sugar molecules mixed in with water molecules?

Alia

That's right! In place of sucrose crystals and water, we have a homogeneous mixture of sucrose molecules in water. "Homogeneous" means that a sample taken from any part of a mixture is the same as a sample taken from any other part.

Student

So that's why the first sip of sweetened coffee tastes the same as the last sip?

Alia

Exactly! In our sucrose example, the homogeneity means that the sweetness of the first sip of the solution is the same as the sweetness of the last sip. You've just described a key characteristic of solutions!

Student

Wait, so sugar water is a solution? I thought solutions were something special in chemistry.

Alia

Sugar water is indeed a solution in the liquid phase. Specifically, a homogeneous mixture consisting of a single phase is called a solution. But solutions aren't always liquids. They can also be solid or gaseous.

Student

Really? I can't imagine what a solid solution would look like.

Alia

Gemstones are actually good examples of solid solutions. A ruby, for example, is a solid solution of trace quantities of red chromium compounds in transparent aluminum oxide. And a blue sapphire is a solid solution of trace quantities of light green iron compounds and blue titanium compounds in aluminum oxide.

Student

Wow, I never would have thought of gemstones as solutions! Are there other examples of solid solutions?

Alia

Yes, another important example of solid solutions is metal alloys, which are mixtures of different metallic elements. The alloy known as brass is a solid solution of copper and zinc. Similarly, the alloy stainless steel is a solid solution of iron, chromium, nickel, and carbon.

Student

That's amazing. What about gaseous solutions? Can you give an example of that?

Alia

The air we breathe is actually a perfect example of a gaseous solution. By volume, this solution is 78 percent nitrogen gas, 21 percent oxygen gas, and 1 percent other gaseous materials. The air we exhale is a gaseous solution of 75 percent nitrogen, 14 percent oxygen, 5 percent carbon dioxide, and around 6 percent water vapor.

Student

I never thought of air as a solution before! This is changing how I look at things. So in a solution, how do we know which part is which?

Alia

In describing solutions, the component present in the largest amount is the solvent and any other components are solutes. For example, when a teaspoon of table sugar is mixed with 1 liter of water, we identify the water as the solvent and the sugar as the solute.

Student

I think I get it. So in coffee, the water would be the solvent and the coffee and sugar would be the solutes?

Alia

That's right! You're making great connections. The process of a solute's mixing with a solvent is called dissolving. To make a solution, a solute must dissolve in a solvent; that is, the solute and solvent must form a homogeneous mixture.

Student

What determines whether something will dissolve in something else? Like, why does sugar dissolve in water but oil doesn't?

Alia

Whether one material dissolves in another is a function of the electrical attractions between their molecules. The stronger these electrical attractions are, the greater the likelihood that dissolving will occur.

Student

OK, that makes sense. So can I keep adding more and more sugar to water forever?

Alia

There is actually a limit to how much of a given solute can be dissolved in a given solvent. We know that when you add sugar to a glass of water, for example, the sugar rapidly dissolves. As you continue to add sugar, however, there comes a point when it no longer dissolves. Instead, it collects at the bottom of the glass, even after stirring.

Student

Oh yeah, I've seen that happen before when making sweet tea. What's happening there?

Alia

At that point, the water is saturated with sugar, meaning that the water cannot accept any more sugar. When this happens, we have what is called a saturated solution, defined as a solution in which no more solute can be dissolved.

Student

So there's like a maximum amount of sugar that can dissolve in a certain amount of water?

Alia

Exactly! For example, a maximum of 200 grams of sucrose dissolves in 100 milliliters of water at 20°C. If you mix 150 grams of sucrose in 100 milliliters of water at 20°C, you'll produce an unsaturated solution - meaning more sugar could still dissolve. If you mix 200 grams of sucrose in 100 milliliters of water at 20°C, you'll produce a saturated solution. And if you try mixing 250 grams of sucrose with 100 milliliters of water at 20°C, 50 grams of sucrose will remain undissolved.

Student

What's an unsaturated solution again?

Alia

A solution that has not reached the limit of solute that will dissolve is called an unsaturated solution.

Student

I was making rock candy once, and my mom talked about making a "super-saturated" solution. Is that a real thing or did she make that up?

Alia

That's absolutely a real thing! There are some special cases where more solute can be dissolved beyond the point of saturation. Such a solution is said to be supersaturated.

Student

How does that work? If a solution is already at its maximum, how can you add more?

Alia

Interestingly, it's fairly easy to produce a supersaturated solution of sugar (sucrose) in water. For sugar, the point of saturation is highly dependent upon the temperature. Consider that while about 200 g of sucrose can dissolve in 100 mL of water at 20°C, at a higher temperature of about 100°C, it's possible to dissolve almost 500 g in 100 mL of water.

Student

Oh! So hot water can dissolve more sugar than cold water?

Alia

Exactly! The first step to making a supersaturated solution of sugar in water is to create a saturated solution at a relatively high temperature. Then you let the hot saturated solution slowly cool down. As it does, the extra sugar solute will remain in solution even after the solution has cooled—you'll have a supersaturated solution. Eventually, that extra sugar will come out of solution in the form of crystals, which is how rock candy (crystals of sugar) is made—add a bit of food coloring for further appeal.

Student

That's exactly what we did! We had to wait for days for the crystals to form. This is starting to make sense now. Are there other important types of solutions I should know about?

Alia

Yes, two other categories of solutions include electrolyte and nonelectrolyte solutions. An electrolyte is any dissolved ion, such as a sodium ion, Na+, or a chloride ion, Cl-, that allows for the flow of an electric current through the solution.

Student

Electrolytes? Like in sports drinks?

Alia

You've made an excellent connection! The greater the concentration of electrolytes, the stronger the ability of the solution to conduct an electric current. Think of this in reverse: If you find that a solution is able to conduct electricity, evidenced by the brightness of a light bulb, then you can conclude it has electrolytes. The concentration of those electrolytes, in turn, can be deduced based upon how brightly the light bulb shines.

Student

So we could test for electrolytes by seeing if a solution conducts electricity?

Alia

Yes! But it need not be a light bulb in the circuit. Rather it can be a meter of some sort, which can be calibrated to measure the concentration of the electrolyte solution.

Student

What kinds of solutions have electrolytes in them?

Alia

Electrolyte solutions tend to be those having water as the solvent. Why? Because water's polarity allows for the easy dissolving of various ions. An electrolyte solution is any solution containing electrolytes. Your blood is an example of an electrolyte solution containing electrolytes such as ions of sodium, calcium, and potassium.

Student

Is that why it's important to stay hydrated?

Alia

Yes, that's part of it! For your body to remain healthy, the concentration of these electrolytes needs to be just right—not too high and not too low. After a vigorous aerobic workout, you may be sweating. As you sweat you're losing not only water but the electrolytes dissolved in that water, which is why many sports drinks are fortified with electrolytes.

Student

That makes so much sense now! What about nonelectrolyte solutions? Is that just any solution that doesn't have electrolytes?

Alia

That's right! A nonelectrolyte solution is one where there are no electrolytes to conduct electricity. Pure water is mostly nonelectrolytic—it won't allow the passage of an electric current. However, obtaining absolutely pure water is rather difficult (to impossible) because so many things dissolve in water quite readily, including the air.

Student

So if I dissolved sugar in pure water, would that conduct electricity?

Alia

That's a great question! The answer is no. Why not? Because when sugar dissolves in water the sugar molecules remain sugar molecules—they don't dissociate into ions like a salt. A nonelectrolytic solution is any solution not containing electrolytes. Sugar dissolved in purified water is a good example.

Student

This is all starting to connect for me now. So salts in water make electrolyte solutions because they break into ions, but sugar in water makes a nonelectrolyte solution because the sugar molecules stay intact?

Alia

Yes, you've got it exactly right! More common in laboratory research are solutions where water is not the solvent, but some non-polar solvent, such as methylene chloride, CH2Cl2. Organic compounds dissolved in such a solvent are fully nonelectrolytic solutions.
