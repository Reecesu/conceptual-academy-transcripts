Student

Hi Alia. I need to understand solubility for my science class. I've always struggled with chemistry concepts, so I'm a bit nervous about this topic.

Alia

That's completely understandable! Many students find chemistry challenging at first, but I think you'll find solubility is a concept that connects well to everyday experiences. Let's start with the basics: solubility is simply a solute's ability to dissolve in a solvent. This primarily depends on the attractions between the fundamental particles of the solute and solvent. If a solute has any appreciable ability to dissolve in a solvent, we say it's soluble in that solvent.

Student

So solubility is just about whether something can dissolve in something else? That seems straightforward enough.

Alia

Yes, but there's more to it. Solubility also depends on the attractions between solute particles themselves and between solvent particles themselves. Let me give you an example with sucrose, or table sugar. Sucrose molecules have many polar hydrogen-oxygen bonds, which allow them to form multiple hydrogen bonds with each other. These hydrogen bonds are strong enough to make sucrose a solid at room temperature with a relatively high melting point of 185°C.

Student

Wait, I'm confused. If sugar molecules are strongly attracted to each other, wouldn't that make sugar less likely to dissolve in water?

Alia

That's an excellent observation! You're absolutely right. For sucrose to dissolve in water, the water molecules must first pull sucrose molecules away from one another. This does put a limit on how much sucrose can dissolve in water. Eventually, we reach a point where there aren't enough water molecules to separate the sucrose molecules from one another. This is what we call the point of saturation, which we discussed earlier - any additional sucrose added to the solution won't dissolve.

Student

Oh, I get it now. So that's why when I add too much sugar to my iced tea, it just sinks to the bottom!

Alia

Exactly! That's a perfect everyday example. Now, when the attractions among solute molecules are comparable to those among solvent molecules, there may not be a practical point of saturation. For example, the hydrogen bonds among water molecules are about as strong as those between ethanol molecules. These two liquids therefore mix together quite well in just about any proportion. We can even add so much ethanol to water that the ethanol, rather than water, becomes the solvent.

Student

So ethanol and water mix really well together. Is that why alcoholic beverages can be so concentrated?

Alia

Yes, that's part of it! When a solute has no practical point of saturation in a given solvent, we say it's infinitely soluble in that solvent. Ethanol is infinitely soluble in water. Similarly, all gases are generally infinitely soluble in other gases because they can be mixed together in just about any proportion.

Student

But not all things dissolve well in water, right? Like oil doesn't mix with water.

Alia

That's correct. Let's look at the other extreme of solubility, where a solute has very little solubility in a given solvent. Oxygen, O₂, is an example. In contrast to sucrose, which has a solubility of 200 grams per 100 milliliters of water, only 0.004 gram of oxygen can dissolve in 100 milliliters of water.


We can explain oxygen's low solubility in water by noting that the only attractions between oxygen molecules and water molecules are relatively weak dipole-induced dipole attractions. More importantly, the stronger attraction of water molecules for one another—through hydrogen bonds—effectively excludes oxygen molecules from intermingling with them.

Student

So the water molecules prefer to stick with each other rather than interact with the oxygen?

Alia

That's a great way to put it! A material that doesn't dissolve in a solvent to any appreciable extent is said to be insoluble in that solvent. Many substances are insoluble in water, including sand and glass. But just because a material is not soluble in one solvent doesn't mean it won't dissolve in another.


For example, sand and glass are soluble in hydrofluoric acid, HF, which is used to give glass a decorative frosted look. Also, although Styrofoam™ is insoluble in water, it's partially soluble in acetone, a solvent used in fingernail-polish remover. If you pour a little acetone into a Styrofoam™ cup, the acetone soon causes the Styrofoam™ to deform.

Student

That's interesting! I've actually seen that happen before when someone put nail polish remover in a Styrofoam cup - it started dissolving. I thought it was melting, but it was actually dissolving?

Alia

Exactly! That's an excellent observation. Now, let's talk about how solubility changes with temperature. You've probably noticed from experience that water-soluble solids usually dissolve better in hot water than in cold water.

Student

Yeah, like when making tea or hot chocolate, the sugar dissolves much faster in hot water.

Alia

That's a perfect example! A highly concentrated solution of sucrose in water can be made by heating the solution almost to the boiling point. This is how syrup and hard candy are made.


Solubility increases with increasing temperature because hot water molecules have greater kinetic energy and therefore are able to collide with the solid solute more vigorously. These vigorous collisions facilitate the disruption of particle-to-particle attractions in the solid.

Student

Does that mean all solids dissolve better in hot water?

Alia

Not necessarily. Although the solubilities of many solid solutes—like sucrose—are greatly increased by rises in temperature, the solubilities of other solid solutes, such as sodium chloride (table salt), are only mildly affected by temperature. This difference involves various factors, including the strength of the chemical bonds in the solute molecules and how those molecules are packed together.


Interestingly, some chemicals, such as calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), actually become less soluble as water temperature increases. This explains why the inner surfaces of tea kettles are often coated with calcium carbonate residues.

Student

That's fascinating! I've always wondered what that white stuff inside my kettle was. So what happens when a hot solution cools down?

Alia

When a sugar solution saturated at a high temperature is allowed to cool, some of the sugar usually comes out of solution and forms what is called a precipitate. When this occurs, the solute, sugar in this case, is said to have precipitated from the solution.


Let's consider a quantitative example: At 100°C, the solubility of sodium nitrate, NaNO₃, in water is 165 grams per 100 milliliters of water. As we cool this solution, the solubility of NaNO₃ decreases, and this change in solubility causes some of the dissolved NaNO₃ to precipitate. At 20°C, the solubility of NaNO₃ is only 87 grams per 100 milliliters of water. So, if we cool the 100°C solution to 20°C, 78 grams (165 grams – 87 grams) precipitates.

Student

That makes sense. Are there other factors besides temperature that affect how quickly something dissolves?

Alia

Great question! Yes, temperature can impact not only how much solute a solution can hold but also the rate at which the solute dissolves. At warmer temperatures, the solvent molecules have greater kinetic energy, allowing them to "attack" the solute more quickly. That's why sugar dissolves faster in hot tea than in cold iced tea.


The surface area of the solute also matters. A large crystal of sugar takes longer to dissolve in water than the same mass of sugar crushed up into a fine powder. When you drop a large sugar crystal into water, only the surface of the crystal is exposed to water molecules. But if you grind that crystal into a powder before adding it to water, there's greater exposure of the sugar molecules to the water—we say the surface area of the sugar has been increased. Thus, the greater the surface area of the solute, the quicker it will dissolve.

Student

That makes a lot of sense! Is that why recipes sometimes call for powdered sugar instead of granulated sugar?

Alia

Yes, exactly! There's one more factor to consider: what happens right at the surface of the solute where molecules are dissolving. Right along the surface, solute molecules are going into solution, making that area fairly concentrated. This means 1) the solution might be saturated at that location, and 2) the number of water molecules is getting less. Both factors can be overcome by stirring the solution, providing agitation to ensure movement of both the solvent and solute.

Student

I've noticed gases seem to behave differently in solution. Like soda goes flat faster when it's warm. Why is that?

Alia

You've made an excellent observation! In contrast to the solubilities of most solids, the solubilities of gases in liquids decrease with increasing temperature. This occurs because with an increase in temperature, the solvent molecules have more kinetic energy, making it more difficult for a gaseous solute to remain in solution. The solute molecules are literally ejected by the high-energy solvent molecules.


As you've noticed, compared to cold carbonated beverages, warm ones go flat faster because the higher temperature causes the carbon dioxide gas molecules to leave the liquid solvent at a higher rate.

Student

Does pressure also affect how gases dissolve?

Alia

Absolutely! The solubility of a gas in a liquid also depends on the pressure of the gas immediately above the liquid. In general, a higher gas pressure above the liquid means more of the gas dissolves.


A gas at high pressure has many gas particles crammed into a given volume. The "empty" space in an unopened soft drink bottle, for example, is crammed with carbon dioxide molecules in the gaseous phase. With nowhere else to go, many of these molecules dissolve in the liquid. Alternatively, we might say that the great pressure forces the carbon dioxide molecules into solution.


When the bottle is opened, the "head" of highly pressurized carbon dioxide gas escapes. Now the gas pressure above the liquid is lower than before. As a result, the solubility of the carbon dioxide drops, and the carbon dioxide molecules that were once squeezed into the solution begin to escape into the air above the liquid.

Student

Is that why shaking a soda before opening it makes it explode? I learned that the hard way!

Alia

Yes! The rate at which carbon dioxide molecules leave an opened soft drink is relatively slow. You can increase this rate by adding granulated sugar, salt, or sand. The microscopic nooks and crannies on the surfaces of these grains serve as nucleation sites where carbon dioxide bubbles can form rapidly and then escape by buoyant forces.


Shaking the beverage increases the surface area of the liquid-to-gas interface, making it easier for the carbon dioxide to escape from the solution. Once shaken, the rate at which carbon dioxide escapes becomes so great that the beverage froths over.


You also increase the rate of carbon dioxide escape when you pour the beverage into your mouth, which has many nucleation sites. That's why you feel that tingly sensation!

Student

That explains why adding salt to soda makes it fizz more! Does adding salt to water affect other gases too?

Alia

Great insight! Yes, when salt is added to water, it affects the solubility of all gases. Consider what happens when sodium and chloride ions are introduced: water molecules are drawn to these highly charged ions, surrounding them and forming a "sphere of hydration." This means fewer water molecules are available to interact with nonpolar gas molecules like oxygen (O₂) or carbon dioxide (CO₂).


Simply put, these gas molecules cannot compete with the salt for water's attention. As salt is introduced to water, the solubility of any gas dissolved in that water decreases. This is called a salting effect.


This explains why adding salt to a carbonated beverage is particularly effective at forming bubbles—you get both the nucleation sites we discussed earlier and this salting effect. It also explains why oxygen is more soluble in freshwater streams and lakes than in seawater. A freshwater trout won't survive in seawater partly because its tissues aren't accustomed to the high salinity, but also because there's less oxygen available for it to absorb through its gills.

Student

Wow, I never made that connection about fish! Can we talk about chemical reactions in solutions? I've heard of precipitation reactions but don't really understand them.

Alia

Certainly! During a chemical reaction, atoms change partners to form new compounds. For example, consider the reaction between lead nitrate, Pb(NO₃)₂, and potassium iodide, KI, to form lead iodide, PbI₂, and potassium nitrate, KNO₃:


Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2 KI → PbI₂ + 2 KNO₃


What happens is that the iodine (I) jumps from the potassium (K) to the lead (Pb) to form lead iodide (PbI₂). Both reactants—lead nitrate and potassium iodide—are soluble in water, which is why this reaction can be performed in an aqueous solution (water as the solvent). We can indicate this by placing (aq) after each water-soluble compound:


Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2 KI(aq) → PbI₂(s) + 2 KNO₃(aq)


While lead nitrate, potassium iodide, and the potassium nitrate product are all soluble in cold water, the lead iodide product is not. The result is that the lead iodide comes out of solution as it's formed—it precipitates. As a yellow solid, this particular "precipitation reaction" can be quite beautiful, especially if the solution is heated to dissolve the lead iodide, which then recrystallizes as the solution slowly cools.

Student

So a precipitation reaction occurs when one of the products is insoluble and forms a solid that can be separated out?

Alia

That's exactly right! There are many types of precipitation reactions, but what they all have in common is that one of the chemical compound products is not soluble in the solvent. As a result, this product precipitates out of solution, making it relatively easy to collect by passing the solution through filter paper.

Student

Are there any general rules for predicting which compounds will be soluble or insoluble?

Alia

Yes, chemists have found some general trends regarding the solubility of compounds in water. These are known as solubility rules. They're not meant to be memorized but are provided as a reference. Some of them are:


1. Salts containing Group I ions (Li⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, Cs⁺, Rb⁺) are soluble, with few exceptions. Salts containing the ammonium ion (NH₄⁺) are also soluble.


2. Salts containing nitrate ion (NO₃⁻) are generally soluble.


3. Salts containing Group 17 ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, or I⁻) are generally soluble. Important exceptions are halide salts of Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, and Hg₂²⁺. Thus, AgCl, PbBr₂, and Hg₂Cl₂ are insoluble.


4. Most silver salts are insoluble. AgNO₃ and Ag(C₂H₃O₂) are common soluble silver salts; virtually all others are insoluble.


5. Most sulfate salts are soluble. Important exceptions include CaSO₄, BaSO₄, PbSO₄, Ag₂SO₄, and SrSO₄.


6. Most hydroxide salts are only slightly soluble. Hydroxide salts of Group I ions are soluble. Hydroxide salts of Group II ions (Ca, Sr, and Ba) are slightly soluble. Hydroxide salts of transition metals and Al³⁺ are insoluble. Thus, Fe(OH)₃, Al(OH)₃, Co(OH)₂ are not soluble.


7. Most sulfides of transition metals are highly insoluble, including CdS, FeS, ZnS, and Ag₂S. Arsenic, antimony, bismuth, and lead sulfides are also insoluble.


8. Carbonates are frequently insoluble. Group II carbonates (CaCO₃, SrCO₃, and BaCO₃) are insoluble, as are FeCO₃ and PbCO₃.


9. Chromates are frequently insoluble. Examples include PbCrO₄ and BaCrO₄.


10. Phosphates such as Ca₃(PO₄)₂ and Ag₃PO₄ are frequently insoluble.


11. Fluorides such as BaF₂, MgF₂, and PbF₂ are frequently insoluble.

Student

That's a lot of rules! Do I need to memorize all of those?

Alia

Not at all! These rules are empirical, meaning they're based simply on investigation and observation rather than detailed theory. Working chemists use these rules to help predict whether chemicals they produce might not be soluble in a solvent.


They don't typically memorize these rules—that would be an exercise in memorization, not chemistry. Like the periodic table, solubility rules can be easily looked up. Also like the periodic table, the more you work with the solubility rules, the more familiar they become. Working chemists know these rules not because they memorized them, but because they work with them regularly.


Consider these two reactions we discussed:


Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2 KI(aq) → PbI₂(s) + 2 KNO₃(aq)


AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)


These are known as double replacement reactions, which means the two reactants exchange atoms with each other. Looking at the solubility rules, Rule 3 states that salts of lead and silver tend to be insoluble. That's why we see (s) for "solid" appearing after PbI₂ and AgCl.
