Nonpolar? I think we covered that in class, but I'm not sure I understand what that means exactly.

Nonpolar molecules have their electrons distributed fairly evenly, so they don't have distinct positive and negative regions like polar molecules do. Water is polar, while grease is nonpolar. As you've noticed, they don't mix well - like how oil floats on water.

Oh, I see. So if water can't remove greasy grime because it's nonpolar, what can?

To remove most grime, we can use a nonpolar solvent such as paint thinner, which dissolves the grime because of strong induced dipole–induced dipole attractions. Paint thinner works well for removing grime left on hands after activities like changing a car's motor oil.

Paint thinner? That seems pretty extreme for washing hands!

You're absolutely right! Nonpolar solvents like paint thinner are toxic, have offensive odors, and are harsh on the skin. Rather than washing our dirty hands and clothes with nonpolar solvents, we have a more pleasant alternative—soap and water.

Student

So how does soap work if water alone can't remove grease? That seems contradictory.

Alia

That's a great question! Soap works because soap molecules have both nonpolar and polar properties. A typical soap molecule has two parts: a long, nonpolar tail of carbon and hydrogen atoms and a polar head containing at least one ionic bond.

Student

So it's like a molecule with two different personalities? One part likes water and one part likes grease?

Alia

That's an excellent way to think about it! Because most of a soap molecule is nonpolar, it attracts nonpolar grime molecules via induced dipole–induced dipole attractions, also called dispersion forces. In fact, grime quickly finds itself surrounded in three dimensions by the nonpolar tails of soap molecules.

Student

Oh, I think I get it. The nonpolar tails grab onto the grease. But then how does the water help wash it away if water and grease don't mix?

Alia

This is where the dual nature of soap becomes crucial. This attraction between the soap's nonpolar tails and the grime is usually sufficient to lift the grime away from the surface being cleaned. With the nonpolar tails facing inward toward the grime, the polar heads are all directed outward, where they are attracted to water molecules by relatively strong ion–dipole attractions.

Student

So the soap forms like a bridge between the water and grease?

Alia

That's a great analogy! If the water is flowing, the whole conglomeration of grime and soap molecules flows with it, away from your hands or clothes and then down the drain. This structure, with grime surrounded by soap molecules, is called a micelle.

Student

Oh, I think I've seen diagrams of that in our textbook! It's like a little ball with all the greasy parts inside and the water-loving parts outside, right?

Alia

Exactly! You're visualizing a micelle perfectly. The nonpolar grime attracts and is surrounded by the nonpolar tails of soap molecules, forming this micelle structure. The polar heads of the soap molecules are attracted by ion–dipole attractions to water molecules, which then carry away the soap–grime combination.

Student

So to summarize, soap molecules have both polar and nonpolar properties, which lets them connect to both the grime and the water at the same time?

Alia

That's correct! That's the key property of soap molecules that makes them so effective.

Student

I'm curious - how is soap actually made? Has it always been around?

Alia

For the past several centuries, soaps have been prepared by treating animal fats with sodium hydroxide, NaOH, also known as caustic lye. In this reaction, which is still used today, each fat molecule is broken down into three fatty acid soap molecules and one glycerol molecule.

Student

So people have been making soap for centuries? That's pretty impressive chemistry for way back then!

Alia

Yes, it is impressive! Traditional soapmaking is an ancient craft. But chemistry has continued to evolve. In the 1940s, chemists began developing a class of synthetic soap-like compounds known as detergents, which offer several advantages over true soaps, such as stronger grease penetration and lower price.

Student

Wait, so soap and detergent aren't the same thing? I've always used those words interchangeably.

Alia

That's a common misconception! While they serve similar purposes, they're chemically distinct. The chemical structure of detergent molecules is similar to that of soap molecules in that both possess a polar head attached to a nonpolar tail. However, the polar head in a detergent molecule typically consists of either a sulfate group, –OSO₃⁻, or a sulfonate group, –SO₃⁻, and the nonpolar tail can have an assortment of structures.

Student

That sounds complicated. Are there detergents I use everyday without realizing they're not actually soap?

Absolutely! One of the most common sulfate detergents is sodium lauryl sulfate, a main ingredient of many toothpastes. A common sulfonate detergent is sodium dodecyl benzenesulfonate, also known as a linear alkylsulfonate, or LAS, often found in dishwashing liquids.

Oh wow, I had no idea toothpaste contained detergents! I guess that makes sense since it needs to clean our teeth. Are these detergents bad for the environment?

That's an important environmental consideration. Fortunately, both of these detergents I mentioned are biodegradable, which means that microorganisms can break down the molecules once they are released into the environment.
