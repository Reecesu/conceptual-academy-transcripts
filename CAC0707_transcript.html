Student

Hi Alia. I need to learn about water purification for my science class. I've always been kind of nervous about science courses, but I'm trying to do better this semester.

Alia

I'm glad you're taking this step! Water purification is a fascinating topic that connects to our everyday lives. As we discussed in an earlier section, it's impossible to obtain 100 percent pure water. However, we are able to purify water to meet our needs by taking advantage of the differences in physical properties between water and the solutes or particulates it contains.

Student

So we can't get completely pure water? Why is that?

Alia

That's correct. Water is an excellent solvent, which means it naturally dissolves many substances. Even the purest water we can produce will still contain some dissolved substances. However, we can purify water to make it potable—safe for drinking. In the United States, potable water is currently used for everything from cooking to flushing our toilets.

Student

I've never really thought about what happens to make my tap water safe to drink. How does that work?

Alia

Great question! The first step most public utilities take to produce potable water from natural sources is to remove dirt particles and pathogens, such as bacteria. This is done by mixing the water with certain minerals, like slaked lime and aluminum sulfate, which coagulate into a gelatinous material that becomes interspersed throughout the water.

Student

Wait, they add chemicals to clean the water? That sounds counterintuitive.

Alia

I understand why that might seem strange! What happens is that slaked lime, which is Ca(OH)₂, and aluminum sulfate, Al₂(SO₄)₃, react to form aluminum hydroxide, Al(OH)₃, and calcium sulfate, CaSO₄. Together, these form a gelatinous material. This process takes place in a large settling basin. Slow stirring causes this gelatinous material to clump together and settle to the bottom of the basin. As these clumps form and settle, they carry with them many of the dirt particles and bacteria.

Student

Oh, so they're like magnets for the dirt? What happens next?

Alia

That's a good way to think about it! After this coagulation and settling process, the water is then filtered through sand and gravel. This further removes impurities.


To improve the odor and flavor of the water, many treatment facilities also aerate the water by cascading it through a column of air. Aeration removes many unpleasant-smelling volatile chemicals, such as sulfur compounds.

Student

Is that why sometimes tap water smells like chemicals or chlorine?

Alia

You've made a good observation! The chlorine smell is actually from the final step in water treatment. After aeration (which actually improves taste by dissolving air into the water—without dissolved air, water tastes flat), the water is treated with a disinfectant, usually chlorine gas, Cl₂, but sometimes ozone, O₃. Then it's stored in a holding tank that feeds into the city mains. The chlorine smell you notice is from this disinfection process, which is crucial for killing any remaining harmful microorganisms.

Student

I see. So developed countries like the US have pretty good water treatment systems. What about other parts of the world?

Alia

That's a thoughtful question. Developed countries do have the technology and infrastructure to produce vast quantities of water suitable for drinking—with the result that many citizens take their drinking water for granted. The number of public water-treatment facilities in developing nations, however, is relatively small.


In these locations, many people drink their water in the form of a hot beverage, such as tea, which is disinfected through boiling. Alternatively, disinfecting iodine tablets can be used.

Student

But boiling water takes fuel, and those tablets might be expensive, right?

Alia

Exactly. Fuel for boiling and tablets for disinfecting aren't always available. As a result, more than 400 people in the world (mostly children) die every hour from preventable diseases or infections such as cholera, typhoid fever, dysentery, and hepatitis, which they contract by drinking contaminated water.

Student

That's horrible! Is anyone trying to solve this problem?

Alia

Yes, there are ongoing efforts to address this critical issue. Several American manufacturers have developed tabletop systems that bathe water with pathogen-killing ultraviolet light. One prototype model disinfects 15 gallons per minute, weighs about 15 pounds, and is powered by photovoltaic solar cells, which permit it to run unsupervised in remote locations.

Student

That sounds promising. But I'm guessing there are other water problems besides just bacteria and viruses, right?

Alia

You're absolutely right. Aside from pathogens, untreated water from wells or rivers may contain toxic metals that seep into the water supply from natural geologic formations. Many of the wells in Bangladesh, for example, are made very deep to avoid the pathogens that run rampant in the surface waters of the region. The water obtained from these deep wells, however, is highly contaminated with arsenic—a naturally occurring element in the Earth's crust.

Student

Wait, so they're avoiding bacteria but getting poisoned by arsenic instead? That seems like a lose-lose situation.

Alia

Unfortunately, that's correct. The arsenic is in the underlying rock, which formed from river sediments carried down from the Himalayas. Because this region is so densely populated, as many as 70 million people may be subject to some level of arsenic poisoning, which manifests itself as skin lesions and a higher susceptibility to cancer. Low-cost methods for removing arsenic from well water are greatly needed, as are worldwide recognition of this problem and the political, economic, and social support to overcome it.

Student

With all these freshwater problems, could we just use ocean water instead? I mean, there's so much of it, right?

Alia

That's a very logical question! With the depletion of sources of natural fresh water in many regions, there has been growing interest in techniques for generating fresh water from the Earth's far larger reserves of seawater or from brackish (moderately salty) groundwater. Worldwide, desalination plants operate in about 120 countries. In many areas of the Caribbean, North Africa, and the Middle East, desalinized water is the main source of municipal supply.

Student

Desalination—that's removing the salt, right? How do they do that?

Alia

Correct! The two primary methods of removing salts from seawater or brackish water are distillation and reverse osmosis. These techniques are also highly effective in removing a host of other contaminants, such as pathogens, fertilizers, and pesticides. Distillation and reverse osmosis, therefore, are also used to purify naturally occurring fresh water. Many popular brands of bottled water, for example, contain fresh water that has been treated either by distillation or by reverse osmosis.

Student

I think I know what distillation is - boiling water and collecting the steam, right? But what's reverse osmosis? That sounds complicated.

Alia

You're right about distillation! It involves vaporizing water with heat and then condensing the vapors into purified liquid water. Earth's desalinized water is often produced using this technique. Because water has such a high heat of vaporization, however, this technique is energy intensive.


There are solar distillers that avoid burning fuels, but they require about 1 square meter of surface area to produce 4 liters of fresh water per day. For a single home or a small village, this surface-area requirement may be easily accommodated.


Now, for reverse osmosis, we need to first understand osmosis. Osmosis involves a semipermeable membrane. A semipermeable membrane contains submicroscopic pores that allow the passage of water molecules but not of larger solute ions or solute molecules.

Student

This is getting really technical. I'm struggling to visualize what a semipermeable membrane is.

Alia

I understand. Think of a semipermeable membrane like a very fine strainer or filter. Water molecules are tiny and can pass through the small holes in this filter, but larger molecules or ions (like salt) are too big and get blocked.


When fresh water is separated from salt water by this semipermeable membrane, water molecules naturally move from the fresh water side to the salt water side. This happens because there are more water molecules along the fresh water face of the membrane than along the salt water face.

Student

Oh, so water moves to the saltier side? Is that why I feel dehydrated when I drink salty things or eat too many chips?

Alia

That's a clever connection! Yes, that's related. When you consume salty foods, the higher concentration of salt in your digestive system draws water from your body tissues through osmosis, which can contribute to feeling dehydrated.


To continue with our discussion, the net movement of water from the fresh water side to the salt water side is called osmosis. The result is a buildup in volume of the salt water and a decrease in volume of the fresh water. These changes in volume, in turn, allow a buildup in pressure, called osmotic pressure.

Student

So how does this help make fresh water from salt water? It sounds like osmosis would make the problem worse!

Alia

That's where reverse osmosis comes in! You're right that natural osmosis moves water from the fresh side to the salty side. In reverse osmosis, we apply external pressure to the salt water side. This pressure must be greater than the osmotic pressure.


When we apply this pressure, we force water molecules to move in the opposite direction—from the salt water side to the fresh water side. The semipermeable membrane still blocks the salt ions, so what passes through is fresh water.

Student

That sounds like it would take a lot of pressure. Is this practical on a large scale?

Alia

You've identified a key challenge! The osmotic pressure for seawater is an astounding 24.8 atmospheres (365 pounds per square inch). Generating pressures greater than this has its share of technical difficulties and is an energy-intensive process.


Nonetheless, engineers have succeeded in building durable reverse osmosis units that can be networked together to generate fresh water from seawater at rates of millions of gallons per day. Reverse osmosis desalination facilities treating brackish water, which require much lower external pressures, are proportionately more economical.

Student

This makes me think about bottled water. Is it really better than tap water? My roommate spends a fortune on it.

Alia

That's a very relevant question! In the United States, natural sources of fresh water are relatively plentiful, allowing utilities to sell fresh water at rates of a fraction of a penny per liter. Nonetheless, consumers are still willing to purchase bottled water at a price that is about 2,000 times as expensive than drinking it straight from the tap. In 2020, Americans spent about $16 billion on bottled water, which was up from about $4 billion in 2010.

Student

Wow, that's a huge markup! But is it healthier or better than tap water?

Alia

Not necessarily. Bottled water also comes with environmental costs. In the United States, billions of water bottles are discarded each year. Of these, only about 20 percent are recycled—the rest end up primarily in landfills. Over 50 million barrels of oil are burned each year to meet the market demand for bottled water in the United States alone. To put this into perspective, according to the Earth Policy Institute, the volume of oil needed to produce a single plastic bottle is about one-quarter the volume of the bottle.

Student

I never thought about the environmental impact. But don't some bottled waters have special properties? I see ads about "oxygenated water" or "structured water."

Alia

In an effort to overcome the ecological negatives of bottled water and a hefty price tag compared to tap water, many bottled-water marketers now focus on supposed peripheral benefits of their product. This includes the addition of dissolved oxygen, which can be no more than 0.0083 g/L at room temperature. For comparison, a single breath of air contains about 100 times the amount of molecular oxygen found in a half liter of "oxygenated" water. Furthermore, most of the gases accumulated in your gut simply pass out the opposite end from your mouth, assuming you don't burp.

Student

So the "oxygenated water" claims are basically meaningless? What about other special waters?

Alia

Yes, those claims don't hold much scientific water, so to speak! Even worse are claims of bottled water that contains "functional" water in which the structure of water has been modified using undetectable "subtle energy" to make the water more nutritious. This is where critical thinking is essential.


There is also water through which an electric current has been passed. The water companies claim that this creates two forms of water: alkaline and acidic. The alkaline water, they say, is good for you because it is "ionized, restructured, micro-clustered, activated, hydrogen–saturated, and oxidation–reduced." These sorts of misleading claims take advantage of the millions of people who don't understand that modifying the structure of water, H₂O, gives you something that is no longer water.

Student

So basically, it's just marketing nonsense using scientific-sounding terms?

Alia

Exactly! Buyer beware! Water is H₂O. If it's not H₂O, it isn't water. In a later chapter, we'll be discussing what actually happens when an electric current passes through water.


Interestingly, about 25 percent of bottled water sold in the United States is simply municipal water that has been purified via reverse osmosis.

Student

Wait, so people are paying for fancy tap water? That seems like a waste of money.

Alia

You're catching on! Many homeowners are now discovering that rather than purchasing purified water, it is less expensive and more ecologically sound to install a small reverse osmosis unit in their own home. Simple carbon filters, such as Brita™, are also highly effective at purifying water of nonpolar contaminants. These filters work because as the water passes through, many bad-tasting chemical compounds are absorbed by the carbon, including chlorine.

Student

That makes sense. My family uses a Brita filter at home, and it does taste better than tap water. What about sparkling water? Is that any better?

Alia

For fun, carbonators can also be purchased so that you can have your very own soda fountain. Carbonating your own water is not only fun but also cheaper and more ecologically sound than purchasing your soda from a store.
