Melting occurs when a substance changes from solid to liquid, and freezing occurs when a substance changes from liquid to solid. But did you know that at the melting/freezing temperature, both processes actually occur simultaneously?

Student

Wait, simultaneously? That doesn't make sense to me. How can something be melting and freezing at the same time? I thought ice either melts or freezes depending on whether it's getting warmer or colder.

Alia

That's a common misconception! Let's look at what happens with water at 0°C. At this temperature, water molecules in the liquid phase are moving slowly enough that they tend to clump together to form ice crystals—they freeze. But at this same temperature, water molecules in ice are vibrating with great commotion, much more than they vibrate at colder temperatures. Many of these molecules break loose from the crystalline structure to form liquid water—they melt.

Student

So you're saying at exactly 0°C, some water molecules are freezing while others are melting? I never thought about it that way.

Alia

Exactly! For water, 0°C is the special temperature at which the rate of ice formation equals the rate of liquid water formation. The opposite processes of melting and freezing counterbalance each other. This means that if a mixture of ice and liquid water is maintained at exactly 0°C, the two phases can coexist indefinitely.

Student

Oh, that makes sense why ice water stays as a mixture! So what happens if we want all the ice to freeze or all of it to melt?

Alia

Great question! Anytime we want a mixture of ice and liquid water at 0°C to freeze solid, we need to favor the rate of ice formation. This is accomplished by removing heat (cooling it down), which facilitates the formation of hydrogen bonds.

Student

Hydrogen bonds? I've heard that term before but I'm not sure I understand what they have to do with freezing.

Alia

Hydrogen bonds are key to understanding the freezing process. When water molecules come together to form a hydrogen bond, heat energy is released. For the molecules to remain hydrogen-bonded, this heat energy must be removed—otherwise, it can be reabsorbed by the molecules, causing them to separate. The removal of heat energy allows hydrogen bonds to remain intact after they have formed, and as a result, ice crystals tend to grow.

Student

So when I put water in the freezer, I'm helping remove that heat energy so the hydrogen bonds stay together?

Alia

That's exactly right! Conversely, if we want to melt ice completely, we add heat. This heat energy goes into breaking apart the hydrogen bonds that hold the water molecules together in the form of ice. As more hydrogen bonds between water molecules break, the ice crystals melt.

Student

I think I get it. So freezing releases heat, and melting requires heat input. That actually connects to something I've experienced - when I put a warm drink in the freezer and check it later, sometimes the bottle feels warmer before it starts getting cold. Is that related?

Alia

That's a really insightful observation! What you're noticing could be related to the heat released during the initial stages of freezing as those hydrogen bonds form. Now, let's talk about how solutes affect freezing.

Student

Solutes? You mean like salt or sugar dissolved in water?

Alia

Yes, exactly! Solutes tend to inhibit crystal formation. When a solute, such as table salt or sugar, is added to water, the solute molecules take up space. When a solute is added to a mixture of ice and liquid water at 0°C, the solute molecules effectively decrease the number of liquid water molecules at the solid-liquid interface.

Student

So the salt molecules get in the way?

Alia

That's a good way to think about it. With fewer liquid water molecules available to join the ice crystals, the rate of ice formation decreases. Because ice is a relatively pure form of water, the number of molecules moving from the solid phase to the liquid phase is not affected by the presence of solute. The net result is that the rate at which water molecules leave the solid phase is greater than the rate at which they enter the solid phase.

Student

That explains why they put salt on icy roads in winter! Is that why salt water has a lower freezing point than pure water?

Alia

Exactly! This imbalance can be compensated for by decreasing the temperature to below 0°C. At lower temperatures, water molecules in the liquid phase move more slowly and coalesce more easily, increasing the rate of crystal formation. In general, adding anything to water lowers the freezing point. Antifreeze for cars is a practical application of this process, and yes, the salting of icy roads is another example.

Student

This is actually starting to make sense! I've heard of something called "supercooling" - is that related to all this?

Alia

Yes, it is! If left undisturbed, chilled purified bottled water can become "supercooled," which means it remains liquid at temperatures below 0°C. This happens because crystal formation usually requires a substrate, such as a microparticle, upon which to start growing. Upon opening, the bottle of supercooled water will rapidly turn into an icy slush.

It is fascinating, isn't it? Similarly, during an ice storm, raindrops formed in warm upper layers of the atmosphere become supercooled as they pass through colder lower layers. Upon hitting the ground, tree branches, and power lines, the supercooled raindrops, known as freezing rain, quickly freeze. The results can be beautiful but also quite hazardous, especially for aircraft.

I never realized there was such complex science behind freezing rain! So to check my understanding - when a solute is present in water, it decreases the rate of freezing because...?

When a solute is present, there are fewer water molecules in contact with the surface of any ice crystal. This makes it more difficult for the ice crystal to grow.

Got it. You know, I've noticed something else about water - when I fill ice cube trays, the ice cubes always seem to take up more space than the water I put in. Why is that?

Alia

That's a great observation! Let's talk about thermal expansion and water's unique density properties. When the temperature of a substance is increased, its molecules vibrate faster and move farther apart on average, resulting in expansion. With few exceptions, all phases of matter—solids, liquids, and gases—expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Student

So things normally get bigger when heated and smaller when cooled?

Alia

Yes, that's right. In many cases, these changes in volume are not very noticeable, but with careful observation, you can usually detect them. Telephone wires, for instance, are longer and sag more on a hot summer day than on a cold winter day. Metal lids on glass jars can often be loosened by heating them under hot water.

Those are great examples! I've definitely run hot water over tight jar lids before.

Alia

Within any given phase, water also expands with increasing temperature and contracts with decreasing temperature. This is true of all three phases—ice, liquid water, and water vapor. However, liquid water at near-freezing temperatures is a fascinating exception.

Student

An exception? How so?

Alia

Liquid water at 0°C can flow just like any other liquid, but at 0°C, the temperature is cold enough that nanoscopic crystals of ice are able to form within the liquid water. Do you know what forms in 0°C liquid water?

Student

Hmm, based on what you just said, I'm guessing tiny ice crystals form?

Alia

Exactly! Nanoscopic ice crystals form in 0°C liquid water. These crystals slightly "bloat" the liquid water's volume. As the temperature is increased to above 0°C, more and more of these crystals collapse, and as a result, the volume of the liquid water decreases.

Student

Wait, that's counterintuitive. You're saying that as water warms from 0°C, it actually gets smaller instead of expanding like most substances do when heated?

Alia

Yes! Between 0°C and 4°C, liquid water contracts as its temperature is raised. This contraction, however, continues only up to 4°C. As near-freezing liquid water is heated, there is a simultaneous tendency for the water to expand due to greater molecular motion. Between 0°C and 4°C, the decrease in volume caused by collapsing ice crystals is greater than the increase in volume caused by the faster-moving molecules. At temperatures just above 4°C, expansion overrides contraction because most of the ice crystals have collapsed.

Student

So water is densest at 4°C? That seems like such a random temperature to be important.

Alia

It may seem random, but it's incredibly significant! Because of the effect of collapsing nanoscopic ice crystals, liquid water has its smallest volume and thus its greatest density at 4°C. By definition, 1 gram of pure water at this temperature has a volume of 1.0000 milliliter. 1 gram of liquid water at 0°C has a slightly larger volume, 1.0002 milliliters. By comparison, 1 gram of ice at 0°C has a volume of 1.0870 milliliters.

Student

So ice is significantly less dense than liquid water? Is that why ice floats?

Alia

Exactly! Even when ice is cooled to temperatures well below freezing, it is still less dense than liquid water. This property is quite unusual - most substances are denser in their solid phase than in their liquid phase.

Student

I never realized how unique water is. But why does it matter that water is densest at 4°C rather than at the freezing point?

Alia

Although liquid water at 4°C is only slightly denser than liquid water at 0°C, this small difference is of great importance in nature. Consider that if water were densest at its freezing point, as is true of most other liquids, the coldest water in a pond would settle to the bottom and the pond would freeze from the bottom up, destroying living organisms in winter months.

Student

Oh! So this unusual property of water actually protects aquatic life?

Alia

Precisely! As winter comes on and the temperature of the water drops, its density changes in a very specific pattern. The entire volume of water in a pond does not cool all at once. Surface water cools first because it is in direct contact with the cold air. Being cooler than the underlying water, this surface water becomes denser and sinks, with warmer water rising to replace it.

Student

So there's a kind of circulation happening?

Alia

Yes, that new batch of surface water then cools to the air temperature, gets denser as it does so, and sinks, only to be replaced by warmer water that cools. This process continues until the entire body of water has been cooled to 4°C.

Student

And then what happens when it gets even colder?

Alia

Then if the air temperature remains below 4°C, the surface water also cools to below 4°C. This surface water does not sink, however, because at this colder temperature, it is now less dense than the water below it. Thus, cooler, less dense water stays on the surface, where it can cool further, eventually reach 0°C, and turn to ice.

Student

So the ice forms at the top, while the water below stays liquid at 4°C. That's so interesting - I had no idea that lakes and ponds had this temperature structure!

Alia

Exactly right! While ice forms at the surface, the organisms that require a liquid environment are happily swimming below the ice in liquid water at a "warm" 4°C. This is a beautiful example of how the physical properties of water sustain life on our planet.

That's amazing! So does this vertical movement of water have other benefits too?

Alia

Great question! An important effect of the vertical movement of water is the creation of vertical currents that transport oxygen-rich surface water to the bottom and nutrient-rich bottom water to the surface, benefiting organisms living in the water. Marine biologists refer to this vertical cycling of water and nutrients as upwelling.

Actually, very deep bodies of fresh water are not ice-covered even in the coldest of winters. This is because all the water must be cooled to 4°C before the temperature of the surface water can drop below 4°C. For deep water, the winter is not long enough for this to occur.

Student

That makes sense. So for really deep lakes, what temperature is the water at the bottom?

Alia

If only some of the water in a pond is 4°C, this water lies on the bottom. Because of water's ability to resist changes in temperature and its poor ability to conduct heat, the bottom of deep bodies of water in cold regions is a constant 4°C year-round.

So if I asked you what the temperature was at the bottom of Lake Michigan on New Year's Eve in 1901, what would you say?

Alia

The temperature at the bottom of Lake Michigan on New Year's Eve in 1901 would have been 4°C. If a body of water has 4°C water, then the temperature at the bottom of that body of water is 4°C, for the same reason that rocks are at the bottom. Both 4°C water and rocks are denser than water at any other temperature. If the body of water is deep and in a region of short summers, as is the case for Lake Michigan, the water at the bottom is 4°C year-round.
