Exactly! Those experiences relate directly to specific heat. Different substances have different capacities for storing energy. This happens because different materials absorb energy in different ways. When energy is added to a substance, it may increase the jiggling motion of molecules, which raises the temperature, or it may pull apart the attractions among molecules and therefore go into potential energy, which does not raise the temperature. Generally, there's a combination of both occurring.

That's a great way to understand it! Now, let me share something specific about water: It takes 4.184 joules of energy to raise the temperature of 1 gram of liquid water by 1°C. Compare that to iron, where it takes only about one-ninth as much energy to raise the temperature of 1 gram of iron by the same amount.

Student

Wait, so water is harder to heat up than iron? That seems backwards. I would have thought metal would be harder to heat.

Alia

Your intuition is understandable, but water actually absorbs more heat for the same change in temperature compared to iron. We say water has a higher specific heat, which is defined as the quantity of heat required to change the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1°C.

Student

So specific heat is like... how much energy you need to make something get hotter?

Alia

Yes! We can think of specific heat as thermal inertia. Remember from physics that inertia signifies the resistance of an object to a change in its state of motion. Specific heat is like a thermal version of inertia because it signifies the resistance of a substance to a change in temperature. With a higher specific heat, more energy is required to change the temperature. Each substance has its own characteristic specific heat, which can help in identifying it.

Student

Thermal inertia... I like that way of thinking about it. So water resists temperature changes more than most substances?

Alia

Exactly! And can you guess why water has such a high specific heat?

Alia

That's part of it, but the complete answer once again involves hydrogen bonds. When heat is applied to water, much of that heat is consumed in breaking hydrogen bonds. These broken hydrogen bonds represent a form of potential energy - similar to when you pull two magnets apart. Much of the heat added to water is stored as this potential energy. Consequently, less heat is available to increase the kinetic energy of the water molecules.

Student

And temperature is related to kinetic energy, right?

Alia

Correct! Because temperature is a measure of kinetic energy, we find that as water is heated, its temperature rises slowly. By the same token, when water is cooled, its temperature drops slowly too. This happens because as the kinetic energy decreases, molecules slow down and more hydrogen bonds are able to re-form. This in turn releases heat that helps to maintain the temperature.

Student

So water basically heats itself back up as it cools? That's pretty cool!

Alia

Yes! As water cools down, it actually heats itself back up by the formation of hydrogen bonds. That's why it takes so long for water to cool down. You can think of water as a very effective "heat sponge" - it has a great capacity to hold onto heat, which is why specific heat is sometimes referred to as heat capacity.

Excellent reasoning! The specific heats of ice and water vapor are about half that of liquid water. Only liquid water has a remarkable specific heat. This is because the liquid phase is the only phase in which hydrogen bonds are continually breaking and re-forming.

Alia

That's a great connection to make! The tendency of liquid water to resist changes in temperature improves the climate in many places. Take Europe, for example. If water did not have a high specific heat, the countries of Europe would be as cold as the northeastern regions of Canada, because both Europe and Canada get about the same amount of sunlight per square kilometer of surface area.

The energy released, 4.184 joules per degree Celsius for each gram of water that cools, is carried by the westerly winds (winds that blow west to east) over the European continent.

On the western coast of the continent, air moves from the Pacific Ocean to the land. Because of water's high specific heat, ocean temperatures don't vary much from summer to winter. In winter, the water warms the air, which is then blown eastward over the coastal regions. In summer, the water cools the air and the coastal regions are cooled.

Alia

Let's keep it simple. Heat must be applied to increase the temperature of a material. Conversely, heat must be withdrawn from a material in order to decrease its temperature. The amount of heat required for a given temperature change is calculated from the equation:


heat = specific heat × mass × temperature change


We can use this formula for any material, provided there is no change of phase over the course of the temperature change. The value of the temperature change is obtained by subtracting the initial temperature Ti from the final temperature Tf:


temperature change = Tf − Ti

heat = (4.184 J/g°C)(1.00 g)(10°C) = 41.8 J


So it takes 41.8 joules of energy to raise 1 gram of water by 10°C.
