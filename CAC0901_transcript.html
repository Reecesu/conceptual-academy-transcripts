Alia

That's right! At its core, chemical reactions are about atoms rearranging to form new compounds. Let's begin by discussing chemical equations, which are how we represent these reactions in written form.


As we discussed in previous chapters, during a chemical reaction, atoms rearrange to form one or more new compounds. A chemical equation shows this process by listing the reacting substances, called reactants, before an arrow that points to the newly formed substances, called products.

Student

Okay, so reactants → products. That makes sense. But what else do we need to include in these equations?

Alia

Great question! Typically, reactants and products are represented by their elemental or chemical formulas. Sometimes molecular models or simply names may be used instead. We also often show phases: (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, and (g) for gas. Compounds dissolved in water are designated (aq) for aqueous solution.


Additionally, we place numbers called coefficients in front of the reactants or products to show the ratio in which they combine or form. These coefficients represent numbers of individual atoms and molecules.

Student

Can you give me an example of a chemical equation?

Alia

Certainly! For instance, to represent the chemical reaction in which carbon burns in the presence of oxygen to form gaseous carbon dioxide, we write the chemical equation:


C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)


The (s) indicates that carbon is a solid, while (g) indicates that oxygen and carbon dioxide are gases.

Student

I see. But why are there no numbers in front of the C, O₂, or CO₂?

Alia

That's very observant! By convention, the coefficient 1 is omitted. So when you don't see a coefficient in front of a chemical formula, it's understood to be 1.


One of the most important principles of chemistry is the law of mass conservation, which we discussed in earlier chapters. This law states that matter is neither created nor destroyed during a chemical reaction. The atoms present at the beginning of a reaction are merely rearranged.

Student

So that means whatever atoms we start with, we end up with the same number after the reaction?

Alia

Exactly! This means that no atoms are lost or gained during any reaction. The chemical equation must therefore be balanced. In a balanced equation, each atom must appear on both sides of the arrow the same number of times.


Let's check our example: C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
On the left side, we have 1 carbon atom and 2 oxygen atoms.
On the right side, we have 1 carbon atom and 2 oxygen atoms.
The equation is balanced because each side shows the same number of each type of atom.

Student

That makes sense. But what if we have a more complicated reaction? How do we know if it's balanced?

Alia

Let me show you another example. In another chemical reaction, two hydrogen gas molecules, H₂, react with one oxygen gas molecule, O₂, to produce two molecules of water, H₂O, in the gaseous phase:


2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(g)


Let's count the atoms:
Left side: 4 hydrogen atoms (2 × H₂) and 2 oxygen atoms (1 × O₂)
Right side: 4 hydrogen atoms (2 × H₂O) and 2 oxygen atoms (2 × H₂O)


This equation is also balanced—there are four hydrogen and two oxygen atoms before and after the arrow.

Student

Oh wait, I'm confused about the numbers. So the 2 in front of H₂ means two hydrogen molecules, and each H₂ has two hydrogen atoms, so that's 4 hydrogen atoms total?

Alia

That's absolutely correct! A coefficient in front of a chemical formula tells us the number of times that element or compound must be counted. For example, 2H₂O indicates two water molecules, which contain a total of four hydrogen atoms (2 × 2) and two oxygen atoms (2 × 1).

Student

What happens if an equation isn't balanced? Is that wrong?

Alia

An unbalanced chemical equation shows the reactants and products without the correct coefficients. For example:


NO(g) → N₂O(g) + NO₂(g)


This equation is not balanced because there is one nitrogen atom and one oxygen atom before the arrow, but three nitrogen atoms and three oxygen atoms after the arrow. This would violate the law of mass conservation.

Student

So how do we fix that?

Alia

You can balance unbalanced equations by adding or changing coefficients to produce correct ratios. It's important not to change subscripts, however, because to do so changes the compound's identity—H₂O is water, but H₂O₂ is hydrogen peroxide.


For the example I just gave, to balance the equation, we place a 3 before the NO:


3NO(g) → N₂O(g) + NO₂(g)


Now there are three nitrogen atoms and three oxygen atoms on each side of the arrow, and the law of mass conservation is not violated.

Student

I see. So we need to adjust the coefficients, not the subscripts. That makes sense because changing the subscripts would make it a completely different substance.

Alia

Exactly! Practicing chemists develop a skill for balancing equations. This skill involves creative energy and, like other skills, improves with experience. More important than being an expert at balancing equations is knowing why they need to be balanced—because atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction, they are simply rearranged.

Student

This is starting to make more sense, but honestly, I'm a bit overwhelmed about how to approach balancing these equations. Any tips?

Alia

Many students enjoy learning how to balance chemical equations. Perhaps this is because balancing an equation is akin to solving a crossword or Sudoku puzzle. Let me share a quick guide to help you:


1. Focus on balancing only one element at a time. Start with the leftmost element.
2. Move to the next element and modify the coefficients so as to balance this element. Don't worry if you temporarily unbalance the previous element.
3. Continue from left to right focusing on only one element at a time.
4. Repeat until all the elements are balanced.


Remember, unbalanced equations are balanced by changing the coefficients, never the subscripts. Also, a coefficient is a multiplier for all the atoms within a molecule. For example, writing 2Fe₂O₃ means four Fe atoms and six O atoms.

Student

That helps. So going back to why chemical equations must be balanced - it's because of the law of mass conservation, right?

Alia

That's correct! Chemical equations must be balanced because of the law of mass conservation, which tells us that atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction—they are simply rearranged. So, every atom present before the reaction must be present after the reaction, even though the groupings of atoms are different.

Student

I think I understand better now. Are there different types of chemical reactions we should know about?

Alia

Yes, chemical equations can be classified based upon how the atoms of a reaction rearrange to become products. There are five main categories: synthesis, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement, and combustion.

Alia

Great! Let's start with synthesis reactions.


In a synthesis reaction, two or more reactants combine to make a product containing the atoms of the reactants in some combination. It follows the general formula A + B → AB.


Some examples include the formation of ammonia and water:
3H₂ + N₂ → 2NH₃
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Student

That seems straightforward. What about decomposition?

Alia

In a decomposition reaction, a reactant consisting of a grouping of multiple atoms splits apart into multiple products. A decomposition reaction follows the general formula AB → A + B.


Some examples include the decomposition of calcium carbonate and hydrogen peroxide:
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂

Student

I think I've seen hydrogen peroxide foam up when it touches a cut. Is that related to this decomposition reaction?

Alia

That's an excellent observation! Yes, when hydrogen peroxide comes into contact with an enzyme called catalase (found in blood and cells), it decomposes into water and oxygen gas. The bubbling you see is the oxygen gas being released. That's a perfect real-world example of a decomposition reaction!

Student

Cool! That helps me understand it better. What about the other types?

Alia

Next, we have single replacement reactions. In a single replacement reaction, an atom (or polyatomic ion) jumps from one reactant to another. It follows the general formula AX + B → A + BX.


Examples include:
Zn + CuCl₂ → ZnCl₂ + Cu
2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂

Student

So in the first example, the zinc replaces the copper in copper chloride?

Alia

That's exactly right! The zinc atom replaces (or displaces) the copper atom in copper chloride, resulting in zinc chloride and free copper.


Now, for double replacement reactions, an atom (or polyatomic ion) jumps from each reactant—there is a double switch. It follows the general formula AY + BX → BY + AX.


Examples include:
AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
HCl + NaOH → HOH + NaCl

That's very perceptive! Yes, HOH is another way to write water (H₂O). In this reaction between an acid (HCl) and a base (NaOH), one product is indeed water. The last type is the combustion reaction, which we'll explore in more detail in later chapters. The products of a combustion reaction include carbon dioxide, CO₂, and water, H₂O, plus lots of energy.


Examples include the combustion of methane and glucose:
CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O
