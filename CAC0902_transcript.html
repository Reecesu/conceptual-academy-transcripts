It matters because if a chemist doesn't respect these ratios, they would be wasting chemicals and money. For instance, if they tried to combine four carbon atoms for every one oxygen molecule when making carbon dioxide, the excess carbon atoms would have no oxygen molecules to react with and would remain unchanged.

Great question! Rather than counting these particles individually, chemists use a scale that measures the mass of bulk quantities. Why not? Couldn't you just weigh out the same amount of each chemical?

Let me explain with an analogy. Think about Ping-Pong balls and golf balls. You know that Ping-Pong balls are much lighter than golf balls. If you were to measure out 1 kilogram of each, you would need many more Ping-Pong balls to create a full kilogram than you would need golf balls.

That's exactly right! You understand that the number of something and the mass of something are two different things and that they should never be confused with each other. This same principle applies to atoms and molecules.

So in chemistry, different atoms have different weights just like different types of balls?

Alia

Precisely! Because different atoms and molecules have different masses, there are different numbers of them in a sample of the same mass. Carbon atoms are less massive than oxygen molecules. So clearly, equal masses of these two substances do not yield equal numbers of carbon atoms and oxygen molecules.

Measuring out 20 times as much mass of golf balls as of Ping-Pong balls, therefore, gives equal numbers of each.

That's exactly right! You're catching on quickly.

Where do we turn to find the relative masses of atoms and molecules? You guessed it! The masses of elements shown in the periodic table are relative masses. Using these masses, we can measure out equal numbers of atoms or molecules.

Yes, they do! For example, the mass of carbon is 12.011 amu. One atomic mass unit (amu) equals 1.661 × 10^-24 gram. The formula mass of a substance is the sum of the atomic masses of the elements in its chemical formula.

Exactly. Consider the formula mass of an oxygen molecule, O₂. It's about 32 amu (15.999 amu + 15.999 amu). A carbon atom, therefore, is about 12/32 as massive as an oxygen molecule.

To measure out equal numbers of carbon atoms and oxygen molecules, we could measure out 12 grams of carbon and 32 grams of molecular oxygen.
