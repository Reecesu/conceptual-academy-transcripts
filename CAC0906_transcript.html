Good observation! The reaction rate tells us how quickly the reactants transform to products. Initially, a reaction flask may contain only reactant molecules. As time passes, these reactants form product molecules, and the concentration of product molecules increases. So, the reaction rate can be defined either as how quickly the concentration of products increases or how quickly the concentration of reactants decreases.

Student

That makes sense. But what determines how fast or slow a reaction happens? Is it just random?

Alia

That's an excellent question! The rate of a chemical reaction is determined by several factors, with one important factor being that reactant molecules must physically come together through collisions.


For example, let's consider the reaction of gaseous nitrogen and gaseous oxygen to form gaseous nitrogen monoxide. Because reactant molecules must collide for a reaction to occur, we can increase the reaction rate by increasing the number of collisions.

Student

So more collisions mean faster reactions? How do we get more collisions?

Alia

An effective way to increase the number of collisions is to increase the concentration of the reactants. With higher concentrations, there are more molecules in a given volume, which makes collisions between molecules more probable.


Think of it like people on a dance floor—as the number of people increases, so does the rate at which they bump into one another. Similarly, an increase in the concentration of nitrogen and oxygen molecules leads to a greater number of collisions between these molecules, resulting in more nitrogen monoxide molecules forming in a given period of time.

Student

That dance floor analogy really helps! But wait—if molecules are constantly bumping into each other in the air around us, why aren't chemical reactions happening all the time?

Alia

That's a perceptive question! Not all collisions between reactant molecules lead to products. There are two main reasons for this:


First, molecules must collide in a certain orientation to react. Nitrogen and oxygen, for example, are much more likely to form nitrogen monoxide when the molecules collide in a parallel orientation. When they collide perpendicularly, nitrogen monoxide typically doesn't form. For larger molecules with numerous possible orientations, this requirement becomes even more restrictive.

Student

Alia

Yes, there's a second important factor: the reactant molecules must also collide with enough kinetic energy to break their bonds. Only then can the atoms in the reactant molecules change bonding partners and form product molecules.


The bonds in N₂ and O₂ molecules, for example, are quite strong. For these bonds to break, collisions between the molecules must contain enough energy. As a result, collisions between slow-moving N₂ and O₂ molecules, even those that collide in the proper orientation, may not form NO.

Exactly! You've made a great connection to everyday experience. The higher the temperature of a material, the faster its molecules move and the more forceful and frequent the collisions between them. Higher temperatures therefore increase reaction rates.


Consider the nitrogen and oxygen molecules in our atmosphere. They're continually colliding with one another. However, at normal atmospheric temperatures, these molecules don't generally have sufficient kinetic energy to form nitrogen monoxide. But during a lightning strike, the intense heat dramatically increases the kinetic energy of these molecules, resulting in many collisions forming nitrogen monoxide.

Yes! The nitrogen monoxide formed during lightning strikes undergoes further atmospheric reactions to form chemicals known as nitrates, which plants depend on for survival. This is an example of nitrogen fixation, which you might have learned about in a biology class.

Absolutely. The energy required to break bonds can also come from the absorption of electromagnetic radiation, such as light. As radiation is absorbed by reactant molecules, the atoms in the molecules may start to vibrate so rapidly that the bonds between them are easily broken.


In many instances, the direct absorption of electromagnetic radiation is sufficient to break chemical bonds and initiate a chemical reaction. For example, the common atmospheric pollutant nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) may transform to nitrogen monoxide and atomic oxygen merely upon exposure to sunlight:

That's a perfect real-world example! Yes, sunlight can initiate chemical reactions that cause dyes in fabric to break down, resulting in fading. Now, let's discuss a key concept related to the energy needed for reactions: activation energy.


Whether broken bonds result from collisions, from the absorption of electromagnetic radiation, or both, they are a necessary first step in most chemical reactions. The energy required for this initial breaking of bonds can be viewed as an energy barrier. The minimum energy required to overcome this energy barrier is known as the activation energy (Eₐ).

Student

Activation energy... so it's like the minimum amount of energy needed to get the reaction started? I'm still not sure I fully understand this concept.

Alia

Think of activation energy like the energy a car needs to drive over the top of a hill. Without sufficient energy to climb to the top of the hill, it isn't possible for the car to get to the other side. Likewise, reactant molecules can transform to product molecules only if the reactant molecules possess an amount of energy equal to or greater than the activation energy.


In the reaction between nitrogen and oxygen to form nitrogen monoxide, the activation energy is very high (because the bonds in N₂ and O₂ are strong). This means that only the fastest-moving nitrogen and oxygen molecules possess sufficient energy to react. Visually, we can represent this activation energy as a vertical hump that molecules need to overcome.

Student

Oh, that hill analogy really helps! So does that mean that in any group of molecules, only some of them have enough energy to react?

Alia

Yes, exactly! At any given temperature, there is a wide distribution of kinetic energies in reactant molecules. Some are moving slowly, and others are moving quickly. The temperature of a material is related to the average of all these kinetic energies.


The few fast-moving reactant molecules have enough energy to pass over the energy barrier, and these are the ones that transform to product molecules.

Student

So when we increase temperature, more molecules get enough energy to overcome that activation energy barrier?

Alia

That's precisely right! When the temperature of the reactants is increased, the number of reactant molecules possessing sufficient energy to pass over the barrier also increases. This is why reactions are generally faster at higher temperatures.


Conversely, at lower temperatures, fewer molecules have sufficient energy to pass over the barrier, so reactions are generally slower at lower temperatures.

On a warm day, the chemical reactions occurring in an alligator are "up to speed," and the animal is more active.
