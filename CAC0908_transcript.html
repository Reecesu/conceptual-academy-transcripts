For example, when electricity passes through water, the water can readily transform into O₂ and H₂. In principle, if we applied a strong electric current to the ocean for an incredibly long time, all the water would be converted into gaseous hydrogen and oxygen. But the first strike of a match would initiate a massive explosion as the two gases exothermically convert back into water. This demonstrates that chemical reactions are reversible - some more readily than others.

Exactly! Let's look at another example that might be easier to observe. Consider nitrogen dioxide, NO₂, which is a brown gas and one of the toxic components of air pollution. At room temperature, nitrogen dioxide molecules join to become colorless dinitrogen tetroxide, N₂O₄:


2NO₂ → N₂O₄


Once dinitrogen tetroxide molecules are formed, they can break apart to re-form nitrogen dioxide:


N₂O₄ → 2NO₂

So they just keep going back and forth? That seems inefficient.

Alia

It might seem inefficient, but it's actually a fundamental principle of chemistry! Once the nitrogen dioxide molecules re-form, they can get together again to re-form the dinitrogen tetroxide. The net result is two competing reactions that keep occurring simultaneously. We depict this using double arrows:


2NO₂ ⇌ N₂O₄

Student

Oh, I think I've seen that double arrow symbol before. But I don't understand why the chemicals would keep changing back and forth.

Alia

Think of it this way - initially, we might start with pure nitrogen dioxide, NO₂. Soon, however, the percentage of nitrogen dioxide decreases and the percentage of dinitrogen tetroxide, N₂O₄, increases. This shift continues to a point where the two reactions balance each other and the percentages of NO₂ and N₂O₄ settle to constant values.


At room temperature, this balance is about 31% NO₂ and 69% N₂O₄. At this point, the forward and reverse reactions have achieved what we call chemical equilibrium, where the rate at which products are formed is equal to the rate at which they are converted back into reactants.

Student

So equilibrium is when everything stabilizes and the amounts stop changing? Is it like when you're adding and removing the same number of books from a shelf, so the total number of books stays the same?

Alia

That's a fantastic analogy! Yes, at equilibrium, the amounts of each chemical remain constant, though individual molecules are still converting back and forth. Chemists don't usually express this balance in terms of percentages, though. They use equilibrium constants.


An equilibrium constant is a number that relates the amounts of reactants to products at equilibrium. For example, if we have a reaction where a moles of reactant A and b moles of reactant B react to give c moles of product C and d moles of product D at equilibrium:


aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD


We can express the relative concentrations using an equilibrium constant, Keq, with this mathematical form:


Keq = [C]^c[D]^d/[A]^a[B]^b

Student

Whoa, that formula looks intimidating. What do all those brackets mean?

Alia

The brackets represent the concentrations of each substance. Don't worry too much about the math right now - the important concept is what the equilibrium constant tells us. Since an equilibrium constant is a ratio of products to reactants, a value of Keq greater than 1 means products are favored over reactants. If Keq is less than 1, reactants are favored at equilibrium. In other words, a large Keq means products are favored while a small Keq means the reactants are favored.

Student

Okay, so it's just a way to know which side of the reaction is "winning" at equilibrium?

If you think of people inside the store as product molecules and those outside as reactant molecules, the steady customer flow means that the number of people entering the store in any given time equals the number of people leaving.


As a result, the number of people inside the store never changes. The individual people change as Ms. X and Mr. Y enter and Mr. A and Ms. B leave, but the total number inside remains constant.

Alia

Exactly! The number of N₂O₄ molecules in a flask stays constant because the rate at which N₂O₄ molecules convert to NO₂ molecules equals the rate at which N₂O₄ molecules are formed from NO₂ molecules.

The important thing to remember is that chemical equilibrium is a dynamic state. Although the concentrations of products and reactants remain constant, individual molecules are continuously changing back and forth.

Student

Great question! If we return to our store analogy, imagine the store holds a big sale. The equilibrium of customers is affected. At the beginning of the sale, more people enter than leave, so the number inside increases. Eventually, the store reaches capacity and a doorman ensures people enter only at the rate others leave. A new equilibrium is established with more people in the store than before.


Similarly, chemical equilibria are affected by prevailing conditions. This is formally stated as Le Chatelier's principle:


"If a stress is applied to a system in chemical equilibrium, the system changes to relieve the stress."

At this higher-temperature equilibrium, nitrogen dioxide, NO₂, predominates, and the overall color of the reaction mixture is darker brown. This is one reason air pollution becomes particularly noticeable on warm days: the higher the temperature, the greater the concentration of brown nitrogen dioxide.
