Yes, exactly! Commercial soap is slightly alkaline, or basic. Many drain cleaners contain sodium hydroxide, also known as lye, which is extremely basic when concentrated. Yes, solutions containing bases are often called alkaline. The term comes from the Arabic "al-qali," meaning "the ashes." Ashes feel slippery when wet because they contain bases like potassium carbonate.

Student

So everyday things like soap and drain cleaners are bases. And acids include things like vinegar and citrus fruits. Got it. But what makes something an acid or a base chemically?

Alia

That's a great question! There are several ways to define acids and bases. We'll focus on the Brønsted-Lowry definition, which was proposed in 1923. According to this definition, an acid is any chemical that donates hydrogen ions, H+, and a base is any chemical that accepts hydrogen ions.

Student

What exactly is a hydrogen ion?

Alia

A hydrogen atom consists of one electron surrounding a one-proton nucleus. When it loses its electron, what remains is a hydrogen ion, H+, which is essentially just a lone proton. So in the Brønsted-Lowry definition, an acid donates a proton and a base accepts a proton.

Student

So acids give away protons, and bases take them. That doesn't sound too complicated.

Alia

Exactly! Let's see this in action. When hydrogen chloride (HCl) is mixed into water, the hydrogen chloride donates a hydrogen ion to the water molecule. In this case, hydrogen chloride is behaving as an acid (proton donor) and water is behaving as a base (proton acceptor).

Student

What happens to the water after it accepts the proton?

Alia

When water accepts a proton, it forms what's called a hydronium ion, H3O+. This is simply a water molecule with an extra hydrogen attached, giving it a positive charge.

Student

So the water molecule becomes positively charged because it gained a proton?

Alia

That's correct! The hydronium ion's positive charge comes from that extra proton. It's an example of a polyatomic ion - a molecule that carries a net electric charge.

Student

What about bases? What happens when they accept protons?

Alia

Let's look at ammonia (NH3) as an example. When added to water, ammonia behaves as a base. Its nonbonding electrons accept a hydrogen ion from water, which in this case behaves as an acid. This reaction forms an ammonium ion (NH4+) and a hydroxide ion (OH-).

Student

Alia

I understand your confusion! This is actually an important aspect of the Brønsted-Lowry definition - it defines acids and bases by their behavior in a particular reaction, not as inherent properties. Water can indeed behave as either an acid or a base depending on what it's reacting with. When mixed with hydrogen chloride, water behaves as a base (accepts H+). When mixed with ammonia, water behaves as an acid (donates H+).

Student

That makes sense. It's kind of like how people behave differently depending on who they're with.

Alia

That's an excellent analogy! Just as you might be quiet around some people but talkative around others, a chemical such as water can behave as a base in one reaction and as an acid in another. It's about the behavior in a specific context, not a fixed identity.

Student

What happens after these reactions? Do the products just stay as they are?

Alia

Actually, these reactions can go in both directions. For example, once formed, an ammonium ion can donate a hydrogen ion back to a hydroxide ion to re-form ammonia and water. We represent this with two oppositely facing arrows, showing that the reaction happens in both directions simultaneously.

So it's like a back-and-forth exchange?

Alia

Exactly! When viewed from left to right, ammonia behaves as a base by accepting a hydrogen ion from water (which acts as an acid). In the reverse direction, the ammonium ion behaves as an acid by donating a hydrogen ion to the hydroxide ion (which behaves as a base).

Let me see if I understand. If I have this reaction: H2PO4- + H3O+ ⇌ H3PO4 + H2O, can I identify which ones are acting as acids or bases?

That's a great example to practice with! In the forward reaction (left to right), H2PO4- gains a hydrogen ion to become H3PO4. Since it's accepting the hydrogen ion, H2PO4- is behaving as a base. It gets this hydrogen ion from H3O+, which is behaving as an acid.


In the reverse direction, H3PO4 loses a hydrogen ion to become H2PO4- and is thus behaving as an acid. The hydrogen ion goes to H2O, which transforms into H3O+ and is therefore behaving as a base.

Student

That makes sense now! But what about molecules that don't have hydrogen? Can they still be acids or bases?

Alia

That's where another definition becomes useful. The Brønsted-Lowry definition is limited to molecules that can donate or accept protons. In the 1930s, Gilbert Lewis proposed a more general definition. According to the Lewis definition, a molecule behaves as a base when it donates a lone pair of electrons, and it behaves as an acid when it accepts a lone pair.

Student

Lone pairs? What are those again?

Alia

Lone pairs are pairs of electrons that aren't involved in bonding and belong to just one atom. The Lewis definition focuses on the action of these electron pairs rather than protons. For example, when water acts as a base with hydrochloric acid, the lone pair on the oxygen atom of water seeks out the positive charge of the proton in HCl.

Student

Can you give an example of something that's an acid under the Lewis definition but wouldn't be under the Brønsted-Lowry definition?

Alia

Carbon dioxide (CO2) is a great example. It has no hydrogen to donate, so it can't be an acid in the Brønsted-Lowry sense. However, in the Lewis definition, it behaves as an acid when it accepts a lone pair from the oxygen atom of a water molecule. This is actually how carbonic acid forms - water and carbon dioxide react, with CO2 accepting electrons from water's lone pair.

Student

That's interesting! So carbon dioxide is technically an acid, even though it doesn't have hydrogen ions to donate. That explains why carbonated drinks taste slightly acidic.

Alia

Exactly! Now, let's talk about what happens when acids and bases react with each other. The reaction between an acid and a base is called neutralization, and it typically produces water and a salt.

Student

Salt? Like table salt?

Alia

In everyday language, "salt" typically means sodium chloride (NaCl) or table salt. But in chemistry, a salt is any ionic compound formed from the reaction between an acid and a base. For example, when hydrogen chloride reacts with sodium hydroxide, it produces sodium chloride (a salt) and water.

Student

Alia

If the specific acid is HCl and the specific base is NaOH, then yes! But there are as many different salts as there are acids and bases. Are all these salts safe like table salt?

Alia

No, not all salts are safe. Sodium cyanide (NaCN), for example, is a deadly poison. But many salts are safer than the acids and bases they come from. Hydrogen chloride is very corrosive, as is sodium hydroxide. But mixing them in equal portions produces saltwater, which is far less destructive than either starting material.

Alia

Often it's calcium chloride (CaCl2) rather than sodium chloride that's used to de-ice walkways. Other common salts include potassium nitrate (also called saltpeter), which is used as a fertilizer and in gunpowder, and sodium fluoride, which helps prevent tooth decay.

That's interesting. So when an acid and base neutralize each other, does the acid always give the negative ion and the base always give the positive ion?

Alia

Yes, that's correct! In neutralization reactions, the positive ion of the resulting salt comes from the base, and the negative ion comes from the acid. The remaining hydrogen and hydroxide ions join to form water.

That's a good question! Not all neutralization reactions result in water formation. For example, the drug pseudoephedrine (a nasal decongestant) behaves as a base when exposed to hydrogen chloride. This increased water solubility means it's more readily absorbed through the digestive system, so it can be taken orally. Many pharmaceuticals that can be taken orally are bases that have been converted to salt forms for this reason.

Since new chemicals are formed during a neutralization reaction, it's definitely a chemical change. The properties of the salt and water formed are quite different from those of the original acid and base.
