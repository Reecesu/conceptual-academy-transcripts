Alia

That's a great starting point! Let's begin by talking about substances that can act as both acids and bases. A substance whose ability to behave as an acid is about the same as its ability to behave as a base is said to be amphoteric. Water is a good example of an amphoteric substance.

Student

Water can be both an acid and a base? That's surprising. I thought water was neutral.

Alia

That's a common misconception. While water is indeed neutral overall, it's also amphoteric, meaning it has the ability to react with itself. In behaving as an acid, a water molecule donates a hydrogen ion to a neighboring water molecule, which, in accepting the hydrogen ion, is behaving as a base. This reaction produces a hydroxide ion and a hydronium ion, which can react together to reform the water molecule.

Student

So water molecules can actually react with each other? I've never thought about that before.

Alia

Yes, they can! When a water molecule gains a hydrogen ion, a second water molecule must lose a hydrogen ion. So, for every one hydronium ion formed, one hydroxide ion also forms. In pure water, therefore, the total number of hydronium ions must be the same as the total number of hydroxide ions. Experiments reveal that the concentration of hydronium and hydroxide ions in pure water is extremely low—about 0.00000010 M for each, where M stands for molarity, or moles per liter. Water by itself, therefore, is a very weak acid as well as a very weak base.

Student

That's such a tiny number! So water molecules don't react with each other very much, right?

Alia

That's correct! Water molecules do react with one another, but not to any large extent. When they do react, they form hydronium and hydroxide ions. This is an important point to understand, as it serves as a basis for most of what we'll cover about acids and bases.

Student

I think I understand. So water has both hydronium and hydroxide ions, but in really small amounts.

Alia

Exactly! Now, let me tell you about something interesting regarding the concentrations of these ions. Further experiments reveal an interesting rule pertaining to the concentrations of hydronium and hydroxide ions in any solution that contains water. The concentration of hydronium ions in any aqueous solution multiplied by the concentration of the hydroxide ions in the solution always equals the constant Kw, which is a very, very small number:


concentration H₃O⁺ × concentration OH⁻ = Kw = 0.000000000000010


We can write this using scientific notation and brackets to indicate molarity:


[H₃O⁺][OH⁻] = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴

Student

Umm, that's a lot of zeros! Scientific notation helps, but I'm not sure I see why this matters.

Alia

Don't worry about all those zeros. Here's why this matters: For pure water, the value of Kw is the concentration of hydronium ions, 0.00000010 M, multiplied by the concentration of hydroxide ions, 0.00000010 M, which can be written in scientific notation as:


[1.0 × 10⁻⁷][1.0 × 10⁻⁷] = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴


The constant value of Kw is quite significant because it means that no matter what is dissolved in the water, the product of the hydronium-ion and hydroxide-ion concentrations always equals 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴. So, if the concentration of H₃O⁺ goes up, the concentration of OH⁻ must go down, and the product of the two remains 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴.

Student

So they're like a seesaw? If one goes up, the other has to go down?

Alia

That's an excellent analogy! Yes, they behave exactly like a seesaw. Let me test your understanding with a couple of questions. In pure water, the hydroxide-ion concentration is 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M. What would the hydronium-ion concentration be?

Student

Well, if they're equal in pure water... then the hydronium concentration would also be 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M, right?

Alia

Perfect! Now, what if we have a solution where the concentration of hydroxide ions is 1.0 × 10⁻³ M? What would the hydronium-ion concentration be?

Student

Let's see... I need to use that equation where they multiply to equal 10⁻¹⁴. So if OH⁻ is 10⁻³, then... um... I'm not sure how to calculate this.

Alia

That's okay. Let's work through it. We know that [H₃O⁺][OH⁻] must equal 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴. If [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻³ M, then:


[H₃O⁺] × 1.0 × 10⁻³ = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴


To solve for [H₃O⁺], we divide both sides by 1.0 × 10⁻³:


[H₃O⁺] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ ÷ 1.0 × 10⁻³ = 1.0 × 10⁻¹¹ M

Student

Oh, I see. So when the hydroxide concentration goes up (to 10⁻³), the hydronium concentration has to go down (to 10⁻¹¹) to keep their product constant at 10⁻¹⁴.

Alia

Exactly! I should also mention that Kw is an equilibrium constant. As described in previous chemistry topics, an equilibrium constant equals the concentrations of the products over reactants. You'll notice that for this reaction the reactant is water. The concentration of water in water is about 55.5 M, which is huge compared to the super tiny concentrations of the hydronium and hydroxide ion products. Because water's concentration is so huge, any changes to it are insignificant, which is why, for Kw, it can be ignored.


Also, chemical equilibrium can be temperature dependent, as is the case for water reacting with water. Strictly speaking, Kw = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴ only at room temperature, 25°C. At higher temperatures, Kw gets larger. For example, at 40°C Kw = 2.9 × 10⁻¹⁴, which means the pH of neutral water is 6.7.

Student

That's interesting! So even "neutral" water can have a different pH depending on the temperature? I didn't realize temperature would affect this.

Alia

Yes, temperature has a significant effect on many chemical equilibria. Now, let's discuss what makes a solution neutral, acidic, or basic. Any solution containing an equal number of hydronium and hydroxide ions is said to be neutral. Pure water is an example of a neutral solution—not because it contains so few hydronium or hydroxide ions, but because it contains equal numbers of these ions.

Student

Oh, so it's the balance between them that matters, not the actual amount?

Alia

That's right! A neutral solution is also obtained when equal quantities of acid and base are combined, which explains why acids and bases are said to neutralize each other.

Student

That makes sense. So what happens if that balance gets disrupted?

Alia

Great question! The balance of hydronium and hydroxide ions in a neutral solution is upset by adding either an acid or a base. Add an acid and the water will react with that acid to produce more hydronium ions. Many of these additional hydronium ions neutralize hydroxide ions, which then become fewer. The final result is that the hydronium-ion concentration is greater than the hydroxide-ion concentration. Such a solution is said to be acidic.


Add a base to water and the reverse happens. The water will react with that base to produce more hydroxide ions. Many of these additional hydroxide ions will neutralize hydronium ions, which then become fewer. The final result is that the hydronium-ion concentration is less than the hydroxide-ion concentration. Such a solution is said to be basic, or sometimes alkaline.

Student

Wait, I'm confused about something. If I add ammonia to water, it makes a basic solution. But there's no hydroxide ion in ammonia's formula, NH₃. Where do the hydroxide ions come from?

Alia

That's an excellent question! Ammonia indirectly increases the hydroxide-ion concentration by reacting with water:


NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻


This reaction raises the hydroxide-ion concentration, which has the effect of lowering the hydronium-ion concentration. With the hydroxide-ion concentration now higher than the hydronium-ion concentration, the solution is basic.

Student

That helps a lot. I've always wondered how something could be basic without having OH in its formula.

Alia

I'm glad that clarifies things! Here's another interesting fact: Above temperatures of 374°C and pressures of 218 atmospheres, water transforms into a state of matter known as a supercritical fluid, which resembles both a liquid and a gas. For a neutral solution of supercritical water, the pH equals about 2, which means that it has high concentrations of both hydronium and hydroxide ions and is highly corrosive. Research is under way to learn how supercritical water might be used to destroy toxic chemicals, such as chemical warfare agents.

Student

Wow, that's a real-world application I wouldn't have expected! Speaking of pH, I've heard that term a lot but don't really understand what it means. How does pH relate to all this?

Alia

Let's talk about the pH scale, which is used to describe acidity. The pH scale is a numeric scale used to express the acidity of a solution. Mathematically, pH is equal to the negative logarithm of the hydronium-ion concentration:


pH = -log[H₃O⁺]


Note again that brackets are used to represent molar concentrations, meaning [H₃O⁺] is read as: "the molar concentration of hydronium ions."

Student

Logarithm? That sounds like advanced math. I haven't really used logarithms before.

Alia

Don't worry. For our purposes, you just need to understand that the logarithm of 10⁻⁷ is -7. The pH is the negative of this value, which means -(-7) = 7. Hence, in a neutral solution, where the hydronium-ion concentration equals 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M, the pH is 7.

Student

Oh, so neutral solutions have a pH of 7. That part I knew! What about acidic and basic solutions?

Alia

Acidic solutions have pH values less than 7. For an acidic solution in which the hydronium-ion concentration is 1.0 × 10⁻⁴ M, for example, pH = -log(1.0 × 10⁻⁴) = 4. The more acidic a solution, the greater its hydronium-ion concentration and the lower its pH.


Basic solutions have pH values greater than 7. For a basic solution in which the hydronium-ion concentration is 1.0 × 10⁻⁸ M, for example, pH = -log(1.0 × 10⁻⁸) = 8. The more basic a solution, the smaller its hydronium-ion concentration and the higher its pH.

Student

So the pH scale goes from 0 to 14, right? With acids below 7 and bases above 7?

Alia

That's generally correct, although theoretically the pH scale is not limited to 0-14. There are extremely strong acids with negative pH values and extremely strong bases with pH values above 14, but they're uncommon in everyday life. Many common solutions like lemon juice, coffee, blood, and household ammonia have pH values between 0 and 14.

Student

How do scientists actually measure pH? Do they calculate all these hydronium concentrations?

Great question! The pH of a solution can be measured electronically using a pH meter. A rough estimate of the pH of a solution can also be obtained with pH paper, which is coated with a dye that changes color with pH. You may have seen litmus paper, which is a simple form of pH paper that turns red in acidic solutions and blue in basic solutions.

That's a perfect summary! You're making excellent connections. I've also learned that pH is important in many everyday applications. For instance, did you know that pH affects your hair?

Alia

The outer surface of hair is made of microscopic scale-like structures called cuticles that, like window shutters, are able to open and close. Alkaline solutions cause the cuticles to open up, which makes the hair "porous." Acidic solutions cause the cuticles to close down, which makes the hair "resistant." A beautician can control how long hair retains artificial coloring by modifying the pH of the hair-coloring solution. With an acidic solution, the cuticles close shut so that the dye binds only to the outside of each shaft of hair. This results in a temporary hair coloring, which may come off with the next hair washing. Using an alkaline solution permits the dye to penetrate through the cuticles into the hair for a more permanent effect.
