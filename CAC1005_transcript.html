Student

Really? I thought rain was supposed to be pure water. Why would it naturally be acidic?

Alia

That's a common misconception. Rainwater is indeed naturally acidic, and a main source of this acidity is carbon dioxide - the same gas that gives fizz to soda drinks. There are about 3,000 billion tons of CO2 in the atmosphere. Most comes from natural sources like volcanoes and decaying organic matter, but a growing amount (about 1000 billion tons) comes from human activities. In other words, about a third of the CO2 in our present atmosphere is from ourselves.

Student

Oh, I know CO2 is what we exhale, right? But how does it make rain acidic?

Alia

That's correct about exhaling CO2! Here's how it makes rain acidic: Water in the atmosphere reacts with carbon dioxide to form carbonic acid. The chemical equation for this is:
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃


Carbonic acid, as its name implies, behaves as an acid and lowers the pH of water. The CO2 in the atmosphere brings the pH of rainwater to about 5.6—noticeably below the neutral pH value of 7.

Student

Wait, so pH of 5.6 is acidic? I thought 7 was neutral... is that right?

Alia

Yes, you're exactly right! A pH of 7 is neutral. Anything below 7 is acidic, and anything above 7 is basic (or alkaline). Because of local fluctuations, the normal pH of rainwater varies between 5 and 7. This natural acidity of rainwater may accelerate the erosion of land and, under certain circumstances, can lead to the formation of underground caves.

Student

So even normal rain is a bit acidic. Then what exactly is "acid rain" that we hear about in the news?

Alia

Good question! By convention, acid rain is a term used for rain having a pH lower than 5. Acid rain is created when airborne pollutants, such as sulfur dioxide, are absorbed by atmospheric moisture. Sulfur dioxide is readily converted to sulfur trioxide, which reacts with water to form sulfuric acid. The chemical equations are:
SO₂ + ½O₂ → SO₃
SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄

Student

That sounds scary. Sulfuric acid seems much stronger than the carbonic acid you mentioned earlier. Where does all this sulfur dioxide come from?

Alia

You're right to be concerned. Most SO₂ is released into the atmosphere by the combustion of sulfur-containing coal and oil. And you're also correct that sulfuric acid is much stronger than carbonic acid. As a result, rain laced with sulfuric acid and therefore higher H₃O⁺ concentrations eventually corrodes metal, paint, and other exposed substances. This damage has cost billions of dollars.

Student

I think I've seen old buildings and statues that look eroded. Is that from acid rain?

Alia

Yes, that's exactly right! In fact, there are some striking examples of this. In New York City's Central Park, there's an obelisk that shows significant deterioration due to acid rain over time. The cost to the environment is also high. Many rivers and lakes receiving acid rain become less capable of sustaining life. Much vegetation that receives acid rain doesn't survive. This is particularly evident in heavily industrialized regions.

Student

That's depressing. Are we making any progress on reducing this problem?

Alia

There is some good news! Acid rain remains a serious problem in many regions of the world, but significant progress has been made toward fixing the problem. In the United States, for example, sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide emissions have been reduced by nearly half since 1980.

Student

That's encouraging at least. So what makes these acid solutions so damaging? Is it just the acidity itself?

Alia

Let's clarify what makes these solutions corrosive. When sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄, is added to water, it readily forms hydronium ions. These hydronium ions are responsible for the corrosive action. This is why acid rain can be so damaging to structures, vegetation, and aquatic life.

Student

So the acid rain falls everywhere. Does it affect all places the same way? I've visited lakes in different parts of the country, and some seem healthier than others.

Alia

That's a perceptive observation! The environmental impact of acid rain actually depends on local geology. In certain regions, such as the midwestern United States, the ground contains significant quantities of the alkaline compound calcium carbonate (limestone), deposited when these lands were submerged under oceans, as has occurred several times over the past 500 million years. Acid rain pouring into these regions is often neutralized by the calcium carbonate before any damage is done.

Student

Oh, that makes sense! So limestone can neutralize the acid. What about other areas that don't have limestone?

Alia

In the northeastern United States and many other regions, the ground contains very little calcium carbonate and is composed primarily of chemically less reactive materials, such as granite. In these regions, the effect of acid rain on lakes and rivers accumulates. Lakes and rivers lined with inert materials are not protected against the acidity.

Student

Is there anything we can do to help those vulnerable lakes?

Alia

Yes, there are solutions being implemented. One demonstrated solution to this problem is to raise the pH of acidified lakes and rivers by adding calcium carbonate—a process known as liming. However, the cost of transporting the calcium carbonate, coupled with the need to monitor treated water systems closely, limits liming to only a small fraction of the vast number of water systems already affected. Furthermore, as acid rain continues to pour into these regions, the need to lime continues.

Student

So liming is just treating the symptom, not the cause. What would be a better long-term solution?

Alia

Exactly right! A longer-term solution to acid rain is to prevent most of the generated sulfur dioxide and other pollutants from entering the atmosphere in the first place. Toward this end, smokestacks have been designed or retrofitted to minimize the quantities of pollutants released. Although costly, the positive effects of these adjustments have been demonstrated.

Student

That makes sense. So are we doing that now? Are emissions going down?

Alia

Yes, there has been progress. Due in great part to public attention and economics, over the past few decades, developed nations have markedly decreased their SO₂ emissions, primarily by capturing the SO₂ rather than releasing it. The world leaders in SO₂ emissions are now collectively with developing nations such as India.

Student

I'm starting to better understand this whole issue. Just to make sure I've got it - which lakes would be most protected against acid rain?

Alia

That's a good question to check your understanding. Lakes that have a floor consisting of basic minerals, such as limestone, are more resistant to acid rain, because the chemicals of the limestone (mostly calcium carbonate, CaCO₃) neutralize any incoming acid.

Student

Thanks! That makes sense. But it sounds like we need even more comprehensive solutions, right?

Alia

You're right. An ultimate long-term solution would be a shift from fossil fuels to cleaner energy sources, such as nuclear and solar energy. It's worth noting that if the world is able to lower its SO₂ emissions, even with a growing population and greater economic activity, we might also wonder if we can lower our CO₂ emissions. For perspective, we release over 40,000 million tons of CO₂ each year, which is about 400 times more than the approximately 100 million tons of SO₂ we release annually. Carbon dioxide is also less chemically reactive than SO₂, which means that capturing it at the source is more difficult. Lowering our CO₂ emissions is many times more the challenge.
