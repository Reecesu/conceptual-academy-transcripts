A fuel cell is a device that converts the energy of a fuel directly into electrical energy. Unlike traditional power generation methods, fuel cells are quite efficient. Would you like me to explain how a basic fuel cell works?

Student

Yes, please. I've heard of fuel cells but don't really understand how they work. Do they burn fuel like car engines?

Alia

That's a common misconception! Unlike engines that burn fuel, fuel cells generate electricity through chemical reactions without combustion. Let me explain how a hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell works as an example.


A hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell has two compartments - one for hydrogen fuel and one for oxygen - separated by porous electrodes. Hydrogen is oxidized when it contacts hydroxide ions at the hydrogen-facing electrode, which we call the anode. The electrons from this oxidation flow through an external circuit, providing electric power before meeting up with oxygen at the oxygen-facing electrode, called the cathode.


At the cathode, oxygen picks up these electrons (it gets reduced) and reacts with water to form hydroxide ions. These hydroxide ions then migrate across the porous electrodes and through an ionic paste of potassium hydroxide (KOH) to join with hydrogen at the anode, completing the circuit.

Student

That sounds complicated. So electrons are flowing from one side to the other... is that what creates the electricity?

Alia

Exactly! You've identified the key point. The flow of electrons through the external circuit is what we harness as electricity. Think of it like a waterfall - the electrons are "falling" from the hydrogen side to the oxygen side, and we're capturing that energy along the way.


Let me add that during this process, when hydrogen and hydroxide ions react, they produce energetic water molecules in the form of steam. This steam can be used for heating or even for generating additional electricity in a steam turbine. And when this steam condenses, it forms pure water that's actually suitable for drinking.

Student

Wait, so fuel cells produce drinking water as a byproduct? That's actually pretty cool! Are there real-world applications for these fuel cells?

Alia

Yes, it is cool! And there are absolutely real-world applications. For instance, the International Space Station uses hydrogen-oxygen fuel cells to meet its electricity needs, and the cells produce drinking water for the astronauts.


Back here on Earth, researchers have been developing fuel cells for transportation. There are experimental fuel-cell buses already operating in many cities. These vehicles produce very few pollutants and can run much more efficiently than vehicles that burn fossil fuels. In fact, these buses emit mostly just water vapor from their tailpipes!

Student

That sounds like a perfect solution for clean energy. Why aren't we using fuel cells everywhere if they're so efficient and clean?

Alia

That's an insightful question. While fuel cells offer many advantages, there are some practical challenges, particularly with hydrogen fuel cells. Perhaps the biggest issue is that hydrogen gas (H₂) is not naturally abundant in its pure form. It must be manufactured, which requires energy.


For example, we can produce hydrogen gas through electrolysis of water, which we'll discuss more in a later section. But this process requires electrical energy. If that electricity comes from a pollution-producing power plant, we lose many of the environmental benefits of the fuel cell.

Student

Oh, I see. So it's kind of like robbing Peter to pay Paul if we're using dirty energy to make clean energy?

Alia

That's a great way to put it! There is indeed that potential contradiction. However, there are ways around this problem. For instance, we could use photovoltaic cells (solar panels) to produce the electricity needed for electrolysis. But today's photovoltaic cells are relatively expensive to produce, and they only work when the Sun is shining.


Another approach is producing hydrogen from fossil fuels. The most efficient and environmentally friendly fossil fuel is methane (CH₄), also known as natural gas, which is now in great supply because of fracking techniques (which we'll discuss in Chapter 17). When heated with steam in the presence of a catalyst, methane can be converted to hydrogen and carbon dioxide through this reaction:


CH₄ (g) + 2 H₂O (g) → 4 H₂ (g) + CO₂ (g)
methane + steam → hydrogen + carbon dioxide

Student

I'm confused now. If we're still producing carbon dioxide, how is that helping with climate change? Isn't that what we're trying to avoid?

Alia

You're raising an excellent point. You're right that this process still produces carbon dioxide, which is a greenhouse gas. However, the advantage is that fuel cells can utilize the produced hydrogen more efficiently than traditional combustion methods would use the original methane. So while not perfect, it's still an improvement in terms of overall emissions and efficiency.


There are also industrial-scale fuel cells specifically designed to work with this process. One example is the molten carbonate fuel cell (MCFC). These fuel cells have been engineered to work in tandem with the on-site production of hydrogen from methane.

Student

How do these molten carbonate fuel cells work? They sound complicated.

Alia

In a molten carbonate fuel cell, hydrogen is oxidized at the anode as it reacts with carbonate ions to produce carbon dioxide, water, and electrons. Those electrons pass through an external circuit, providing electric power. Meanwhile, the carbon dioxide is piped to the cathode where electrons are received by oxygen, which reacts with the carbon dioxide to form carbonate ions. The carbonate ions migrate through the electrolyte back to the anode for subsequent reactions.


What's neat about MCFCs is that they can be scaled to create power plants ranging from just a few kilowatts to as many as 50 megawatts. For comparison, a typical coal-fired power plant is about 500 megawatts.

Student

So these are like smaller power plants? How is that useful?

Alia

Great question! Unlike a centralized coal-fired power plant that produces electricity for an entire region, fuel cell power plants are suitable for individual buildings, such as hospitals, factories, or skyscrapers. They run quietly, producing few exhausts, and can be installed in the basement or just outside the building they serve.


Another advantage is that MCFCs also produce thermal energy, which can be used to heat water and ventilation systems. This dual-purpose nature boosts their overall efficiency.

Student

I'm starting to see why people are excited about fuel cells. I'm curious though - could we use fuel cells in cars? I know electric cars are becoming popular.

Alia

That's exactly what many researchers are working toward! The electricity from a fuel cell can be used to charge the batteries of an electric vehicle. But there's also research on placing fuel cells directly on board vehicles to power them.


There are challenges though. For hydrogen fuel cells in cars, storage is a significant issue. Only about 3 kilograms of hydrogen are needed to drive a car some 500 kilometers, but the volume of this much hydrogen is about 36,000 liters - which is huge! Compressing the gas into smaller volumes (as is done in fuel cell buses) or chilling it to the liquid phase requires energy, which reduces efficiency.

Student

That seems like a major obstacle. Is there any way around it?

Alia

Yes, researchers are exploring solutions! One promising approach involves using porous materials, such as carbon nanofibers, that can hold large amounts of hydrogen on their surfaces - essentially acting like hydrogen "sponges." These materials can be controlled by temperature - the warmer they get, the more hydrogen is released, allowing hydrogen to be "squeezed out" on demand.


Another alternative is using liquid hydrocarbons such as methanol (CH₃OH) in fuel cells. With liquid fuel, miniature cells can be built to power portable electronic devices like smartphones and tablets. Such devices could potentially operate for long periods on a single ampoule of liquid methanol that you could purchase at a local supermarket.

Student

That all sounds promising! But I'm still wondering - if fuel cells are so great, why do we still use batteries for most devices? What's the difference?

Alia

That's a fantastic question that gets at a key distinction. As long as fuel is available, a fuel cell can supply electrical energy indefinitely. Regular batteries, like dry-cell or alkaline batteries, can't do this.

Student

Why not? What makes fuel cells different from batteries in that way?

Alia

The difference is in how they generate electricity. Dry-cell and alkaline batteries generate electricity as the chemical reactants they contain are reduced and oxidized. Once these reactants are consumed, the battery can no longer generate electricity.


In contrast, fuel cells are continuously supplied with reactants from an external source. As long as you keep supplying hydrogen and oxygen to a hydrogen fuel cell, for example, it will keep producing electricity. It's similar to how a car will keep running as long as you keep refilling the gas tank, whereas a battery is more like a closed system with a limited supply of "fuel" built in.
