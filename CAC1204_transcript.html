Alia

Great question! Alcohols are organic molecules in which a hydroxyl group is bonded to a saturated carbon. The hydroxyl group consists of an oxygen bonded to a hydrogen. Because of the polarity of the oxygen-hydrogen bond, low-formula-mass alcohols are often soluble in water, which is itself very polar.

Student

So the hydroxyl group is just an oxygen and hydrogen together? And that's what makes it an alcohol?

Alia

That's correct, but with an important detail - the hydroxyl group must be attached specifically to a saturated carbon atom in the molecule. This hydroxyl group is written as -OH. Would you like to see some examples of common simple alcohols?

Student

Yes, that would help a lot. I learn better with examples.

Alia

Certainly! Some common simple alcohols include methanol (CH₃OH), ethanol (C₂H₅OH), 1-propanol (C₃H₇OH), and 2-propanol (C₃H₇OH). Each has different properties, including different melting and boiling points. For instance, methanol has a melting point of -98°C and a boiling point of 65°C, while ethanol has a melting point of -114°C and a boiling point of 78°C.

Student

Wait, 1-propanol and 2-propanol have the same formula? How can that be?

Alia

Excellent observation! 1-propanol and 2-propanol are isomers - they have the same chemical formula (C₃H₇OH) but different structures. In 1-propanol, the hydroxyl group is attached to an end carbon in the chain, while in 2-propanol, it's attached to the middle carbon. This difference in structure gives them slightly different properties, such as 2-propanol having a lower boiling point (82°C) compared to 1-propanol (97°C).

Student

Oh, I think I get it. So let's start with methanol - is that something people drink?

Alia

That's a critical question that highlights an important safety concern. Methanol, also called wood alcohol because it can be obtained from wood, should NEVER be ingested. In the body, methanol is metabolized to formaldehyde, H₂CO, and formic acid, HCO₂H. Formaldehyde is harmful to the eyes, can lead to blindness, and was once used to preserve dead biological specimens. Formic acid, the active ingredient in an ant bite, can lower the pH of the blood to dangerous levels. Ingesting only about 15 milliliters (about 3 tablespoons) of methanol may lead to blindness, and about 30 milliliters can cause death.

Student

Wow, that's scary! I had no idea it was so dangerous. So what is methanol used for if it's not for drinking?

Alia

Billions of pounds of methanol are produced annually in the United States. Most of it is used for making formaldehyde and acetic acid, which are important starting materials in the production of plastics. In addition, methanol is used as a solvent, an octane booster, and an anti-icing agent in gasoline.

Student

And ethanol is the one in alcoholic drinks, right? That seems safer.

Alia

Yes, ethanol, C₂H₅OH, is indeed the "alcohol" in alcoholic beverages, and it's one of the oldest chemicals manufactured by humans. While ethanol is less toxic than methanol, it's worth noting that the body metabolizes ethanol into acetaldehyde, CH₃COH. Acetaldehyde won't cause blindness, but it does lead to some painful side effects, which people who drink too much experience as part of their hangover.

Student

That explains hangovers! So how do they make ethanol for drinks?

Alia

Ethanol is prepared by feeding the sugars of various plants to certain yeasts, which produce ethanol through a biological process known as fermentation. Ethanol is also widely used as an industrial solvent. For many years, ethanol intended for industrial use was made by fermentation, but today industrial-grade ethanol is more cheaply manufactured from petroleum by-products such as ethene.

Student

I've heard that different alcoholic drinks have different amounts of alcohol. How does that work?

Alia

The liquid produced by fermentation has an ethanol concentration no greater than about 12 percent, because at this concentration, the yeast cells begin to die. This is why most wines have an alcohol content of about 12 percent—they are produced solely by fermentation. To attain the higher ethanol concentrations found in such "hard" alcoholic beverages as gin and vodka, the fermented liquid must be distilled.


In the United States, the ethanol content of distilled alcoholic beverages is measured as proof, which is twice the percentage of ethanol. An 86-proof whiskey, for example, is 43 percent ethanol by volume. The term proof evolved from a crude method once employed to test alcohol content. Gunpowder was wetted with a beverage of suspect alcohol content. If the beverage was primarily water, the powder would not ignite. If the beverage contained a significant amount of ethanol, the powder would burn, thus providing "proof" of the beverage's worth.

Student

That's a cool story about the proof test! Are there any other common alcohols I might know about?

Alia

Yes, a third well-known alcohol is isopropyl alcohol, also called 2-propanol. This is the rubbing alcohol you buy at the drugstore. Although 2-propanol has a relatively high boiling point, it evaporates readily, leading to a pronounced cooling effect when it is applied to skin—an effect once used to reduce fevers. However, isopropyl alcohol is very toxic if ingested. In place of isopropyl alcohol, washcloths wetted with cold water are also quite effective in reducing fever, and they are far safer. You are probably most familiar with the use of isopropyl alcohol as a topical disinfectant.

Student

Oh yeah, I've definitely used that to clean cuts and scrapes. So alcohols can be classified in different ways, right?

Alia

That's right! Alcohols can be classified as being primary, secondary, or tertiary. In a primary alcohol, the hydroxyl group is attached to the terminal carbon of a carbon chain. An example is ethanol. In a secondary alcohol, the hydroxyl group is attached to a central carbon, as seen with 2-propanol. For a tertiary alcohol, the hydroxyl group is attached to a carbon that is bonded to three other carbon atoms, as occurs in 2-methyl-2-propanol.

Student

I'm trying to visualize 2-methyl-2-propanol. Would that be like a central carbon with the OH group, and then three carbons attached to it, one of which is a methyl group?

Alia

You're on the right track, but let me clarify. In 2-methyl-2-propanol, we have a central carbon that has the hydroxyl (-OH) group attached to it. This central carbon also has three other carbon atoms attached to it. One of these carbons is from the methyl group (-CH₃) that gives the molecule part of its name. The other two carbons are part of the propanol backbone. So the central carbon has four attachments: one OH group and three carbon atoms.

Student

That makes more sense, thanks. So we've talked about alcohols. What are phenols?

Alia

Phenols contain a phenolic group, which consists of a hydroxyl group bonded to a benzene ring. Because of the presence of the benzene ring, the hydrogen of the hydroxyl group is readily lost in an acid-base reaction, which makes the phenolic group mildly acidic.

Student

Wait, I thought -OH groups were basic? Like in sodium hydroxide (NaOH)? Why would phenols be acidic?

Alia

That's an excellent question that shows you're making connections! The hydroxide ion (OH⁻) in sodium hydroxide is indeed basic. However, in a phenol, the hydroxyl group behaves differently because it's attached to a benzene ring.


The reason for this acidity is related to how well the molecule can accommodate a negative charge. How readily an acid donates a hydrogen ion is a function of how well the acid is able to accommodate the resulting negative charge it gains after donating the hydrogen ion. After a phenol donates the hydrogen ion, it becomes a negatively charged phenoxide ion. The negative charge of the phenoxide ion, however, is not restricted to the oxygen atom.


Recall that the electrons of the benzene ring are able to migrate around the ring. In a similar manner, the electrons responsible for the negative charge of the phenoxide ion are also able to migrate around the ring. Just as it is easy for several people to hold a hot potato by quickly passing it around, it is easy for the phenoxide ion to hold the negative charge because the charge gets passed around. Because the negative charge of the ion is so nicely accommodated, the phenolic group is more acidic than it would be otherwise.

Student

That makes sense - the benzene ring helps stabilize the negative charge by spreading it out. So if phenols are mildly acidic, are regular alcohols acidic too?

Alia

Great question! Alcohols are less acidic than phenols. An alcohol does not contain a benzene ring adjacent to the hydroxyl group. If the alcohol were to donate the hydroxyl hydrogen, the result would be a negative charge on the oxygen. Without an adjacent benzene ring, this negative charge has nowhere to go. As a result, an alcohol behaves only as a very weak acid, much like water.

Student

Are phenols common compounds? What are they used for?

Alia

Yes, phenols are quite common. The simplest phenol is actually called phenol. In 1867, Joseph Lister discovered the antiseptic value of phenol, which, when applied to surgical instruments and incisions, greatly increased surgery survival rates. Phenol was the first purposefully used antibacterial solution, or antiseptic.


However, phenol damages healthy tissue, so a number of milder phenols have since been introduced. The phenol 4-hexylresorcinol, for example, is commonly used in throat lozenges and mouthwashes. This compound has even greater antiseptic properties than phenol, yet it does not damage tissue. Listerine® brand mouthwash (named after Joseph Lister) contains the antiseptic phenols thymol and methyl salicylate.

Student

Oh, I never knew that's where Listerine got its name! So we've covered alcohols and phenols. What about ethers? I've heard of ether being used in old-time surgery.

Alia

Ethers are organic compounds structurally related to alcohols. The oxygen atom in an ether group, however, is bonded not to a carbon and a hydrogen, but rather to two carbons.


For example, ethanol and dimethyl ether have the same chemical formula, C₂H₆O, but their physical properties are vastly different. Whereas ethanol is a liquid at room temperature (boiling point 78°C) and mixes quite well with water, dimethyl ether is a gas at room temperature (boiling point –25°C) and is much less soluble in water.

Student

So the same atoms can be arranged differently and give completely different properties? That's actually pretty cool.

Alia

Exactly! That's one of the fascinating aspects of chemistry. The oxygen in an alcohol, such as ethanol, is bonded to one carbon atom and one hydrogen atom. The oxygen in an ether, such as dimethyl ether, is bonded to two carbon atoms. Because of this difference, alcohols and ethers of similar molecular mass have vastly different physical properties.

Student

Why aren't ethers soluble in water if they have oxygen in them? I thought oxygen made things more water-soluble.

Alia

That's a thoughtful question! Ethers are not very soluble in water because, without the hydroxyl group, they are unable to form strong hydrogen bonds with water. Furthermore, without the polar hydroxyl group, the molecular attractions among ether molecules are relatively weak. As a result, little energy is required to separate ether molecules from one another. This is why low-formula-mass ethers have relatively low boiling points and evaporate so readily.

Student

I think I remember hearing about ether being used as an anesthetic in old movies. Is that true?

Alia

Yes, you're absolutely right! Diethyl ether was one of the first general anesthetics. The anesthetic properties of this compound were discovered in the early 1800s, and its use revolutionized the practice of surgery.


Because of its high volatility at room temperature, inhaled diethyl ether rapidly enters the bloodstream. Because this ether has low solubility in water and high volatility, it quickly leaves the bloodstream once introduced. Because of these physical properties, a surgical patient can be brought into and out of general anesthesia (a state of unconsciousness) simply by regulating the gases breathed. Modern-day gaseous anesthetics have fewer side effects than diethyl ether, but they operate on the same principle.

That's wonderful to hear! Chemistry is indeed all around us, from the medicines we take to the foods we eat to the materials we use daily. Understanding the structure and properties of molecules like alcohols, phenols, and ethers helps us understand how they function in our world.
