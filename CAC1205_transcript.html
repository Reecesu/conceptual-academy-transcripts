Sure, I guess. I don't know anything about amines though. What are they exactly?

Alia

Amines are organic compounds that contain what we call the amine group. This amine group consists of a nitrogen atom bonded to one, two, or three saturated carbons. Unlike some other organic compounds, amines are typically less soluble in water than alcohols. This is because the nitrogen-hydrogen bond is not as polar as the oxygen-hydrogen bond in alcohols.

Student

Wait, what does "polar" mean again? I remember hearing that term but I'm not sure how it relates to solubility.

Alia

Great question! A polar bond means that electrons are not shared equally between atoms, creating a slight positive charge on one atom and a slight negative charge on the other. The more polar a bond is, the more it can interact with water molecules, which increases solubility. The nitrogen-hydrogen bond in amines is less polar than the oxygen-hydrogen bond in alcohols, which is why amines don't dissolve in water as readily as alcohols do.

Student

Oh, that makes sense. So less polar means less soluble in water?

Alia

That's right! And this lower polarity, along with weaker hydrogen bonding in amines, also means their boiling points are typically lower than those of alcohols with similar formula mass.

Student

So amines have nitrogen in them... is that like ammonia? I remember that smell from cleaning products.

Alia

You're making an excellent connection! Ammonia does contain nitrogen, and many simple amines actually have distinctive odors similar to ammonia but often much more offensive. In fact, one of the most notable physical properties of many low-formula-mass amines is their offensive odor.

Student

Offensive how? Like, bad smelling?

Alia

Exactly. There are two appropriately named amines called putrescine and cadaverine, which are responsible, in part, for the odor of decaying flesh.

Student

Eww! That's gross but kind of interesting. So these chemicals are what make dead things smell bad?

Alia

Yes, they contribute significantly to those unpleasant odors. These low-formula-mass amines tend to have particularly offensive smells. But beyond their odor, amines have another important chemical property - they're typically alkaline.

Student

Alkaline? You mean like basic, the opposite of acidic?

Alia

That's correct! Amines are alkaline because the nitrogen atom readily accepts a hydrogen ion. For example, ethylamine acts as a weak base and accepts a hydrogen ion from water to become the ethyl ammonium ion. This reaction generates a small amount of hydroxide ions, which slightly increases the pH of the solution.

Student

So is that why they're called alkaloids? Because they're alkaline?

Alia

You're on the right track! A class of alkaline amines found in nature are indeed called alkaloids. Because many alkaloids have medicinal or other biological effects, there is great interest in isolating these compounds from the plants or marine organisms that contain them.

Student

Wait, are these in medicine? Like prescription drugs?

Alia

Yes, many medications are derived from or inspired by alkaloids. In fact, an alkaloid, such as caffeine, reacts with an acid to form a salt that is usually quite soluble in water. This is in contrast to the unionized form of the alkaloid, known as a free base, which is typically insoluble in water.

Student

Caffeine is an alkaloid? I drink coffee every morning! I had no idea it was related to what we're talking about.

Alia

That's a perfect real-world connection! Caffeine is indeed an alkaloid. And speaking of pharmaceuticals, nearly all pharmaceuticals that can be administered orally contain the nitrogen heteroatom in the water-soluble salt form.

Student

So if caffeine is an alkaloid, and alkaloids react with acids... is that why some soft drinks have phosphoric acid listed in the ingredients?

Alia

Excellent observation! Yes, most caffeinated soft drinks also contain phosphoric acid because it reacts with the caffeine to form the caffeine-phosphoric acid salt, which is much more soluble in cold water than the naturally occurring tannin salt. Interestingly, it also adds a desirable tingle to the tongue.

Student

That's actually pretty cool. I never thought about the chemistry behind my soda before! So what are these tannin things you mentioned?

Alia

Most alkaloids exist in nature not in their free-base form, but rather as the salts of naturally occurring acids known as tannins. The alkaloid salts of these acids are usually much more soluble in hot water than in cold water. The caffeine in coffee and tea exists in the form of the tannin salt, which is why coffee and tea are more effectively brewed in hot water.

Student

Is that why my mom always uses hot water for tea, not cold? I never knew there was a chemical reason for that.

Alia

Exactly! Your mother is using chemistry principles whether she realizes it or not. Hot water dissolves those tannin salts much more effectively. Tannins are also responsible for the stains caused by these beverages.

Student

Oh! Is that why coffee stains my teeth and my mugs?

Alia

Precisely! Tannins are responsible for the brown stains in coffee mugs or on a coffee drinker's teeth. Because tannins are acidic, they can be readily removed with an alkaline cleanser such as baking soda.

That's the beauty of chemistry - it's all around us in our daily lives! I'm glad you're seeing these connections. To summarize what we've covered about amines and alkaloids: amines contain a nitrogen atom bonded to carbon atoms, they're typically alkaline, many have distinctive odors, and when they occur in nature as alkaloids, they form salts with acids like tannins that affect their solubility in water.
