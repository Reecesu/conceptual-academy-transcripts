Alia

A nucleic acid is a polymer, which means it's made up of repeating smaller units. The monomer units of the nucleic acid polymer are called nucleotides. There are two major classes of nucleic acids: deoxyribonucleic acids, or simply DNA, and ribonucleic acids, also known as RNA.

Student

I've heard of DNA and RNA before, but I'm not sure what they actually do. Are they both found in the same place in our cells?

Alia

Good question! DNA is found primarily within the cell's nucleus, where it holds all the organism's genetic information. RNA, on the other hand, is synthesized from DNA and transports this genetic information to structures outside the nucleus, where the proteins are built from a supply of individual amino acids.

Student

So DNA stays in the nucleus like a master copy, and RNA takes the instructions elsewhere?

Alia

Precisely! Let me show you more about what nucleotides look like. Each nucleotide consists of three components: a nitrogenous base, a ribose sugar, and a phosphate group. "Nitrogenous" simply means it contains nitrogen atoms, and ribose is a specific sugar that contains five carbon atoms.

Student

That's a lot of parts. What makes DNA different from RNA again?

Alia

The two major classes of nucleic acids are distinguished from each other by the type of ribose in the nucleotide monomers. In DNA nucleotides, there's an oxygen atom missing on one of the carbon atoms in the ribose ring - that's why it's called "deoxy" ribonucleic acid. DNA is the primary source of genetic information in plants and animals and is found in the cell nucleus as well as in certain organelles known as mitochondria.


In RNA nucleotides, the ribose has an oxygen atom on each carbon atom of the ribose. These RNA polymers are synthesized in the nucleus and then travel outside the nucleus to the cytoplasm, where they direct the synthesis of proteins.

Another excellent question. There are four types of nucleotides in a DNA polymer. These four nucleotides differ from one another in the nitrogenous base they contain, which may be adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), or thymine (T).


There are also four types of nucleotides used to build RNA polymers. The RNA nucleotides contain the same nitrogenous bases found in DNA nucleotides except for thymine. Instead of thymine, RNA nucleotides contain the nitrogenous base uracil (U), whose structure is only slightly different from that of thymine.

That's exactly right! Those are the two main structural differences between DNA and RNA nucleotides. All DNA nucleotides lack an oxygen atom on the ribose, and DNA uses thymine while RNA uses uracil.

That's a great transition to the history of how we came to understand DNA. Genetics is the study of how living bodies come into being. Our modern understanding of genetics began in the 1850s in an abbey garden, where a monk named Gregor Mendel documented how varieties of sweet peas could pass traits, such as flower color, from one generation to the next.

Alia

Yes! Science often progresses in unexpected ways. From Mendel's work arose the idea that heritable traits are passed from parents to offspring in discrete units called genes. In the early 1900s, researchers correlated Mendel's heritable genes to cellular microstructures known as chromosomes, which are elongated bundles of DNA and protein that form whenever a cell is getting ready to divide. Each gene, it was found, resides at a specific location on a particular chromosome. Offspring inherit genes by receiving replicates of their parents' chromosomes.

That's a common misconception. Chromosomes are indeed made of both DNA and proteins. In fact, up until the 1940s, it was not known whether the DNA portion or the protein portion of the chromosomes was the carrier of genetic information. Most investigators were inclined to believe that proteins, with their great diversity, were the carriers.

Student

Alia

In the 1940s, DNA was found to be a polymer containing adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine. Along the DNA chain, these bases could be sorted in any order. This potential variability in base sequencing opened up the possibility that DNA was the carrier of genetic information.


The real breakthrough came in 1953, when James Watson, an American biologist, and Francis Crick, a British biophysicist, together deduced that DNA occurs as two separate strands of nucleotides coiled around each other in a double helix. The strands are held together by hydrogen bonding between opposing nitrogenous bases.

Student

Oh, the double helix! I've seen pictures of that. It kind of looks like a twisted ladder, right?

Alia

That's a perfect description! You can think of it like a ladder made of rope sides and rigid wooden rungs that has been twisted to create a double helix. The ropes are the equivalent of DNA's two sugar–phosphate backbones, and the rungs represent pairs of nitrogenous bases.

Student

So the sides of the ladder are made of sugar and phosphate, and the rungs are those bases - A, T, G, and C. Are the bases just randomly paired up?

Alia

Not at all - and that's what was most critical about Watson and Crick's model. Hydrogen bonding occurs only between specific bases—guanine pairs only with cytosine and adenine pairs only with thymine. This means that if you know the sequence of one strand, you can automatically deduce the sequence of the second strand, also known as the complementary strand.

Student

Alia

Exactly! If the first strand contains the sequence CTGA, the complementary strand must contain the sequence GACT. The complementary pairing is G with C, and A with T.

Alia

That's a crucial question. In living tissue, cells divide to create duplicates of themselves. In a maturing organism, cells divide frequently to provide growth. In a mature organism, cells divide at a rate sufficient for the replacement of those that die. Each time a cell divides, genetic information must be preserved.


In a process called replication, DNA strands are duplicated so that each newly formed cell receives a copy. Replication also allows copies of DNA to pass from parent to offspring.

Alia

Watson and Crick proposed that DNA replication begins with the unraveling of the double helix. Each single strand then serves as a template for the synthesis of its complementary strand. Free nucleotides are coupled to the single strand of DNA according to the rules of base pairing: guanine + cytosine, adenine + thymine. One double helix thus turns into two. As a cell divides, one of the two new strands is segregated in each of the two newly formed cells.

Alia

That's an apt comparison! For their elucidation of DNA's secondary structure and function, Watson and Crick, along with biophysicist Maurice Wilkins, received the 1962 Nobel Prize in Physiology and Medicine. Subsequent research soon led to an understanding of how the nucleotide sequences in DNA translate into the synthesis of proteins.

Yes, and that's an important point to bring up. Franklin did not share in the 1962 Nobel Prize because it is not awarded posthumously - she had passed away by then.

In modern terms, a gene is a particular sequence of nucleotides along the DNA strand. Each gene codes for the synthesis of one or more proteins in an organism. Organisms, of course, need enormous numbers of genes to produce all the proteins they need.

The other 80 percent of the nucleotides in a DNA strand appear to serve primarily as spacers, their main job being to separate genes on the DNA molecule.
