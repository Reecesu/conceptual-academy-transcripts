This uneven distribution of sodium ions creates an electric potential of around -70 millivolts across the cell membrane. A nerve impulse is essentially a reversal in this electric potential that travels down the length of the neuron to what we call the synaptic terminals. During this impulse, the sodium ions flush back into the neuron, temporarily changing that voltage.

If the receptor sites are on a muscle or an organ, then the binding of the neurotransmitter may trigger a bodily response, such as muscle contraction or the release of hormones.

Serotonin is the neurotransmitter used by the brain to block unneeded nerve impulses. So that we can make sense of the world, the frontal lobes of the brain selectively block out a multitude of signals coming from the lower brain and from the peripheral nervous system.
