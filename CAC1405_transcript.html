Alia

Amphetamines open these reuptake channels so that neurotransmitters, such as norepinephrine and dopamine, pass freely in and out of the synapse. This increases their concentration in the synapse, which provides a feeling of being "high."


Cocaine acts a bit differently. It binds specifically to the dopamine reuptake protein, blocking the reuptake of this neurotransmitter. As a result, dopamine accumulates in the synapse, leading to feelings of euphoria.

Student

So how do alcohol and these benzodiazepines actually affect the brain? Do they work the same way?

Alia

Alcohol and benzodiazepines exert their depressant effect primarily by enhancing the action of a neurotransmitter called GABA. GABA keeps electric impulses from passing through a neuron by binding to a receptor site on a channel that penetrates the cell membrane of the neuron.


When GABA binds to the receptor site, the channel opens, allowing chloride ions to migrate into the neuron. The resulting negative charge buildup in the neuron maintains the negative electric potential across the cell membrane. This inhibits a reversal to a positive potential and prevents an impulse from traveling along the neuron.


Ethanol mimics the effect of GABA by binding to GABA receptor sites. This allows chloride ions to enter the neuron. The effect of alcohol is dose-dependent, which means the greater the amount consumed, the greater the effect.

That's exactly right. At small concentrations, few chloride ions are permitted into the neuron; these low concentrations of ions decrease inhibitions, alter judgment, and impair muscle control. As the person continues to drink and the chloride ion concentration inside the neuron rises, both reflexes and consciousness diminish, eventually to the point of coma and then death.


Recall from our discussion of cocaine and amphetamines that the body responds to the long-term abuse of these stimulants by creating more depressant receptor sites. Likewise, the body recognizes the excessive inhibitory actions produced by alcohol and tries to recover by increasing the number of synaptic receptor sites that lead to nerve excitation.


In this way, an individual can develop a tolerance for alcohol. To receive the same inhibitory effect, the drinker is forced to drink more, which induces the body to create even more excitable synaptic receptor sites. Eventually, an excess of these excitatory receptor sites leads to perpetual body tremors, which can be subdued either by more drinking or, with greater difficulty, by a long-term cessation of alcohol consumption.

Chloride ions inside the neuron help to maintain the negative electric potential. This inhibits the neuron from being able to conduct an impulse. Chloride ions, therefore, decrease the activity of a neuron.
