Great question! The pain relievers you can buy at the store are called analgesics. Analgesics are a class of drugs that help us to tolerate pain without abolishing nerve sensations.


Over-the-counter analgesics, such as aspirin, ibuprofen, and acetaminophen, work by inhibiting the formation of prostaglandins. Prostaglandins are biochemicals the body quickly synthesizes to generate pain signals.

Alia

That's a good way to think about them! Prostaglandins are chemicals produced by your body in response to injury. The starting material for all prostaglandins is arachidonic acid, which is found in the membranes of all cells. Arachidonic acid is transformed to prostaglandins with the help of an enzyme.


There are a variety of prostaglandins, each having its own effect. Analgesics like aspirin, ibuprofen, and acetaminophen inhibit the synthesis of prostaglandins by binding to the arachidonic acid receptor site. With no prostaglandins, no pain signals are generated.

Alia

That's a very astute observation! In addition to reducing pain and fever, aspirin and ibuprofen (which is the active ingredient in Advil) act as anti-inflammatory agents. This is because they block the formation of a certain type of prostaglandin responsible for inflammation.

Acetaminophen only blocks the formation of the prostaglandins responsible for pain and fever, but not those that cause inflammation. That's why Advil helps with your swollen ankle, but Tylenol may not reduce the swelling as effectively.
