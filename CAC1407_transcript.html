One common type is arteriosclerosis, which is a buildup of plaque on the inside walls of arteries. Does that make sense as a starting point?

Good question. As we discussed in an earlier section, plaque deposits are primarily an accumulation of low-density lipoproteins, which are high in cholesterol and saturated fats. When plaque builds up, the arteries become less elastic and have a decreased volume. Both of these effects make pumping blood more difficult, causing the heart to become overworked and weakened over time.

Student

So the heart has to work harder because the pipes it's pushing blood through are narrower and less flexible?

That's an excellent way to think about it! Yes, exactly like trying to push the same amount of water through a narrower, more rigid pipe. This accumulated damage to heart muscle from arteriosclerosis or other stresses can result in abnormal heart rhythms, known as arrhythmias. People with heart disease often experience chest pains, called angina, which results from an insufficient oxygen supply to heart muscles. They typically have decreased stamina and frequently need to pause to catch their breath during or after exercise.

I've heard my grandfather talking about his angina. So that's from his heart not getting enough oxygen?

Yes, that's correct. Your grandfather's experience is quite common. Now, there's another danger with arteriosclerosis that we should discuss. Another risk is the potential for a blood clot to form around the site of plaque formation. Such a clot can break off and be carried through the bloodstream until it clogs a blood vessel, effectively cutting off the blood supply to tissue, which then begins to die.

Student

That sounds serious. Is that what happens in a heart attack?

Alia

Precisely. A heart attack occurs when the dying tissue is heart muscle. Some heart attacks progress slowly, allowing the victim time to seek medical assistance, which may involve the administration of a quick-acting clot-dissolving enzyme. Other heart attacks are more rapid, killing the victim within minutes. Surviving a heart attack means living with a heart weakened by dead tissue.

Student

That's scary. So what can people do to prevent heart disease in the first place?

Alia

The most effective way to treat heart disease is to do what can be done to prevent it from happening in the first place. This includes avoiding stress, exercising regularly, and eating a well-balanced, low-cholesterol diet.

Student

But I've heard that some people eat healthy and still have high cholesterol. Is that true?

Alia

That's an astute observation. For many people, a healthy lifestyle does little to lower their blood cholesterol levels because their livers naturally produce more cholesterol than they obtain from their diet. For such people, physicians can prescribe what are known as statins, which are drugs that inhibit the synthesis of cholesterol. Two popular statins are atorvastatin, sold as Lipitor, which is a synthetic drug, and lovastatin, sold as Mevacor, which is a natural product isolated from the fungus Aspergillus terreus.

Interesting! I didn't realize some medications come from fungi. Are there other types of heart medications besides statins?

Alia

Yes, there are several other classes of heart medications. Vasodilators are a class of drugs that increase the blood supply to the heart by expanding blood vessels. They are useful for treating angina. They also reduce the workload of the heart, because opening up the blood vessels makes pumping the blood easier.

Student

So if I understand correctly, vasodilators make the "pipes" wider so blood can flow more easily?

Alia

That's an excellent analogy! Traditional vasodilators include nitroglycerin and amyl nitrite. They can be administered by a number of routes: orally or sublingually (under the tongue) or as a transdermal (through the skin) patch. A benefit of the latter two approaches is that they allow the drug to enter the body slowly, in contrast to the effect of taking the drug orally or by injection. These organic nitrates are metabolized to nitric oxide, NO, which has been shown to relax muscles in blood vessels.

Student

Wait, nitroglycerin? Isn't that an explosive? How can that be medicine?

Alia

That's a common confusion! Yes, nitroglycerin is indeed used in explosives, but in medicine, it's used in very small, controlled doses. The chemical properties that make it reactive also make it effective at releasing nitric oxide in the body, which helps dilate blood vessels. It's a great example of how the same compound can have very different applications depending on concentration and context.

Student

That makes sense. Are there medications that work directly on the heart itself rather than on the blood vessels?

Alia

Absolutely. Drugs that relax the pumping action of the heart have also been developed. When bound to receptor sites called beta-adrenoceptors in heart muscle, the neurotransmitters norepinephrine and epinephrine stimulate the heart to beat faster. A series of drugs called beta blockers slow down and relax an overworked heart by blocking norepinephrine and epinephrine from binding to the beta-adrenoceptors.

Student

So these beta blockers basically prevent the "speed up" signal from reaching the heart?

Alia

That's exactly right! Propanolol, sold as Inderal, was the first beta blocker developed and is useful for treating angina, arrhythmias, and high blood pressure.

Student

This is making more sense than I expected. Are there other ways to slow down the heart?

Alia

Yes, there are. Another group of drugs that relax heart muscle are the calcium channel blockers. One example is nifedipine. Muscle contraction is initiated as a nerve impulse signals calcium ions to enter muscle cells. As their name implies, calcium channel blockers inhibit the flow of calcium ions into muscles, thereby inhibiting muscle contraction. This slows the heart rate, relaxing and dilating the muscles of blood vessels, which lowers blood pressure.

Student

So calcium is needed for muscles to contract, and these drugs block calcium from entering muscle cells? That's interesting! I didn't realize calcium did more than just strengthen bones.

Alia

Great connection! Yes, calcium plays many important roles in the body beyond bone health. Muscle contraction is one of its critical functions. You're making excellent progress in understanding these concepts.

That's a perceptive question. Yes, in fact, long-time alcoholics require greater doses of a beta blocker in order to relax cardiac muscle. With more of these receptor sites to block, alcoholics require a correspondingly greater dose of the beta blocker to achieve the desired degree of cardiac relaxation.
