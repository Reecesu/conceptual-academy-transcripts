Student

Sure, that sounds like a good place to start. I know people eat plants and animals, but I've never really thought deeply about it.

Alia

Great! Let's begin with a fundamental concept: humans eat at all trophic levels. The formation of food begins with photosynthesis, which is the biochemical process used by plants to create carbohydrates and oxygen from solar energy, water, and atmospheric carbon dioxide.

Student

Photosynthesis... that's the thing plants do with sunlight, right? Making their own food or something?

Alia

Exactly! Plants use sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into carbohydrates and oxygen. The equation looks like this:
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂


Interestingly, only about 1 percent of the solar energy reaching Earth's surface is used in photosynthesis each day. But even that small percentage produces around 170 billion tons of organic material per year. This organic matter contains the energy that fuels virtually all living organisms.

Student

Wow, that's a lot of organic material! So all the food energy in the world comes from that 1 percent of sunlight?

Alia

That's correct! And that energy moves through what we call a trophic structure, or food chain. These structures consist of several hierarchical levels. The first level is producers—mostly photosynthetic organisms that use light energy to synthesize organic compounds. On land, plants are the main producers. In water, the main producers are photosynthetic organisms called phytoplankton.

Student

Ok, so plants and these phyto-whatever are at the bottom of the food chain. Then what comes next? Animals that eat plants?

Alia

Yes! Producers support all other trophic levels, which we collectively call consumers. Organisms that consume producers are primary consumers. In terrestrial environments, these are herbivores—"grass-eaters"—such as grazing mammals, most insects, and most birds. In aquatic environments, the primary consumers are microscopic organisms collectively known as zooplankton.

Student

And I'm guessing there are levels above that too? Like lions eating zebras or something?

Alia

You've got it! Above the primary consumers, the trophic levels are made up of carnivores—"meat-eaters." Each level eats consumers from lower levels. Secondary consumers eat primary consumers, tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers, and quaternary consumers eat tertiary consumers.

Student

What happens to animals that die naturally? Like if they're not eaten by something else?

Alia

That's an excellent question. Any organism that dies before being eaten becomes subject to the action of decomposers—organisms that break down organic material into simpler substances that then act as soil nutrients. Common decomposers include earthworms, insects, fungi, and various microorganisms.

Student

Oh, I see. So nothing really goes to waste in nature. That's pretty cool.

Alia

Exactly! Nature is remarkably efficient. However, there's something important to understand about energy transfer between trophic levels. With each transfer from one level to the next, there's a significant loss of energy. Typically, not more than 10 percent of the energy contained in the organic material of one trophic level is incorporated into the next higher level.

Student

Only 10 percent? Where does the rest of the energy go?

Alia

The rest is used for the organism's life processes or lost as heat. This means the availability of food energy is greatest for organisms lowest on the food chain. A grasshopper, for example, will find many more blades of grass to feed on than a field mouse will find grasshoppers. And the field mouse will find more grasshoppers than a snake will find field mice.

Student

So that's why there are fewer predators than prey in nature?

Alia

Precisely! This dwindling supply of food resources quickly limits the number of trophic levels, which rarely exceeds the quaternary level. Accordingly, the higher the trophic level, the smaller the possible population of organisms.

Student

So where do humans fit in all this? Since we eat both plants and animals, what level are we?

Alia

We humans actually eat at all trophic levels. When we eat fruits, vegetables, or grains, we are primary consumers. When we eat beef or other meat from herbivores, we are secondary consumers. When we eat fish such as trout or salmon, which eat insects and other small animals, we are tertiary or quaternary consumers.


The important thing to note is that our great and growing numbers are possible only because of our ability to eat as primary consumers. Eating meat is actually a luxury in terms of energy efficiency.

Student

A luxury? I never thought of a hamburger as a luxury before.

Alia

It's a luxury from an energy perspective. For people who eat chickens, for instance, the amount of biochemical energy they obtain from eating the chickens is minuscule compared with the amount of biochemical energy used in raising the chickens. In the United States, more than 70 percent of grain production is fed to livestock.

Student

So it would be more efficient if we just ate the grain directly?

Alia

Exactly. Producing meat requires more land to be cultivated, more water for irrigation, and more fertilizers and pesticides applied to croplands. Here's a fascinating statistic: If people in the United States ate 10 percent less meat, the savings in resources could feed 100 million people.

Student

That's kind of mind-blowing. I never realized my diet choices had such a big impact. So as the human population grows, will meat become even more of a luxury?

Alia

That's a very insightful question. As the human population expands, meat consumption will likely become even more of a luxury than it is today, due to these resource constraints.

Student

This is making me think about my diet differently... But I have a question. If an orca (killer whale) eats both sharks and gray whales that feed on phytoplankton, in which case is the orca eating at a higher trophic level?

Alia

That's an excellent question that shows you're understanding these concepts! Let's think about it. Sharks feed on fish and marine mammals, which makes them secondary, tertiary, or quaternary consumers. Phytoplankton-feeding gray whales, however, are primary consumers.


So when an orca feeds on a shark, it is eating at a higher trophic level than when it feeds on a gray whale. In the first case, the orca might be a quaternary or even quintenary consumer, while in the second case, it's only a secondary consumer.

Student

That makes sense! I think I'm starting to understand this trophic levels concept better now. It's kind of interesting how energy flows through ecosystems.

Alia

You're absolutely right, and I'm glad you're finding it interesting! Understanding these energy relationships is fundamental to ecology and helps explain many patterns we see in nature. You're making great progress in grasping these concepts.

Thanks! This isn't as complicated as I thought it would be. What's next in understanding food production?

After all, whether we're eating plants directly or indirectly through animal products, plant growth is the foundation of our food supply. Would you like to learn about plant nutrients next?
