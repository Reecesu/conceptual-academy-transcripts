Plants require various elements to survive and grow properly. Plant material consists primarily of carbohydrates, which are made of carbon, oxygen, and hydrogen. Plants get these three elements from carbon dioxide and water. However, the soil in which plants live also provides many other elements vital to their survival and good health.

Alia

That's exactly right! These nutrients are classified into two categories: macronutrients, which are needed in large quantities, and micronutrients, which are needed only in trace quantities. Some micronutrients are needed in such small amounts that a plant's lifetime supply is provided by the seed from which the plant grew.

Student

That's interesting! Can you tell me more about which specific nutrients plants need?

Alia

Certainly! Let me share the essential elements for most plants.


Macronutrients include:
- Carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (from air and water)
- Nitrogen (absorbed as NH₄⁺ and NO₃⁻)
- Phosphorus (absorbed as phosphate)
- Potassium (absorbed as K⁺)
- Calcium (absorbed as Ca²⁺)
- Magnesium (absorbed as Mg²⁺)
- Sulfur (absorbed as SO₄²⁻)


Micronutrients include elements like iron, manganese, zinc, copper, boron, chlorine, and molybdenum.

Student

Wow, that's a lot of nutrients! It reminds me of vitamin bottles with all those elements listed. So do plants actually use these the same way we use vitamins?

Alia

That's a great connection! Similar to how we need vitamins for our bodily functions, plants need these nutrients for their metabolic processes. Let's focus on three key macronutrients first: nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. These three are especially important.


Plants need nitrogen to build proteins and a variety of other biomolecules, such as chlorophyll, the green pigment responsible for photosynthesis. Plants absorb nitrogen from the soil in the form of ammonium ions, NH₄⁺, and nitrate ions, NO₃⁻.

Student

Wait, I'm confused. If nitrogen is in the air we breathe, why can't plants just take it directly from the air?

Alia

Excellent question! Even though nitrogen makes up about 78% of our atmosphere, plants can't use nitrogen gas (N₂) directly. The triple bond between the two nitrogen atoms is extremely strong and stable. Plants need nitrogen to be "fixed" into forms they can use, like ammonium or nitrate ions.


This process is called nitrogen fixation, which is defined as any chemical reaction that converts atmospheric nitrogen to a form of nitrogen that plants can use.

Student

So how does nitrogen get "fixed" then?

Alia

There are two main natural pathways for nitrogen fixation. The first is through bacteria. Most of the ammonium ions in soil come from nitrogen fixation carried out either by bacteria living in the soil or by microorganisms living in root nodules of certain plants, especially those of the legume family, including clover, alfalfa, beans, and peas. These plants are generally called nitrogen fixers.


These microorganisms possess the enzyme nitrogenase, which catalyzes the formation of ammonium ions from atmospheric nitrogen and soil-bound hydrogen ions.

Student

Oh! That's why my mom always tells me that bean plants are good for the garden! Is that related?

Alia

Exactly! Your mom is practicing good science. Farmers and gardeners often plant legumes like beans or peas because they improve soil quality by adding nitrogen.


The second pathway for nitrogen fixation results from the action of lightning on atmospheric nitrogen. The high energy of lightning is sufficient to initiate the oxidization of atmospheric nitrogen to nitrate ions, which are then washed into the soil by rain.

Student

Lightning creates plant nutrients? That's cool! I never would have thought weather could help plants grow.

Alia

Nature has many fascinating interconnections! In a natural setting, nitrogen fixation is the original source of ammonium and nitrate ions in the soil. However, most of this fixed nitrogen is recycled from one organism to the next. After a plant dies, for example, bacterial decomposition of the plant releases ammonium and nitrate ions back into the soil, where these ions become available to plants that are still living.

Student

So let me see if I understand: plants need nitrogen to make proteins, and they get it from special bacteria, lightning, or decomposing plants?

That's a perfect summary! Let me ask you a concept check question: Why do you think the seeds of nitrogen-fixing plants such as soybeans and peanuts are unusually high in protein?

You're on the right track! Plants use nitrogen to build proteins. Nitrogen-fixing plants assimilate a lot of nitrogen through their relationship with nitrogen-fixing bacteria, so they produce proteins in large quantities. That's why legumes like soybeans and peanuts are such good protein sources in our diet.

Student

That makes sense! So what happens if plants don't get enough nitrogen?

Alia

Plants deficient in nitrogen have stunted growth. Because nitrogen is needed for making chlorophyll, another symptom of nitrogen deficiency in plants is yellow leaves. The yellowing is most pronounced in older leaves—younger ones remain green longer because soluble forms of nitrogen are transported to them from older dying leaves.

Student

I think I've seen yellow leaves on some plants. Does that always mean they need nitrogen?

Alia

Not always, but it's a common cause. Now, let's discuss phosphorus, another critical nutrient.


Plants need phosphorus to build nucleic acids, phospholipids, and a variety of energy-carrying biomolecules such as ATP. All phosphorus comes to plants in the form of phosphate ions. The major natural source of these ions is eroded phosphate-containing rock. Significant amounts of phosphates are also recycled as organisms die and become incorporated into the soil.


After nitrogen, phosphorus is most often the limiting element in soil. Phosphorus-deficient plants are stunted in their growth.

Student

This is starting to make more sense. What about potassium? I've seen that on fertilizer bags at the garden store.

Alia

Yes, potassium is the third major nutrient! Potassium ions activate many of the enzymes essential to photosynthesis and respiration. As with phosphates, the major natural sources of potassium ions are eroded rock and the recycling of potassium ions from decomposing plant material.


After nitrogen and phosphorus, soils are usually most deficient in potassium. Symptoms of potassium deficiency include small areas of dead tissue, usually along leaf tips or edges. As with nitrogen and phosphorus, potassium ions are easily redistributed from mature to younger parts of the plant, so deficiency symptoms first appear on older leaves.

Student

Are there any other effects of potassium deficiency?

Alia

Yes, there's an interesting effect in cereal grains. When cereal grains, such as wheat or corn, are potassium-deficient, they develop weak stalks and their roots become more easily infected with root-rotting organisms. These two factors cause potassium-deficient plants to be easily bent to the ground by wind, rain, or snow.

Student

That seems like it could be a big problem for farmers! So these three nutrients—nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium—are the most important?

Alia

They're the nutrients most commonly deficient in soil, which is why commercial fertilizers typically highlight these three. They're often referred to as "NPK" on fertilizer packages.


An interesting fact about plants is that they readily absorb potassium ions, but they have a low tolerance for sodium ions, which they actively pump out of their systems. Interestingly, all living cells, not just plant cells, have a low tolerance for sodium ions and therefore have evolved mechanisms for pumping them out. As shown in Figure 14.20 in your textbook, a neuron pumping out sodium ions sets the stage for a nerve impulse. Multiple nerve impulses ultimately lead to conscious thought.


From a macroscopic perspective, plants and animals are significantly different. On a deeper submicroscopic level, however, their chemistries are much the same. In other words, plants and animals are more closely related than one might initially think.

Student

That's fascinating! I never thought plants and animals would share that kind of cellular function. Are there other important nutrients plants need?

Alia

Yes, let's talk about three more macronutrients: calcium, magnesium, and sulfur.


Both calcium and magnesium are absorbed by plants as the positively charged calcium and magnesium ions, Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺, respectively, and sulfur is absorbed as the negatively charged sulfate ion, SO₄²⁻. Most topsoil contains enough of these ions for adequate plant growth.

Student

If there's usually enough in the soil, why do we need to know about them?

Alia

That's a thoughtful question! Even though deficiencies are less common, understanding the role of these nutrients helps us recognize problems when they do occur.


For example, calcium ions are essential for building cell walls. Once absorbed by the plant, calcium ions are relatively immobile; that is, they do not travel well from one part of the plant to another. The plant therefore is not very capable of reallocating calcium supplies in times of need. This is why new-growth zones, such as the tips of roots and stems, are most susceptible to calcium deficiencies. The results are twisted and deformed growth patterns.

Student

So unlike nitrogen that can move around in the plant, calcium stays where it is?

Alia

Exactly! You're picking up on the important concept of nutrient mobility within plants.


Magnesium ions are essential for the formation of chlorophyll, which is the green-pigmented molecule essential for photosynthesis. Chlorophyll houses a magnesium ion at the center of a structure called a porphyrin ring. Besides its presence in chlorophyll, magnesium is essential because it activates many metabolic enzymes. Deficiencies in magnesium, which are rare, result in yellow leaves, because the plant is unable to generate chlorophyll.

Student

Alia

Another insightful question! With nitrogen deficiency, yellowing typically starts with older leaves first. With magnesium deficiency, yellowing often shows a different pattern, with green veins remaining while the tissue between veins turns yellow. The specific pattern can help diagnose which nutrient is lacking.


Most of the sulfur in plants occurs in proteins, especially in the amino acids cysteine and methionine. Another essential compound that contains sulfur is coenzyme A, a compound essential for respiration, for the synthesis and breakdown of fatty acids, and for the vitamins thiamine and biotin. Sulfur can be absorbed by leaves as gaseous sulfur dioxide, SO₂, an environmental pollutant released from active volcanoes and from the burning of wood or fossil fuels.
