Your mom's compost bin is a perfect example! For mineral fertilizers, these are literally mined from the earth. For example, saltpeter, which is NaNO3, was once used extensively as a source of nitrogen for fertilizers. But by the late 1800s, the supply of this nitrogen-containing mineral was almost exhausted.

Student

Oh no! So what did they do when they ran out?

Alia

That's where an important scientific breakthrough came in. In 1913, Fritz Haber, a German chemist, developed a process for producing ammonia from hydrogen and atmospheric nitrogen. This reaction is:


N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃


This technique is now the primary means of producing ammonia, which can be stored in high-pressure tanks as a liquid and injected into the soil.

Student

Wait, so we can just make ammonia from the air? That sounds too good to be true.

Alia

It does sound remarkable, but it's true! The air around us is about 78% nitrogen gas (N₂). The Haber process allows us to capture that atmospheric nitrogen and convert it into a form plants can use. Alternatively, the ammonia can be converted to a water-soluble salt, such as ammonium nitrate, NH₄NO₃, that is then applied to the soil either as a solid or in solution.

Student

Good question! For other essential nutrients, such as phosphorus and potassium, mining still remains an important endeavor. These elements can't be captured from the air like nitrogen can.

A fertilizer that contains only one nutrient is called a straight fertilizer. Ammonium nitrate, NH₄NO₃, is an example of a straight fertilizer, yielding only nitrogen.

Student

So it's "straight" because it's just one nutrient, not a mix?

Precisely! Any fertilizer containing a mixture of the three most essential nutrients - nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium - is called either a complete fertilizer or a mixed fertilizer. All mixed fertilizers are graded by what we call the N-P-K system.

Student

Yes, those numbers you've seen represent the N-P-K system, which lists the percent of nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) they contain. A typical mixed fertilizer might be graded 6-12-12, meaning it contains 6% nitrogen, 12% phosphorus, and 12% potassium.

Good question! A typical compost might be rated anywhere from 0.5-0.5-0.5 to 4-4-4. Compost N-P-K ratings are much lower because of their high percentage of organic bulk.

It might seem that way at first, but that organic bulk serves important purposes! It helps to keep the soil loose for aeration and serves as food for beneficial organisms that live in the soil.
