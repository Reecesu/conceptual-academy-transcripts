Excellent question. Fertilizer runoff from fields can contaminate drinking water supplies and affect human health. For instance, an ailment known as blue-baby syndrome results from drinking water containing high concentrations of nitrate ions, a main ingredient of most fertilizers.

Blue-baby syndrome? That sounds scary. What exactly happens?

Nitrate ions in the bloodstream compete with oxygen for the positively charged iron ions of hemoglobin molecules. This leads to a form of anemia known as methemoglobinemia, to which babies are particularly sensitive. Aside from shortness of breath, one of the major symptoms is a bluish color of the skin.

Topsoil? I guess that's the dirt on top, but why is it so important?

Topsoil is much more than just "dirt" - it's a complex living system. Synthetic fertilizers have no organic bulk and do not provide a food source for soil microorganisms and earthworms. Over time, a soil treated with only these fertilizers loses biological activity, which diminishes the soil's fertility.

So you're saying the soil is actually alive in some way? I never thought about it like that before.

That's right. Healthy soil is teeming with life. When soils become void of organic bulk, they become chalky and susceptible to wind erosion. It certainly has. Over the past 100 years, damaging farming practices have decreased the amount of topsoil in parts of the United States by as much as 50 percent. Oh! I think I've heard about this - the Dust Bowl, right? We talked about it in history class, but I didn't make the connection to farming practices.

Exactly. In one particularly severe storm, large dust clouds were carried all the way from the Midwest to Washington, D.C., and then into the Atlantic Ocean. It continues to this day in its efforts to help protect the nation's topsoil for future generations.

Any source of water other than rainwater requires an irrigation method to deliver water to the fields. Flooding is a common method, but it is not efficient because most of the water is lost in runoff and evaporation.

Sprinkler systems are indeed an improvement over flooding because they do not cause soil erosion. However, such systems also lose large amounts of water because a significant portion of the airborne water evaporates before reaching the ground.

This is all starting to sound really inefficient and wasteful. Are there other problems with irrigation?

Yes, there's another important issue with irrigation: salinization. All liquid water on the Earth's surface, no matter how fresh, contains some salts. After irrigation water evaporates from farmland, these salts are left behind, and over time, repeated irrigation causes the salinity of the soil to increase.

That's an astute observation. Thus, a river passing through farmlands gets saltier and saltier as it runs to the sea. By the time the Colorado River reaches the Gulf of California, for example, it is too salty for productive farming.
