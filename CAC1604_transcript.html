I'm glad you're exploring this topic! Wastewater treatment is actually a fascinating and important part of our infrastructure. Let's talk about what happens to the water that goes down our drains and toilets.


When wastewater enters municipal sewer systems, it needs to be treated before being released into bodies of water like rivers or oceans. The level of treatment depends largely on where the treated water will go. Water that will be released into rivers requires the highest level of treatment to protect communities downstream.

By the time human waste reaches the wastewater facility, it has lost its original form and appears as a murky stream. However, this stream contains many insoluble products including small plastic items and gritty materials like coffee grounds. You'd also find hardened balls of grease from cooking fats that people have poured down their drains.

Student

Wait, so people shouldn't pour cooking oil down the drain? I've definitely done that before.

Alia

That's a common misconception! Wastewater treatment experts emphasize that these insolubles—especially cooking grease—should be disposed of as solid waste rather than washed down drains or flushed down toilets. When you pour cooking oil down the drain, it can solidify and create problems in the treatment system.

Student

I had no idea! I'll definitely stop doing that. So what's the first step in treating all this wastewater?

Alia

The initial step in all wastewater treatments involves screening out these insolubles I mentioned. This screening process may include the use of a grit chamber to catch items that shouldn't be in the water system.


After screening comes what we call primary treatment. During primary treatment, the screened wastewater enters a large settling basin, where suspended solids settle out as sludge.

Student

So the sludge just... sits there? What happens to it eventually?

Alia

Good question! After a period of time, the sludge is removed from the bottom of the settling basin. In many cases, it's sent directly to a landfill as solid waste. However, some facilities are equipped with large furnaces where dried sludge is burned, sometimes along with other municipal wastes such as paper products. The resulting ash is more compact and takes up less space in a landfill.

Student

That makes sense. So after the primary treatment gets rid of the solids, is the water clean enough to go back into the environment?

Alia

Not quite. The wastewater effluent from primary treatment, as well as higher levels of treatment, is commonly disinfected before release. This is typically done with either chlorine gas or ozone.

Student

I know chlorine is used in swimming pools to kill bacteria. Is it the same idea here?

Alia

Exactly! Chlorine gas is commonly used because it remains in the water for an extended time after leaving the facility, providing residual protection against diseases. However, there are drawbacks. The chlorine reacts with organic compounds within the effluent to form chlorinated hydrocarbons, many of which are known carcinogens or cancer-causing agents. Also, chlorine kills only bacteria, leaving viruses unharmed.

Student

That doesn't sound great. Are there better alternatives?

Alia

Ozone is an alternative that has several advantages. It kills both bacteria and viruses, and there are no carcinogenic by-products that result from treating wastewater effluent with ozone. A disadvantage of ozone, however, is that it provides no residual protection for the effluent once it is released.


It's interesting to note that most facilities in the United States use chlorine for disinfecting, whereas European facilities tend to favor ozone. In some locations, both chlorine and ozone gases have been replaced by strong ultraviolet lamps, which, like ozone, kill both bacteria and viruses but provide no long-term residual protection.

Student

So if the water only goes through primary treatment, is it safe enough to release into rivers and oceans?

Alia

Actually, no. The potential for pathogens to grow in primary effluent is extremely high, and by virtue of the Clean Water Act of 1972, the release of primary effluent is not permitted in most places. This is why we have secondary treatment.

Student

What happens in secondary treatment?

Alia

Secondary treatment involves first passing the primary effluent through an aeration tank. This supplies the oxygen necessary for continued decomposition of organic matter by oxygen-dependent aerobic bacteria.


After aeration, the effluent is sent into a tank where any fine particles not removed in primary treatment can settle. Because sludge from this settling step is high in aerobic bacteria, some of it is recycled back to the aeration tank to increase efficiency. The remainder of the sludge is hauled off to a landfill or an incinerator.

Student

That's interesting. So the bacteria are actually helping clean the water? I always thought of bacteria as bad things.

Alia

That's a common misconception! While some bacteria cause disease, many others are incredibly beneficial. In this case, the aerobic bacteria (which need oxygen to survive) break down organic matter in the wastewater. It's a perfect example of how we use natural processes in our engineered systems.

Student

That's pretty cool. Is secondary treatment the final step, or is there more?

Alia

Many municipalities also require a third level, a tertiary level, of wastewater treatment. There are a number of tertiary processes, and most involve filtration of some sort.


A common method is to pass secondary-level effluent through a bed of finely powdered carbon, which captures most of the particulate matter and many of the organic molecules not removed in earlier stages.

Student

That sounds expensive. Why go through all that trouble?

Alia

The advantage of tertiary-level treatment is greater protection of our water resources. Unfortunately, tertiary treatment is costly and is normally used only in situations in which the need is deemed vital. Primary and secondary levels of treatment are also not without great cost.

Student

So to make sure I understand - primary treatment removes solid waste using screens and settling basins, secondary treatment uses bacteria to break down organic matter, and tertiary treatment filters out remaining particles and pathogens. Is that right?

Alia

That's an excellent summary! Primary wastewater treatment removes the bulk of solid waste and sludge from the sewage effluent using screening devices and large settling basins. Secondary treatment provides oxygen to oxygen-dependent bacteria that serve to decompose organic matter. Tertiary treatment removes pathogens and wastes not removed by earlier treatments by filtering the effluent through beds of powdered carbon or other fine particles.

Student

This is actually all making sense now. I'm wondering though - do all places use these massive treatment facilities? What about cabins in remote areas?

That's a thoughtful question! For dwellings in remote locations such as summer cabins, many people are opting for composting toilets, which use no water. Rather, they allow human waste to decompose aerobically (with oxygen) as air is vented over the waste, which is buried in peat moss. Dried, odor-free compost, which is removed every few months, is useful as a garden fertilizer.

That's pretty clever! I had no idea there were waterless toilets. Are there other innovations like that happening around the world?

Alia

Yes! For example, in Hong Kong, about 80 percent of all toilets flush using seawater. Developed since the 1960s, this system now saves the equivalent of about 25 percent of fresh water consumption. Also, effluent from fresh water activities, such as personal hygiene and dishwashing, is treated and reused for watering city trees.
