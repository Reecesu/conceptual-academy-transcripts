Student

Hi Alia. I need to learn about renewable energy sources for my environmental science class. I've never been good at science, so I'm a bit nervous about this unit.

Alia

That's completely understandable! Many students feel that way at first. We can take it step by step. Today, we could explore biomass energy, which is one of the renewable energy sources. Would that be a good starting point?

Student

Sure, that sounds good. Is biomass like solar panels or something?

Alia

Actually, biomass is different from solar panels. Biomass is a form of chemical energy. Plants use photosynthesis to convert radiant solar energy to chemical energy. This chemical energy comes in the form of the plant material itself—that's what we call biomass.

Student

Oh, so biomass is just plants? That seems too simple to be a major energy source.

Alia

It does seem simple, but it's quite powerful! We can use the energy of biomass in two main ways: we can process it to produce transportable fuels, or we can burn it at properly equipped power plants to produce electricity.


One advantage of biomass is that it can be grown on demand in regions where water supplies are abundant. And here's an important environmental benefit: if biomass is produced at a sustainable rate, the carbon dioxide released when it is burned balances the carbon dioxide consumed during photosynthesis.

Student

Wait, so burning biomass doesn't contribute to climate change? That doesn't make sense. Doesn't burning anything release carbon dioxide?

Alia

That's a great question that gets at an important misconception. Yes, burning biomass does release carbon dioxide. However, when biomass is grown sustainably, it creates a closed carbon cycle. The plants absorb carbon dioxide as they grow, and then release it when burned. So there's no net increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide.


This is different from fossil fuels, where we're releasing carbon that was stored millions of years ago. Additionally, biomass combustion products generally contain about one-third the ash produced by coal and about one-thirtieth the sulfur, making it cleaner in several ways.

Student

That makes more sense. So if I understand correctly, biomass doesn't add net carbon dioxide to the atmosphere when the growth rate equals the burning rate?

Alia

Exactly! You've got it. When the rate of biomass production equals the rate of consumption, there's no net production of carbon dioxide. The carbon cycle remains balanced.

Student

That's cool. So what kinds of fuels can we make from biomass?

Alia

One of the most common fuels obtained from biomass is ethanol. This is particularly relevant for transportation. Did you know that the U.S. transportation industry is 97 percent dependent on petroleum and consumes 63 percent of all petroleum stockpiles?


Fuels from biomass, notably ethanol, are natural alternatives to petroleum-based fuels. In fact, ethanol has a higher octane rating than gasoline, which is why it is a preferred fuel for race-car drivers.

Student

I didn't know race cars used ethanol! I thought they just used some super-premium gasoline.

Alia

Many people don't realize that! Here's another interesting historical fact: ethanol was also the preferred fuel of automotive pioneers Henry Ford and Joseph Diesel, who originally intended their automobiles to run on biofuels. By a curious coincidence, ethanol from fermented grains was about to be used by the automobile industry just prior to the passage of the 18th amendment to the U.S. Constitution prohibiting the consumption of ethanol. Prohibition allowed the petroleum industry to take over.

Student

That's wild! So Prohibition affected more than just drinking? It changed our entire energy system?

Alia

Yes, it had far-reaching consequences beyond what most people realize. Now, here's something to consider: Does biomass sound reminiscent of fossil fuels? It should, because biomass is simply a fossil fuel without the fossil—dead plant material that has yet to turn into coal, petroleum, or natural gas. Just about anything you can do with fossil fuels, you can do with biomass, except, of course, deplete it and create as much pollution.

Student

So biomass is like... baby fossil fuels? Before they've had time to fossilize?

Alia

That's a creative way to think about it! Yes, in essence, biomass is organic material that hasn't been subjected to the millions of years of heat and pressure that transform it into fossil fuels.


Today in the United States, government programs requiring the addition of 10 percent ethanol to gasoline are becoming widespread. Ethanol, also known as grain alcohol, can be prepared from the fermentation of food biomass—any grain will do, but those rich in simple carbohydrates such as sugars work best.

Student

Wait, is that the same ethanol that's in alcoholic drinks? So my car is basically drinking alcohol?

Alia

Yes, it's the same chemical compound! Ethanol is indeed the same alcohol found in alcoholic beverages. The difference is in how it's processed and the additives included for fuel purposes. When added to gasoline, it creates what's often called "gasohol" - gasoline containing an alcohol additive. The alcohol provides an octane boost, allowing an engine to run more efficiently with less pollution. If the alcohol is produced from biomass grown within a nation, there is the added benefit of a reduced dependence on foreign oil.

Student

That makes sense. Are there countries that use a lot of ethanol for fuel?

Alia

Absolutely! In 2020, worldwide production of ethanol for fuel was over 95 billion liters. Of that, the United States was the leading producer (53 billion L) followed by Brazil (30 billion L). While ethanol is produced in the United States primarily from the fermentation of corn, in Brazil, it is produced primarily from the fermentation of sugar cane.


Brazil started its ethanol for fuel program decades ago in response to the oil crisis of the 1970s. Up until 2005, it was the world's leading producer. Notably, most vehicles in Brazil are equipped to run using E100, which is all ethanol containing no fossil fuels. Thus, the carbon dioxide produced from these vehicles is offset by the carbon dioxide absorbed by the growing sugar cane.

Student

But doesn't using corn or sugar cane for fuel mean less food for people? That doesn't seem right when there's hunger in the world.

Alia

That's a very thoughtful concern and a common critique of some biofuel programs. Ethanol from fermentation is relatively expensive because of the great financial and environmental costs of growing food biomass, a process that requires vast amounts of water and fertilizer.


You're right that using food crops for fuel can create competition with food supplies. This is called the "food versus fuel" debate. Ethanol derived from petroleum is actually less expensive, but only because crude oil prices are kept artificially low. If taxpayer subsidies, exemptions from paying for the environmental damage from mining and drilling, and the cost of military protection are factored in, the price of crude oil quadruples.

Student

So what's the solution? Can we make ethanol from non-food plants?

Alia

Yes, that's exactly the direction research is heading! The ultimate starting material for the creation of ethanol is cellulose, which is the world's most abundant organic chemical, found in all plants. The idea is to break cellulose down to its glucose monomers, which can then be fermented to form ethanol.


As you may have learned in biology, cellulose found in plants is tightly locked. This is important for a plant's structural rigidity, but it makes it very difficult to break down the cellulose into glucose. Nonetheless, much research is being conducted to discover ways in which this can be done in a cost-effective and environmentally friendly manner.

Student

I vaguely remember learning about cellulose in biology. It's in plant cell walls, right?

Alia

That's correct! One promising approach to breaking it down is to cook the cellulose in liquid ammonia, NH3. Other approaches involve mimicking the cellulose-digesting action of the microbes that perform this feat within the stomachs of termites and ruminants, such as cows and goats.


Ethanol from cellulose is sometimes called cellulosic ethanol or grassohol. An advantage of cellulosic ethanol is that it does not use up food-stocks such as corn and sugar that could be used instead to help alleviate world hunger, which addresses the concern you raised earlier.

Student

Oh, "grassohol"—that's a clever name! So we could potentially make fuel from grass clippings and stuff like that?

Alia

Exactly! Lawn clippings, agricultural waste like corn stalks, wood chips, and even certain fast-growing grasses could all be potential sources for cellulosic ethanol. It's a much more sustainable approach that doesn't compete with food production.


Now, besides converting biomass into transportable fuels like ethanol, there's another way to use biomass energy. Would you like to learn about how biomass can be burned directly to generate electricity?

Student

Sure, though burning anything for electricity sounds kind of old-fashioned. Isn't that what we're trying to move away from?

Alia

That's a common perception, but there are important differences when it comes to biomass. Converting biomass to a transportable fuel is an extra step that decreases energy efficiency. Higher efficiencies are obtained by burning the biomass directly to produce electricity, also known as biopower.


Most biopower is generated by paper companies and forest products companies using wood and wood wastes as fuel. Some municipalities use solid-waste incinerators that provide electricity and waste disposal simultaneously. On average, about 80 percent of the dry weight of municipal solid waste is combustible organic material.

Student

So we can generate power from garbage? That's killing two birds with one stone!

Alia

Yes, it can address both waste management and energy production simultaneously. The traditional approach to generating electricity from biomass is to burn the biomass in a boiler, where water is converted to steam used to drive a steam turbine.


But efficiency can be more than doubled by first converting the biomass to a gaseous fuel, which may be done by applying air and steam at high pressure. Alternatively, gaseous fuels are also produced by mixing the biomass with very hot sand, as is done at a facility in Vermont. The gaseous fuel is burned, and the hot combustion products are directed to a gas turbine that generates electricity. In addition, the exhaust gases from the turbine can be used to produce steam for industrial applications or for additional power generation.

Student

That sounds pretty advanced for just burning plants. How much electricity do we actually get from biomass in the US?

Alia

In the United States, electricity produced from biomass grew from 0.20 GW in the early 1980s to 8 GW in 2000, to about 24 GW in 2014. The U.S. biopower generating capacity, however, has since remained fairly constant.


A reason for this is because, as a renewable energy, biopower faces stiff competition from photovoltaics and wind. Consider that about half of all biopower within the United States is generated within California. The production of biopower in California has remained fairly steady, while electric power from photovoltaics and wind have grown remarkably.

Student

So solar and wind are outpacing biomass now? I guess that makes sense - you don't have to grow and process anything, just set up the equipment.

Alia

That's a good observation. Each renewable energy source has its own advantages and limitations. As we'll explore later, solar and wind have seen dramatic cost reductions and efficiency improvements in recent years, making them increasingly competitive.


Before we move on, let me check your understanding: What do biomass and fossil fuels have in common?

Student

Well, based on what we've discussed, I think they both ultimately come from solar energy, right? Plants use sunlight to grow (biomass), and fossil fuels are just ancient plants that used sunlight millions of years ago.

Alia

Excellent! That's exactly right. They both originate from solar energy. The key difference is the timeframe - biomass represents a current or recent capture of solar energy through photosynthesis, while fossil fuels represent solar energy captured millions of years ago and stored underground. That's why biomass can be renewable when managed properly, while fossil fuels are not.
