By weight, hydrogen packs more energy than any other fuel, which is why it's used to launch spacecraft. It can be readily produced from the electrolysis of water, and the electrolysis equipment can be powered by solar-derived electricity. This way, hydrogen provides a convenient means of storing solar energy, which classifies it as a solar fuel.

That's a common misconception. Solar panels (or photovoltaics) generate electricity directly, but that electricity needs to be used immediately or stored somehow. Hydrogen offers us a way to store that energy in chemical form.


When hydrogen burns, it produces almost nothing but water vapor. This energetic vapor can drive gas turbines to produce electricity. Any waste heat can be used for industrial purposes or to warm buildings. The water that condenses from the turbine can be used for agriculture, human consumption, or recycled to produce more hydrogen.

Student

That sounds almost too good to be true. If it's so clean and efficient, why aren't we using it everywhere already?

Alia

That's a great question! While hydrogen has many advantages, there are still technical and economic hurdles to overcome. But let's continue exploring its benefits first.


Because hydrogen is a gas, it's easily transported through pipelines. In fact, pumping hydrogen through pipelines is more energy efficient than transmitting electricity through power lines. This means hydrogen facilities could be located in regions where production is cheapest—desert regions for solar thermal and photovoltaics, windy regions for wind turbines, and moist regions for biomass—and then transported to meet energy needs of distant communities.

Exactly! That's one of the advantages of a flexible system. Hydrogen can even be used as a fuel for automobiles.

It's quite innovative actually. Porous metal alloys have been developed that absorb large quantities of hydrogen gas. When you press the gas pedal of a hydrogen-powered car, it sends a warming electric current to this alloy, which is housed in the gas tank. As the alloy heats up, it releases hydrogen, which powers either an internal-combustion engine or an electricity-producing fuel cell. Gas stations of the future may truly be "gas" stations!

That's a perfect question. Why do you think the combustion of hydrogen produces no carbon dioxide?

That's absolutely correct! There is no carbon with which to form carbon dioxide. Hydrogen is an ideal fuel that, when burned, produces almost nothing but water vapor—no carbon dioxide, no carbon monoxide, and no particulate matter. That's why it's considered so clean.

Alia

As we discussed in a previous section (Section 11.4), an efficient way to produce electricity from hydrogen or any other fuel is with a fuel cell. Utilities could produce megawatts of electricity by stacking fuel cells. Present-day fuel cells have an efficiency of about 60 percent, which is well above the 34 percent efficiency of a typical coal-fired power plant.


Interestingly, one potential source of hydrogen is coal, which when treated with high-pressure steam and oxygen produces hydrogen gas and methane gas. Burning these gases in a gas turbine results in a coal-to-electricity efficiency as high as 42 percent. If the gases are first run through electricity-producing fuel cells, the efficiency is even higher. Meanwhile, because the coal is not burned directly, fewer pollutants are released into the environment.

You're right to question that. Coal is indeed a major polluter when burned directly. However, when processed properly to extract hydrogen, it creates fewer emissions than traditional coal burning. That said, can you think of an even cleaner and more abundant source of hydrogen on our planet?

Excellent! The cleanest and most abundant source of hydrogen on our planet is indeed water. Creating hydrogen from water is an energy-intensive process, however, that is best done using electrolysis, a technique we discussed in Section 11.6.


Ideally, the electrical energy required to create hydrogen from water could be provided by a solar-driven photovoltaic cell. The electricity generated by the cell is then sent to a second cell, where water is electrolytically cleaved to produce hydrogen. Current "solar fuel" research seeks to develop photovoltaic surfaces that directly generate hydrogen from water, as shown in textbook Figure 17.41.

It's a valid concern. Over the past several years, there has been great progress toward commercially feasible solar-energy-to-hydrogen systems. For such systems, the efficiency at which solar energy is converted to hydrogen needs to be as high as possible.


Initial systems had efficiencies of no more than 6 percent. One of the main hurdles was developing a photovoltaic system optimized for 1.23 volts, which is the voltage needed for the hydrolysis of water. Such a system was developed by 1998 and provided an efficiency of 12.4 percent. With newer nanotechnologies now under development, researchers are anticipating efficiencies in excess of 60 percent.

Student

It certainly is! And if hydrogen can be efficiently produced using electricity from photovoltaics or wind turbines, then a number of interesting possibilities arise.


Photovoltaics and wind turbines are "distributed" sources of energy, as opposed to "centralized" sources. In other words, they can be placed in people's backyards or in neighborhood solar energy parks, in contrast to a centralized coal-fired power plant.

Student

Oh, I get it! So instead of one big power plant, we could have lots of smaller energy producers. Kind of like how people now have solar panels on their houses?

Alia

Precisely! The solar electricity and hydrogen can thus be produced at the point of use, thereby avoiding energy losses inherent in shipping energy over long distances. Such a system would not likely put the centralized utility out of business; instead, it would help the utility cope with ever-increasing energy demands.


Perhaps most importantly, the greatest energy growths for the next century will be occurring in developing nations where the infrastructure for centralized energy sources does not exist. Starting people off in these countries with noncentralized and nonpolluting sustainable energy sources would be a worthwhile investment.

Even greater opportunities exist in developing nations, where there are more than 2 billion people without electricity and many more to come with population growth. Conservation measures can be quite effective, as was demonstrated after the Arab oil embargo imposed by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries in 1973. From the start of this embargo until 1986, energy consumption in the United States remained constant as a result of increased efficiency while the economy grew by 30 percent.

Not at all. New technologies and materials suggest there is still much we can do. Super-insulated buildings, for example, now require only one-tenth as much heating in winter and cooling in summer. Hybrid combustion–electric vehicles can now drive up to 100 miles per gallon (mpg), which is a big step up from the average U.S. fuel efficiency of about 25 mpg.


If you were to replace a single 60-watt incandescent bulb with an equivalent 8-watt LED light, you would be saving the release of about 1800 kg of carbon dioxide each year, assuming a fossil fuel power plant. This LED light would also be about 90 percent cheaper to operate.

This is why for a democracy to work, it's important to nurture the attitude of each vote being exceedingly important. Conversely, the easiest way to defeat a democracy would be to nurture an attitude of voter apathy.
